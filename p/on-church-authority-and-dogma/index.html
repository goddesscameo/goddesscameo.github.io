On Church Authority and Dogma

'What is truth?', retorted Pilate.

11 minute read

“What is truth?”, retorted Pilate; words said under the auspice of man’s burgeoning selfhood. The threshold of independence problematized traditional modes of sourcing truth from without by introducing the priority of political statecraft, suppressing the Great Sanhedrin that wanted Jesus dead. Pair this with the lack of internal conviction in the minds of the people, and claims to the truth carried the risk of appearing as façade. By all means, the question “what is truth?” could not have been asked in previous eras, since in those times the truth was easily imposed onto the subject and common agreement amongst soon-to-be individuals found its place. In the not-so distant past, man did not see himself as separate from the world, let alone separate from others, and so to see a given object was to already see its significance in a way more intimately bound with the soul than it is in current times. Because of this, the fact of truth was a matter of perception. One only had to view a mountain to intuit a type of geomancy, to see that the spiritual reality behind the mountain would reveal itself in the very act of its appearance to the subject. It would be mistaken to view this as a superior stage of man since the possibility of this perception’s intimacy rests on the fact that man was much closer to an objectified character than he is today. He was much closer to the natural spirits which mired him in their contingency, placing himself alongside them, rather than recognizing his potential to rise above the natural world. This is the reason myths might tell of eras in which unity with nature, which is a stand in for the world, was prevalent, and at the same time followed by a transition into the new. The death of Baldur in Norse mythology, for instance, preserves for us the memories of the world-historical process in question. His death - that plunge into darkness foreshadowing the end of the old world and the coming of Ragnarök - crystallized the feeling of a gradual fading away of this kind of perception, representing the loss, or more accurately, the transformation, of an ancient human capacity. Man’s condition in those previous times presents for us a very glaring issue, in hindsight. If truth was simply given in perception, if one’s consciousness is where Gods speak and see on your behalf, then the individual remains a passive receptacle, prohibiting the development of an independent self. The capacity for freedom, for generating any sort of conviction from within, cannot develop. This is why the imagery of Baldur being cast down into darkness is a perfect one for this transition. To create the space for freedom, the Gods had to withdraw, and the old perceptual light of Baldur had to be extinguished, otherwise the Gods would continue seeing through you, arresting the development of you. For a time, humanity was plunged into a metaphorical darkness, into a state where the inner connection to the spiritual world was no longer immediately felt as a result. In some ways, today we are still living in this necessary trauma. Necessary, since the awakening of an individual consciousness mandates the sleeping of the old form of direct perception. With the withdrawal of divinity, for the first time, man could look at the world, at Jesus, and ask, even in derision, “what is truth?”. Pilate stands precisely at the threshold of this independence, articulating the question for the epoch. The more esoteric strands of Christianity may provide the answer to some, but for the masses, the void left by the retreat of the Gods remained and grew intolerable. People cannot live without some relation to truth, and so this void was filled at first by external authorities. Priests, traditions, secret and public, sacred texts and languages, and organized institutions offered to tell humanity what the truth is. The birth of dogma is here; this is the origin of truth received externally in an age where truth can be arrived at internally.

Ideally, dogma acts as a provisional holding, while the capacity for self-generated truth lays dormant. Since the demand for grounding and the need for moral guidance was abundant, the Church stepped in to respond to and supply man with the answers of what to believe in, and how to live. The early church councils used reason in an authoritative and collective form, not having fully shed the previous era’s externalized mode of conviction. A group of bishops gathered and debated, then declared that such and such is true, and to believe in the same. Surely, this arrangement is better than the chaos which would follow the collective dark night of the soul that the retreat of the Gods inaugurated, and so the Church preserved the seed of truth in Christianity while the deeper capacity for an individual spiritual activity laid dormant. Unfortunately and eventually, this “holding” became a proverbial holding cell when the authority of the Church, having claims to the truth, inevitably faced challenges. The Church, having claimed the authority to declare truth, could not easily later say “but really, now you should find this truth for yourself”, since this posture would be a monument of its own undoing. As a result, the provisional status of organized religion became absolute, and the dogma that was meant to preserve truth until humanity could attain it for ourselves became a barrier of entry that those who did not pass through, could not ever hope to achieve individual certainty. We may consider what happened when challenges arose throughout history. The Church never said “let us collectively examine this or that challenge together using our inner capacities”. Instead, it said “the dogma has already spoken, the challenge is false, and whatever partial truth one individually sees in a given position is heretical”. Such an attitude can only be silenced for so long. The Church’s refusal to engage honestly with viewpoints brought by modern man’s ego postponed the inevitable blowback. When the viewpoints returned via the Reformation, the scientific revolution, the Enlightenment, and so on, they returned with much greater force. So much so that the authority that had refused to examine itself was swept aside. What the Church failed to recognize is that the very partial truths seen by heretics was often the first stirrings of the individual ego awakening to its own capacity for inner life. Every suggestion and formulation of even Christianity itself was testament to the creative powers of mind the ego was responsible for. By suppressing these stirrings, the Church drove them underground, where they fermented and returned, no longer interested in dialogue with ecclesiastical authority. In fact, it rejected the external authority of the Church altogether, instead opting for the theory-internal verification that an individual’s inner life would fashion from the facts it created. Thus, the modern condition was born.

The modern condition is characterized by an insecurity of certainty, coming out of the previous age of dogma. The old structure promising truth collapsed under the weight of its own refusal to examine the ability to truly reproduce what it claimed, meaning does the Church or the Mosque or the Temple really reproduce the religion, or does it reproduce the structure of authority passing down dogmas previously established? We can do a simple test. Ask a believer today to justify the religion they hold to without appealing to a holy book. Then ask them to do so without appealing to the authority of history, whether it be tradition or consensus. Most will struggle, others will fall silent, some will retreat into a circular reasoning. Press further, and many will admit uncertainty or ignorance, and this uncertainty is precisely what becomes the gateway to agnosticism, atheism, and materialism. This is not an unreasonable position at first glance, after all, they are the honest conclusions of individuals who have been told that truth comes from outside. The atheist then says “I see no evidence”, and the materialist says “the only truth which I can validate that comes from the outside is the physical world”. What the atheist means by evidence is always evidence for someone, whether it is a percept or a measurement. We cannot fault the atheist too hard for not realizing they are in the context of a fallen state, separate from God and the spiritual world. But we are able to fault them for assuming that the evidence speaks for itself, that the facts are simply there, pressing their truthfulness upon any rational observer. However, the evidence does not interpret itself. The scientist interprets it, using a method learned from others, validated and sanctioned by a community, and grounded in certain assumptions about the uniformity of nature and the reliability of the senses, all of which are accepted on authority. The authority of the scientific consensus or methodological naturalism acts, in a way similar to the authority of the church body, as a way to offload the responsibility of individual certainty onto another entity. That is not to say that what is being posited by these authorities is wholly false, in the same way that church dogma may or may not be true. The materialist then goes further and declares that authority belongs to the physical world. Verily you may ask them: how do you know? They will point to physics, to chemistry, to geology, and to the success of other natural sciences, yet physics itself cannot observe the claim that “physics is only real”. That is a metaphysical commitment being adopted from a particular tradition, in the same way that the dogmatic Christian appeals to the Church body. How piercing of an observation that the catechism has been replaced by the peer-reviewed journal, all while leaving the state of the individual in an ignorant light. Such attitudes do not answer the real question whatsoever. They offer different whats to believe in, typically the laws of nature and the authority of scientific consensus, but they never address the how. How does one come to stand in a relationship with truth? How does one generate this conviction certainly rather than taking it on assumption? These questions are betrayed by an infidelity of the soul in the quest for truth. The very structure of external justification trains modern man like pavlovian dogs not to ask them and instead to receive the truth, just as he has always done.

Some, sensing the emptiness of this arrangement, turn backward and adopt the reactionary stance of blind traditionalism. Much can be said about the attempt to resurrect old dogmas, rituals, and authorities. It is a tempting regression since it offers the comfort of certainty without the responsibility of inner life. But the traditions of old died for a reason, and they will die again, delaying the inevitable collapse once more. Resurrecting the dead only concludes in the necrotic husk of spiritual development, parading itself around as alive and polluting the living world with its antiquated purpose.

A final attitude in the modern condition is found in the one who says “I am spiritual but not religious”. Such an individual has typically experienced a rupture of some kind. They may have been raised in a religious tradition and found its claims hollow and its authorities hypocritical. Perhaps some personal slight against them has been committed. Maybe they have grown up outside any tradition and absorbed the ambient secular culture, but have felt that something was missing which materialism could not provide. In any case, two important revelations are before us. The first is that they have discovered that external authority cannot be trusted. If something speaks from the outside and demands submission, the soul will interpret this as a foreign, tyrannical force upon it. Not because the individual is rebellious by natural disposition, but because the ego has awakened enough to sense that truth accepted purely on command is no truth at all. Secondly, they have discovered that direct certainty matters greatly. One wants to feel the sacred rather than assenting to propositions about it. So they conclude that religion is the problem, and a general “spirituality” is the solution, with one being external and dogmatic, with the other being internal, personal, and free. The conclusion presented here is half-right, and that aspect of truth in it is what makes it so seductive to the mind of the everyman. The stance presented recognizes the need for a new kind of inwardness and assumes that rejecting external authority is enough, that this feeling is the same as knowing. All three postures, in fact, remain determined by the structure they seek to escape. Still they ask for truth to be justified from the outside by evidence or feeling alone.

No external source, church, scripture, dogma, propaganda, or consensus will ever be able to furnish the soul with truth. The only valid ground is the individual’s own disciplined spiritual activity. All manner of offloading the responsibility can occur, but at the end of the day, the buck has to stop with you. That it is to say, it must end with individuality as an achievement. No one is born with inner authority, it must be cultivated through a methodical inner working of ethical life and meditation; the same state of constant prayer which theosis lauds as its goal. When one fully develops a capacity for inner knowing, the truth one perceives is not “their truth” as opposed to someone else’s. It is objective reality. No one else can generate it for you, and you cannot borrow the eyes of the Gods in hopes that it will affect your own development. Therefore, the only legitimate authority is ultimately self-authority, the authority of a fully developed and freely acting ego that has become one with the content of its own knowing.
