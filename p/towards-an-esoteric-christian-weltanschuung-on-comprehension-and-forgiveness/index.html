Towards an Esoteric Christian Weltanschuung: On Comprehension and Forgiveness

The possible worldviews and their configurations.

69 minute read

The Advent of Thought

The emergence of thought in man has brought with it a cornucopia of reverberations, the fruits of which are laid bare in the various works, paintings, books, sculptures, cultures, and beliefs that he himself holds. Thought did not always exist in man. However, now that it does, it is in the best interest of the thinking man to detail and reflect upon what exactly it gives him, the ground upon which it can develop in him, and, consequently, what it says about him such that he can recognize it as his own self-expression. As Laozi bids in the Dao De Ching,

“He who knows others is wise; he who knows himself is enlightened.”

To begin with, a definition is of service, that is, one that helps illuminate what the action most relevant to discussion is. The word which we will define presents itself to us as thought. Thought is the product of thinking. The word think emerged initially as a “a convergence of two Old English verbs from the same prehistoric source but with distinct forms and senses”. The first, þyncan, meant “to seem” or “to appear”, preserved in the archaic methinks. The second, þencan, meant “to conceive in the mind, meditate, or intend”, and is the verb that gives us our modern sense of cognitive reasoning. Grammatically, the latter was actually the causative for the former, implying an original sense of “to cause (something) to appear to oneself”. During the Middle English period, these two verbs merged in the form of “thinken”, finding the older sense of the word as “seem” largely absorbed and/or lost. The connection to the noun “thought” is fairly direct. Thought derives from the Old English þoht or geþoht, which was the past participle of þencan, thus, a “thought” is proven through etymological reconstruction, in addition to colloquial understanding, as the product of the act of thinking. Both “think” and “thought”, along with the related word “thank”, ultimately trace back to the same PIE (Proto-Indo-European) tong-, meaning “to think or feel”, uniting these concepts of mental activity, its results, and its remembrance into a single family.

Of importance here are two things: remembrance and feeling. Remembrance, or memory, is necessary in order for this act to retain structural continuity with itself. For an act of thinking to be more than a passing, fleeting impulse, it must reference and build upon previous mental content. Memory is precisely the faculty that allows a thought to cohere and develop from a semblance (þyncan), into a concept (þencan, “to cause to seem to oneself”). It allows perceptions and reflections on those percepts to combine into a continuous process. Without it, each cognitive act would be alienated from its origin, incapable of forming the sustained patterns we recognize as reasoning and contemplation. Feeling, likewise, can be considered a qualitative motive. Modern sensibilities find this a bit confused, holding “thought” and “feeling” as antinomies. The thinking person is considered to be one that produces from themselves the logical results of their thought, undiluted with illogical feeling. The emotional person is regarded, or rather disregarded, as ungrounded and swayed by the intense immediacies of emotion, to be corrected by a composed and reserved logic. This kind of parody of the relationship between thought and feeling misses the interplay of the two as they rightfully are. It is often people that are driven by intense emotion which employ logic and thought how they do, seeing them as reasonable centers by which emotions peripherally orbit and are attracted by. In “opposition”, one may feel very emotional towards that which they deem logical and true. Both perspectives are validated if we are to be called free beings, but what is meant to be emphasized is that the root word’s association of thought with feeling indicates to us that early conceptions of thinking were charged with valuation and personal resonance. To think about something was to have it matter to you on some level. To desire it (as in Old English’s þencan’s sense of “wish, desire”), and to appraise its appropriateness was to recognize the unity of thought with this thought, or my thought. Feeling is what transforms a neutral mental image into an object of our intention and concern. It becomes the bridge between the apparition of seeming, and the meaningful cognition found in thinking.

Having defined thinking as this internally generative act of causing something to appear to oneself, we can say that this product called “thought” is a thing which describes an active process that we wholly generate and inhabit. Defined as internal act, we may extrapolate this into the understanding that when we have a thought, we are within every fiber of this thought, a complete participator in its activity. Unlike observing a rose, where its hidden qualities may elude us, a thought contains nothing that you have not yourself thought into it. Thought is the one element of our consciousness of which we have the most immediately complete possession of. It is the most intimate of knowledge kinds, because we are the originators of this knowledge. In other words, the feeling of being fully active and present within thought is a proof of its internal reality and origin.

Every time we think, we cannot just be describing a thing we see (so for example, a single triangle, or a specific lion). We are internally re-constructing it in relation to its own ideal possibility- to its concept. To think of a “tree” is to hold in mind not just a pine or an oak, but the generative principle of “tree-ness” itself, including its form, growth, and life. What we gesture towards, in this internal act of participation (or self-particularizing), is that we recognize the instance by connecting it to the universal pattern it expresses in the form of its concept. The concept is what holds a given perception together as a unity, which retroactively makes percepts intelligible in the first place. Moreover, this internality exists everywhere. If the concept of a tree is the unitary pattern by which all trees exist in virtue of, then that given pattern is not a human invention. We discover it through thinking. The cosmos is structured by these living patterns, or concepts (the various laws of form, growth, etc.), and our thinking is the site where these concepts become conscious of themselves. We are the point where the universe turns back in on itself to understand itself. In this sense, the saying that “we are the ‘thoughts’ of God”, coheres with common understanding. It is in effect saying that we are, the self-directed and conscious expressions of that cosmic intelligence, and at the same time saying that we are downstream and conditioned by some absolute totality. Our very capacity to think conceptually is our share in the expressions of this world-shaping activity.

A distinction can then be made between the local act of thought and the global. Or, in more spiritual terms, “human” thought and “cosmic” thought. On the one hand, we have the global delineation of thought, this universal process of internal generation, present in all things, and on the other, the instantiation of this act in us as individual people. This raises a point for us. If thinking is our primary tool for engaging with reality, then the specific way we orient our thinking will determine the reality we perceive. The activity of human thought decides what counts as the world for us. Different starting points in thought concretize into attitudes. They develop logically from basic mental postures. Once thought awakens in man as his primary faculty, thought begins to explore its relationship to being. Each differentiated attitude becomes itself a rudimentary status of thought which positions itself as answering the question of what one recognizes as ultimately significant for the soul. These positions are hereby known as worldviews: conceptions of the “world”, or object, in the soul of man, as provided by thinking.

Zodiacal Associations

Spirit has been roughly defined before here, and so laboring to explain it in detail is re-treading ground, wasting time. However, what has not been defined before is the zodiac. Ordinary knowledge recognizes the zodiac as the belt of space in the sky, which is divided into 12 sections. The Sun then passes through each section. Astrology, as it goes with the materialistic sciences, has extracted all ideal significance from this “map”, reducing it into a way to tell the wobble of the Earth, or a way to tell time, resembling an archaic form of the independent variable found in the laboratory- time. The thinking man would best be fit for exploring all aspects of the concept of the zodiac. It is, in actuality, a topography of man’s nature and spiritual potential. The constellations correspond to the highest spiritual beings, such as the Seraphim and Cherubim, who sacrificed their substance to create our solar system. One can consider it a structural necessity that if there is life, there were higher beings which made it a condition that they influence and, in some cases, intervene on the development of this life. Likewise, any given “solar system” (which we may define as an appropriately partitioned section of the cosmos) hosting life is considered an organism, and will develop a zodiac. Surely, this stands in contrast to the tabloid conceptions of the zodiac as influencing personality traits. The signs actually act as cosmic formative forces in the development of man. For example, the emergence of the Heart in the form of man was founded under Leo (the Lion), speech under Taurus (the Bull), and symmetry under Gemini (the Twins). In the sense that higher beings act as influences on our development, the zodiac is both a map in the sky, and a map in ourselves. It is a picture of the spiritual archetype of man, projected into space, including man’s origin and future potential.

Building from the PIE root gwei-, meaning to live, the zodiac unveils itself as the animating intelligence of the cosmos. The Latin spiritus (breath, spirit) can help us in cognizing and recognizing the cosmos as a state of breath; the universe is always in a cycle of inspiring and expiring. The zodiac stands as a pattern of this breath. Each of the twelve signs represents a mode of this universal life-force in the form of a specific way in which the universe performs its living, or animating, or breathing. Aries (the Ram), for example, is the primal impulse to be, the fiery spark (the element of fire does not always refer to the state of matter ‘plasma’, but can also be considered the spiritual force of passion). Taurus is the power of persistence and embodiment. Gemini is the capacity to relate and communicate. These patterns continue throughout all twelve signs, describing a completed circle of animation.

Human thoughts are, accordingly, the localized incarnations of these animating forces. We, and hence our thoughts, are microcosms borne of this living cosmos, and our capacity to think shares in this principle. When we think, we are actually engaging in specific modes of cosmic living, transforming them into conscious activity. Thought dedicated to meticulous analysis and refinement is Virgo at work. The quality of thought which concedes ideality and spirituality yet prioritizes reconciliation and balance is the work of Libra. The drive to seek philosophical truth is Sagittarius animating our curiosity, while the need to build enduring conceptual structures is Capricorn’s formative will thinking through us, and so on and so forth. We will get to the zodiacal associations soon enough. Each of the twelve attitudes, or worldviews given to us by thought, can be understood as perspectives birthed when the mind operates predominantly under the influence of one, or more, of these twelve animating principles. By virtue of thought, our capacity to have this internal world, is itself animated by, and an expression of, a particular zodiacal quality of the cosmos’ living intelligence. The twelve worldviews soon to be discussed are thus twelve characteristic ways the one animated reality reflects upon and comprehends itself through the human soul. Our thinking stances chart a path already paved by the influences of what is possible through the zodiac, and, like the Sun, we all must eventually pass through each 12 of the influences.

The Truth of Each Worldview

No single worldview adequately possesses the truth in any exclusive sense. Each is a one-sided, but necessary, perspective that the human spirit adopts as it evolves in consciousness. The spiritual scientist should be able to freely inhabit any of the worldviews and their configurations. What is meant to be centrally conveyed is that the truth is the whole. Thought, in crafting worlds internal to the soul, crafts a diversity of worlds, each with their own life. From a spiritual standpoint, these worldviews are not ranked by any kind of superiority. For those familiar with Hegel’s Science of Logic, a comparison can be made where Essence is not “more true” than Being (although Being “finds its truth” in Essence, this is not a ranking of worth). It would make no sense to so say this, nor would it make sense to create a ranking of “souls” and soul-outlooks ranging in degree of value. They are different soul-configurations of modes of perception, and are all equally valid as expressions of the human spirit engaging with the cosmos through the activity of thought. God would value each as a unique individuality all the same. As such, the developmental sequence of each from another shows a progression in human self-awareness. We will revisit this nascent wisdom later on.

Opposing worldviews exist in a dialectical, but fruitful, tension. One cannot be “more true” than the other. Each contains the seed of its opposite, and finds its completion only when the soul inwardly experiences the other. We can take materialism for example. Materialism posits that only the physical, measurable world is real. Its truth is the stubborn reality of the external, material world. The one-sidedness present in it is one of a denial of interiority and spirit. Idealism, on the other hand, posits that ideas and spirit, or consciousness, are primary. Its truth is the reality of the internal. The one-sidedness present in it is its potential disregard for concrete being. Although these are not diametrically opposed claims, they still inhabit opposites “sides” insofar as their claims on reality are exclusive. The point is that one must fully inhabit the materialist perspective until one confronts its own limits, e.g., the mystery of consciousness, the origin of natural laws, etc. At this limit, the truth of an “Idealism” emerges immanently from Materialism’s own questioning. The limit represents the birthplace of the opposing view. The opposite journey is equally valid. By holding both, you no longer have a worldview; you behold the spiritual reality that gave rise to both perspectives. In dealing with these worldviews, to rectify a one-sided perspective is to draw out the other from the self’s inner core. In the case of the materialist, the remedy is in drawing Spirit out from materialist explanations.

The 12 Worldviews

To restate for clarity, a worldview is the content of the soul’s perspective on the world and the self. Each worldview describes an engagement with the world, as a possible object of thought. As an example, the materialist and spiritualist are arguing about the core of the object itself, presenting each as the crux of how one should view the world. Fortunately, worldview is the layer of philosophic doctrine that can be debated logically. This is because it is the most consciously articulated layer by way of thought. Below are the 12 worldviews, listed in association with their zodiacal signs.

Materialism and Spiritualism

The most concrete and abundant worldview, and the one we will begin with (for no other reason than this being the most easily accessible and most relevant for our current time), is materialism. Its main tenant announces that only “physical matter” is fundamentally real. Everything in existence, be it consciousness or thoughts, is ultimately an epiphenomenon of complex material interactions; everything “non-material”, which historically has been a definition disputed, is simply an emergent phenomenon from the “lower” world of matter. The worldview certainly has validity within the domain of sense-perceptible phenomena, acting as the chief of understanding the laws of physics and chemistry and the like. However, when elevated to a total worldview, a problem occurs. To demonstrate this problem, Rudolf Steiner presents us two scenarios involving shoemakers. Imagine if the historical mystic Jakob Böhme, who was actually a shoemaker in Görlitz, had used the concepts and techniques of his trade to explain the cosmos. He might say,

“There is a great last at the foundation of the world. Over this last the world-leather was stretched; then the world-nails were added… The difference between day and night comes from the world-boot being polished in the morning and getting dirty by evening."

Everyone would rightfully laugh at this absurdity because shoemaking concepts have no validity for explaining cosmic phenomena. The real Jakob Böhme, however, knew better. He used his skilled knowledge of leather and soles to make excellent boots for the townspeople. But when he sought to understand the spiritual foundation of the world, he resorted to something else entirely. He resorted to inner contemplation and spiritual insight- concepts appropriate to that domain. He did not commit a kind of category error. Likewise, the error which materialism makes is in failing to see the concepts of matter and chemistry as tools for understanding the physical aspect of the world, not the world in its totality. The strict materialist worldview renders the human “I”, freedom, consciousness, spirit, and all other higher ideals as illusions, which is both philosophically bankrupt, and spiritually deadening. Ultimately, the materialist worldview looks outward and downward, seeking to explain the “higher” by way of the “lower”. Its strength lies in its commitment to empirical observation and logical consistency within the physical realm, however, its weakness can only be solved with the inclusion of Spiritualism.

Spiritualism (called Spiritism in some works, not to be confused with the movement of Spiritism asserts that spirit is the foundational reality. The physical, or material world is either an emanation, manifestation, or illusion, depending on the soul-mood, created by spirit. Consciousness is primary; the divine mind takes precedent and matter is secondary and derivative. Spiritualism acts as a counter polarity to materialism. It correctly identifies that the essence of being, and the forces of life and thought, cannot be derived from dead matter. It is valid for the domain of inner experience, morality, and artistic creation. However, in its one-sided form, it risks becoming dismissive of the tangible reality of the physical world, viewing it as a great Maya (which, is a stance closer to Pneumatism, but is still delineated from it). The Spiritualist looks inward and upward, seeking to explain “lower” matter, by the “higher” spirit.

From materialism, one may begin with a naïve materialism. Let us take, for example, the committed materialist neuroscientist. He maps the brain’s wetware, convinced that consciousness is a chemical illusion, cast by the synapses. To explain mind, he must reduce it to diagrams of neutral pathways and neurotransmitters, yet, in his work, lies the seed of his doubt. His instruments measure the brain’s activity, but the observer of those measurements remains outside the diagram. The very act of doing science presupposes a unified site (the “I”) that matter-based models cannot locate, pushing them to consider that perhaps we only deal with metal representations, the phenomena of perception (phenomenalism). This world of phenomena, however, is quite stubborn and operates with a similar consistency. It demands an explanation beyond subjective appearance, leading them to affirm a structured and objective reality (realism). Investigating this reality, he finds that it is not a collection of fixed particles as initially thought, but a field of forces and processes which the observable world is only a result of (dynamism). These forces appear to organize themselves into discrete, self-organizing systems, each a kind of sovereign center, or Monad (monadism). Finally, they confront the core question: what is the nature of a unifying, self-aware center? Surely it cannot be a composite material thing. The logic of the materialist’s journey here forces a singular conclusion. The ultimate reality of these centers, must be non-material Spirit (spiritism). A more practical path can be found in the extreme modern materialism of today. The materialism of a 19th century physiologist, for example, can, through its own rigor, unintentionally and directly demonstrate the necessity of spirit! By describing the human being as a perfect machine, it raises the question: “What intelligence designed this machine?”. Thus, the dead end of one-sided materialism can become the start of a spiritual quest.

Conversely, the transition from spiritualism to materialism often begins with a spiritualism that cannot adequately account for the brute fact and suffering of the physical world. Seeking firmer ground, it may move through Idealism and Rationalism, eventually hardening into a Mathematism that sees only abstract structure, which can then be interpreted materially. The transition from one side of a worldview to another, does not necessarily have to follow a linear, zodiacal path, however, I will attempt to outline a logical path by which the spiritual scientist may guide another outside the dogmatism of a given worldview.

The sign of the worldview of materialism is Cancer, the crab, corresponding to the human breastplate. It was under the sign that the human form developed the basics of the ribs and thoracic cage, as a structure which encases and protects the vital organs. The ribcage can be considered the container of the heart and lungs, organs which facilitate vitality and breath. The crab’s hard shell is the perfect natural symbol for this worldview in the sense that it represents limit and protection. The shell creates a definitive, physical boundary between the soft inner life and the outer world. Similarly, materialism says that the hard “factual” boundary of physical matter defines all reality, both protecting and denying non-physical explanations. The very actions of the crab in retreating in its shell of hard matter reflect the tendency of materialism to retreat into the base level of measurable, mechani-chemical processes. Spiritualism’s constellation is Capricorn (the sea-goat), whose human correspondence is the skeletal framework. The skeleton provides the internal structure which allows the body to stand and move. Spiritualism does just this, providing the enduring structural idea that spirit is the foundational reality. The sea-goat exists in both water and on land, which respectively are the material and spiritual, or temporary and enduring realms. It is a composite creature with the tail of a fish, and the forward section of a mountain goat. This signifies the spiritualist view that no matter the domain (on water, or on land), spirit permeates and overarches all levels of being. Cancer calls on us to ground the world in the tangible and immediate world, while Capricorn nudges us towards the enduring structure behind the immediate. In the human body, this is echoed by the ribs and the skeleton. One provides a protected space for life, and the other provides the framework that allows that life to stand tall and go beyond it’s limit. A complete human being requires both.

Sensualism and Pneumatism

The immediacy of experience finds sensualism granting validity solely to sense impressions and direct experience. Everything from the understanding and reason is peeled off, and likewise, only the raw impression is left. Sensualism (or sensationalism) proclaims all genuine knowledge as originating in, and validated by, sense perception. It yells out “I only believe what I can see, hear, touch, taste, or smell”. Anything untraceable to sensory data is considered speculation or fantasy; it is meaningless to metaphysics and comprehension. Logically, it is the result of a naïve materialism, one that is more sophisticated, since it acknowledges that we don’t experience “matter” directly, but rather sense data. The domain of validity is, like materialism, found in the empirical sciences, natural history, or really any discipline which relies on careful observation. It provides the ground of factual certainty about the external world. The sensualist, or sensationalist, can be described with an attitude of outward receptivity. The soul here is akin to a passive mirror, faithfully recording impressions upon it from the external world. The limit lies in the fact that it cannot account for the organizing principles of the mind, such as the unity of consciousness, which shapes and interprets these sensations but, are not themselves sensory. The strict sensualist struggles to explain how we move from raw sensation to coherent principles.

Pneumatism, on the other hand, sees the immediacy of experience and claims that the senses are a doorway to the spiritual breath, or pneuma, that animates and organizes the world. For the pneumatist, the growth of the plant cannot be accurately characterized with a sequence of observable stages. The plant is expressing an inner life force, just as a thought is not merely a brain, but the resulting activity of a spiritual being. The relational attitude here is in and through, seeking a reality manifesting within the immediately sensory world. While sensualism’s starkness is corrected with a re-enchantment, a one-sided pneumatism risks devaluing the disciplined observation of the sensory facts themselves. Its proper domain can be considered any family member of spiritual perception.

In the case of a sensualist emerging from their shell of immediacy, it would likely begin with a routine and steadfast observation of the sensory world. We can take a botanist, who happens to be a pure sensualist, kneeling in a meadow. Her truth is measured in data. The precise hue of a petal, or the exact scent compound presents the world as a magnificent, but closed, encyclopedia of sensory facts. She believes that to know a flower is to exhaustively provide a catalog of its empirical profile. After years of this, a revolution begins to stir in her heart. She no longer sees the isolated data points, but begins to notice and accept the phenomena of process. The unfurling of a bud, the slow solar tracking of a stem, etc. mark that she has not abandoned her senses, however she has accepted the unity of sense impressions in the form an appearance. In fact, she uses her senses to watch the show of the flows, noting how the appearance of the plant changes with their attention and the time of day (phenomenalism). This procession of forms insists on some stable unity behind the forms. She then becomes convinced that these appearances are not arbitrary, they are reports from some independent, enuring entity that exists outside her perception (realism). Studying this, its solid form dissolves into activity. Suddenly, she stops seeing “phenomena”, and instead sees a nexus of processes and forces like fluid dynamics, photosynthesis, and cellular division. The plant is thus cognized as a becoming (dynamism). What holds this dynamic event into this specific persistent pattern? This question would lead her to conceive of the plant as a coherent, self-contained unity of force: a Monad (monadism), only to inevitably reflect on this indivisible unity, and the nature of this organizing center. A machine has parts; this has an indivisible, self-directing essence. She concludes, then, that this essence cannot be material. The core of being is non-physical. Everything is essentially spiritual in nature (spiritism). Finally, being enraptured by the living, spiritual, flower, her perception completes its turn. At first seeing Spirit as this remote, abstract conclusion, she feels it directly, that intelligent breath (pneumatism) which gathers moisture and light, harmonizing it into the beautiful rose.

The reverse path would find the pneumatism reckoning with inner forces that are vague and mystical. Seeking to escape speculation, one would make a conscious retreat into the solid, unquestionable ground of immediate sensory experience as a reliable ground for knowledge, dismissing inner perception as subjective fantasy. Life does not always go in such a linear and polarizing manner, however, these transitions and journeys are here to aid the spiritual scientist in “enlightening” someone stuck in their ways.

Sensualism’s constellation is Leo, the lion. The king of beasts bestows upon us the knowledge of us as being constituted by a radiant center and vital force. This radian “I” mirrors the stance of the sensualist. Just as the sun, which is the ruler of Leo, is the radiant center of our solar system, the sensualist’s conscious “I” is the central receiver and validator of all sensory data. Knowledge beings and ends with the sense impressions cast upon the self. The human correspondence here is, of course, the heart. It being the engine of life, delivering vital force (such as blood) to the entire organism, reminds one of sensualism’s captured attention. Sensualism is concerned with the vitality of immediate experience- the raw life of the senses before any interpretation or synthesizing. As a fire sign, but not a wild passionate fire like Aries, Leo represents the steady, warming glow, just as sensualism provides the warm and familiar, undeniable certainty of direct experience. “I feel the heat, I see the color”. This only makes it right that the sensuous is a reliable beacon for knowledge. Pneumatism is Aquarius, the water bearer, pouring out water from the bucket. The “water” being poured out is the universal animating spirit (or breath) of life. Pneumatism perceives the spiritual force flowing in and through all beings, just as Aquarius pours water for all of man. The influence which the constellation has had on man is found within the lower legs and circulatory system. In the ancient Zodiac man, Aquarius governs the lower legs from the knees to the ankles, and on the more esoteric side, is linked to the distribution of life fluids. The lower legs circulate the body, connecting it to the earth and enabling movement. The circulatory system distributes oxygen (the physical correlate of spirit/breath). Pneumatism likewise sees the spiritual “breath”, or pneuma, circulating without exception. Aquarius, despite the water being “poured out” (much can be said on this), is actually an air sign, associated with the higher ideas and mind. Unlike the more mystical feeling of Pisces, Aquarian spirit is often perceived intellectually as a formative principle. Accordingly, the pneumatist seeks to understand the laws and patterns as it manifests in life processes. Together, these constellations paint a duality of the centripetal and the centrifugal. In the human body, this relationship is between the heart, and the circulatory system. One is the sovereignty of the whole, but the other, equally important, is the means by which this sovereignty is circulated through the whole.

Phenomenalism and Psychism

The worldviews which meditate on the nature of appearance and consciousness are phenomenalism and psychism. Phenomenalism argues that we can only truly know and speak about phenomena, which is defined as the world as it appears to our consciousness. To put it differently, the thing-in-itself is permanently inaccessible. Knowledge can only be categorized as the systematic description and correlation of appearances. While sensualism trusts raw sense data, phenomenalism recognizes that our perception is already structured by consciousness, so it is a very cautious and skeptical worldview. Valid, surely for the descriptive sciences or phenomenology. Any discipline that rigorously brackets out assumptions about ultimate causes and unities finds its home in phenomenalism. It’s strength likewise is in its intellectual honesty. It gives no freebies. However, the attitude of meticulous observation and correlation can only go to the extent of depicting the relationship between phenomena. It never knows their essence, and this, in essence, is its resignation. The core reality of a thing is unknowable, which can lead to a fragmented world of appearances (of phenomena), naturally leading phenomenalism into the constellations of realism, dynamism, monadism, etc.

Psychism forms the direct counter to this stance as it relates appearance and consciousness, confronting a point which phenomenalism sidesteps entirely. If the world is a field of appearances, to whom or what does it appear? The core declaration of psychism is that the soul, or psyche, is the active and primary reality. The phenomena surely can’t land upon a passive receiver; they are, in an undemanding way, generated or even organized by the perceiving psyche. Such a view is very powerful in the domain of inner experience, and any study where the shaping power of perception and interpretation is central. Its validity lies there. The relational attitude is one of subjective origin, looking inward to the perceiving self as the ground of the experienced world. Unfortunately, in its one-sided form, psychism risks losing the external world, where other minds are projections of one’s own psyche, thereby losing the shared, objective reality that science and common sense require.

Finding a phenomenalist transitioning to psychism begins with the typical cataloguing of appearances. Then, the consistent, lawful nature of phenomena begins to suggest that things are not random, but that there is an ordering principle at play (realism). One then may ask themselves, “what is the nature of the context in which appearances arise? What is the ‘I’ that observes this consistent play?”. By deeply contemplating the act of observation itself - after all, the world is bracketed in this worldview, and so the only other observation aside from phenomena is observation itself - a realization that phenomena are always “for-consciousness” dawns on the soul. The observing psyche is the active, unifying precondition for any world to appear at all. The center of gravity is shifted immanently from the appearances, to the appearing power, and this lets one know, within the constraints of the phenomenalist, something about the world. That something is that the things in the world, hereby known as unknown, emerge from their encounter with the psyche.

Conversely, the path from psychism to phenomenalism usually happens when the threat of psychism’s emphasis on the subjective ground in dissolving the ground of shared objectivity rattles the soul. To rescue a common world from solipsism, one deliberately suspends questions about the psyche’s generative power and returns to a disciplined, intersubjective description of the phenomena themselves, seeking a neutral and stable ground upon which different psyches can mutually agree.

Phenomenalism’s sign is Virgo, the virgin, often depicted holding a sheaf of wheat, which symbolizes discernment and selective nourishment. The human correspondence is the metabolic/intestinal system. Taking this into consideration, Virgo is traditionally linked to assimilation and digestion. Virgo’s task is to separate the wheat from the chaff. I don’t feel the need to draw how this connects to the bracketing character of phenomenalism. Nonetheless, the aspect which does deserve clarification is in Virgo’s purity of observation. The virgin symbolizes an untouched, pure state, and similarly, phenomenalism desires a “pure” observation, free from the contamination of metaphysical assumptions. The reference to Heidegger may be apt here, given his inspiration by the phenomenologist Husserl, and the distinction he draws between metaphysics and ontology.

Virgo is an earth sign associated with practical service. Phenomenalism serves the practical service of philosophy by providing a disciplined starting point, found within the field of phenomena, upon which other worldviews may build or react. Psychism’s constellation of Pisces, symbolized by the two fish, Christ, and having the human correspondence of feet. It also happens to be the sign I was born under, so I have a bias towards it. The symbol, featuring two fish swimming in opposite together, yet bound together, symbolize a kind of dissolution and transcendence of boundaries. As a water sign, this only adds to the representation; the element of water has no shape, taking on whatever the shape of its container is. Psychism’s claim that the psyche or the world-soul is the primary, boundless medium from which all individual phenomena and forms precipitate mirrors the water-like sign of Pisces. The fish is a central early Christian symbol for Christ, representing the divine “I”, or world-psyche that enters into and permeates the substance of the world. He is the ultimate psychic reality who says “I am the life”, dissolving the boundary between the individuated souls, and the divine spirit. Likewise, our feet carry us past distinctions drawn in the sand and tend to trivialize boundaries. Humans have been moving around since man has been man.

Realism and Idealism

The sentiment arises within the soul, at one point or another, to ask if the world exists independently of it or not. Realism posits that the world has an actual, objective reality, independent of human thought. Particular things participate in, or reflect, this distinct universal reality. For the realist, the tree first exists, has properties, and would fall if struct, irrespective of any witness. The strength of such a worldview lies in its empirical foundation. By that same token, realism is valid in the domain of understanding natural kinds, mathematical truths, and spiritual archetypes. It grants that the world is intelligible because it is structured by real, universal principles. The attitude of discovery and receptivity to objective form finds the soul’s task as discerning the pre-existing ideas manifesting in the world and consciousness. However, the limit of one-sided realism is its naivety. In its simple form, realism fails to account for the active role of mind, the senses, and concepts in shaping what we perceive and how we understand it.

Idealism here steps in and asserts that ideas and structures of mind are the primary, formative forces of reality. The perceived world is not a ready-made given, it is shaped and given its coherent form by the activity of mind or spirit. In its strongest form, the world is akin to a thought, or an idea in the mind of a cosmic subject (of course, typically God). As a matter of course, it follows that the appropriate domain for idealism is in philosophy, mathematics, and, truthfully, all acts of the purposeful shaping of reality, or areas where the formative power of idea is self-evident. Idealism, like dynamism, constitutes an advancement towards recognizing the spiritually active character of the human being. It correctly identifies that the order and significance we find in the world are reflections of our own ideational activity, just raised to a cosmic level. Hegel is an example of a thinker, who applied the soul mood of Logicism (we will describe the moods soon) within the worldview of Idealism. The idealist attitude is one of active, formative power. The risk, however, is that it can become so focused on mental constructions, that it minimizes the givenness of the world that presses upon us.

The path from realism to idealism can be described as the story of the soul awakening to its own participatory role in knowing. Say, for example, one starts with a quite confident realism. Say that your truth is the objective external world of independent objects. You are a landscaper. However, in studying this objective world, you begin to notice that it begins to speak to you in a personal way, one which makes you aware of your relationship to it (psychism, having gone through spiritualism and pneumatism). The inward focus on the animating spirit brings to mind that the world is a product of the interaction between spirit and psyche- that the objects within it including their forms and relationships are constructs and ideas. The external tree is a manifestation of the ideal concept of the tree, particularly of its archetypical form and purpose, which, as a former realist, is certainly real. But this archetype can only exist in the realm of “mind”, or spirit. The physical world is a consequence and expression of an a priori, ideal reality that you can only engage with if you also interface with it on the level of ideality (idealism).

The opposite route begins, of course, with one as an idealist. Convinced reality is fundamentally mental, with the physical world being a derivative manifestation, what truly accounts for the unyielding presence of the world? Perhaps this was evoked by a friend. “If the world is simply ideas, what if I punch you?”, they say, and instead of retreating back to idealist dogmatism, you posit that the laws of rationalism, and subsequently math (mathematism) give these ideas objective necessity. Perfect mathematics needs to describe something, and this leads to the postulate of a concrete substrate: materialism. We know this matter only through raw sense data, which comes to be recognized as private appearance, leading to phenomenalism. And of course, the consistent ordering of these phenomena forces the inference of a stable, external cause, hence realism. Although in this case not as “clean”, transitions in worldviews, such as from idealism to realism occur.

True spiritual science is higher than both positions and affirms both. There are objective spiritual realities and archetypes (realism), and the human spirit actively participates in their knowing and unfolding (idealism). The goal is a living thought, which is simultaneously a cosmic truth and creative act.

Realism is Libra, the scales. The human correspondence is the pelvic girdle region, including the loins and kidneys. Libra is traditionally associated with kidneys, seeing as they are the organs that filter out and balance the internal fluid environment based on the body’s objective needs. Symbolically, the scales are judgment, objective measurement, and of course, balance. Libra seeks equilibrium between opposing forces in an external, objective system. This reminds one of the realist’s conviction that there is a lawful and objective reality out there by which we act and measure ourselves with. It is independent of perception, out there waiting to be measured correctly. As an air sign, it is concerned with the higher. This means that even if it is concerned with relationships and the space between things, it is for the purpose of balance. The pelvic girdle presents the basis of balance in the human form, balancing the body’s upper weight and allowing a stable uprightness (we are, after all, upright man). Realism reflects this by condition of a worldview that assumes an objective world, upon which a stable system can be built.

Idealism is the sign of Aries, which corresponds to the head. It is under the sign of Aries that our “head” developed in our current, materialistic form. The Ram embodies the primacy of the idea, charging forward with its energy preceding and shaping its path. Aries is the first sign, and thus it is the initiator. Recall idealism’s proclamation that the idea is primary and that reality is not “discovered” out there, but initiated by the head, and shaped by the formative power of mind/spirit (the proverbial head). As a fire sign, the fire of Aries is the flame/passion of conception, the active force that imposes form and makes the findings of realism intelligible in the first place. In idealism, the world of phenomena is actively formed and constructed by thinking, such as with the example of a world-spirit like Hegel’s Geist (world here means “the object” vis a vis the subject, and so the universe. Hegelian cosmology would not allow for a “Spirit of the Earth” beyond vague conceptual remnants of such a spiritual reality, and the focused consideration of man’s particular history as a thinking being). The head rules the body in idealism. Aries rules the head. Idealism subsequently places the sovereign, thinking “I”, or the cosmic “mind”, at the helm of reality, from which all else derives form and meaning. Together, these two worldviews establish a union related to the question of the order of reality. Libra posits the measure of a real objective world (realism), and Aries posits that order and form are imposed by some kind of consciousness (idealism, whether it be subjective, objective, or what have you). Man finds this correlate in him via the dynamic of the pelvic girdle and head, where one provides a stable ground for being, and the other provides the directing idea which provides being with itself.

Dynamism and Rationalism

The worldviews which are dominated by the question of what the world’s active principle is are dynamism and rationalism. Dynamism initially emerges as a critique of static, fixed conceptions of reality. It maintains that force, energy, and dynamic activity constitutes true reality. What we perceive as stable objects are in actuality very slow processes, or momentary equilibria in the universal flux of becoming. Heraclitus stands as the dynamist par excellence, capturing the worldview in his famous edict, that “No man steps in the same river twice. For it is not the same river, and he is not the same man”. Any given living is not a “thing” proper, it can only be cognized by this worldview as a metabolic event. Such a worldview belongs duly in the domains of process philosophy and in some areas of physics, where change and interaction are the primary datum. The relational attitude it possesses, as facing outward to active processes, seeks to understand the world through the interplay of forces. However, the one-sided danger it risks is in its potential formlessness. A pure dynamism risks dissolving all stable entities, laws, and forms, into an unintelligible Heraclitean flux, leaving no ground for enduring identity, or the structured concepts by which we understand change itself. In a radical self-conception of this worldview, intelligibility itself becomes subjective, that is, subject to the various indexes of spatio-temporality or processes which invalidate all. Rationalism is the opposing polarity, running to the aid of formlessness. It insists that reason and logical thought are the primary sources of knowledge, and the ultimate structure of reality. The cosmos likewise is governed by eternal, rational laws. It comprises a coherent, thinkable order which the human mind can discover through its own innate logical faculty. This worldview retains validity in the domain of any deductive science. The weakness it risks is the one-sidedness of abstraction. Rationalism may overly schematize, becoming detached from the living and breathing, which uncoincidentally is the very vitality that dynamism champions.

If one ever finds themselves as a “dynamist”, they must at one point or another begin to make sense of this chaos. Such a process begins with a conception of how these forces are organized into coherent, enduring wholes. This leads to the concept of reality as composed of distinct, self-organizing centers of force (monadism), or perhaps immediately into the acknowledgement of the spiritual world and the hierarchies as guiding this eternal process (spiritualism). This spirit, may or may not be conceived as ever-present within all processes. At any point, one may remain in a worldview, at no fault of anyone but themselves. The most likely result after the soul cognizes spirit as governing the world, is to feel the effects of spirit on the soul (psychism), and, furthermore, to know the world as shaped by the ideas and mental forms of the subject, and the divine mind. The relationship between these ideas are then grasped as the necessary laws of logic and reason (rationalism). The contrasting avenue one might find themselves walking, is from rationalism to dynamism. This occurs when rationalism’s formal structures feel empty and lifeless. To re-animate them, one posits that logical laws aren’t dead laws, they are actually the dynamic patterns of a living cosmos, and thus returning logic to the “flow” of creative force (dynamism).

Dynamism is Scorpio. To briefly record a point on Scorpio, what we originally call Scorpio was the sign represented by the Eagle. The scorpion represents the lower, instinctual forces of poison and contraction. It is the power that binds consciousness to the material, and the sting of death and mortality. These negative forces often appear dominant. However, the Eagle represents that same power as transformed. The eagle soars above the earthly plane as a solar creature, symbolizing a regenerative insight, making it capable of soaring above the domain of the scorpion, and a spiritual alchemy that transforms the venomous sting of the scorpion into the strong vision of the eagle. This is the hidden aim of the dynamist worldview- implicitly it seeks to perceive the ascending, formative power within the flux. The dynamist who achieves this sees a world of purposeful creation. Truly dynamism should be represented by the symbol of Scorpio-Eagle, with the lower force being indicative of the scorpion, and the higher force as indicated by the eagle. Were we to not be enlightened by this zodiacal truth, we would be left with a one-sided symbol that is counter to the very tendency of dynamism. The correspondence with man is found through the reproductive systems and “organs of elimination”. Scorpio governs the pelvic region below Libra, associated with the sexual/reproductive organs and the rectums. These are the organs which reflect the previously mentioned transformative process (hence, dynamism). Were we to leave dynamism in its one-sided form, just as it is a perpetual flux, the regeneration Scorpio embodies is the cycle where the end of one state is the beginning of another, in one giant “becoming”. The necessity of the Eagle here is the necessity of the positive moment in “becoming”, the moment of “being”, next to “nothing” and vice versa. Rationalism’s sign is Taurus, the Bull, finding its place in man as the organ of speech, or the throat (larynx). It was under the sign of the Bull that the foundation of our organ of speech developed materially. The bull possesses an earthly, grounding constancy. It is the power of manifestation, which is counterposed to the view of dynamism. The fixity of the bull symbolizes rationalism’s ground in immutable, fixed laws of logic and reason. Just as the bull plows the earth into predictable furrows, rationalism holds the worldview that the cosmos is structured by predictable, thinkable principles that do not change.

The “Word”, as produced by the larynx, is highly important. In many traditions, the creative power of the divine is expressed through the “Word”, or “Logos”. Rationalism says that reason is the formative structure and power behind reality. It claims that the rational order is the cosmic order. Taurus’s bull-headed (literally) substantiality presents the polarity of grounding by which dynamism expresses the procedural character of reality with. It is the ground upon which any transformation done by Scorpio-Eagle can gain footing.

Monadism and Mathematism

The final pairs of worldviews are concerned with the actual construction of the world. Dynamism posited that reality is dynamic activity. The logical direction, seeking a kind of organizing principle, lands on the monad as the subject of the activity of dynamism. The core assertion of monadism, as classically articulated by Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz, is that reality is composed of monads. These are simple, immaterial, soul-like substances that are the true [metaphysical] atoms of the cosmos. As Leibniz declared, “The monad… is nothing but a simple substance that enters into composites”. Each monad is indivisible and ceaselessly active, reflecting the entire cosmos from its own unique perspective. For the monadist, the rock, plant, the bird, and man are conceived of as expressions of unified centers of force. This worldview claims, or at least should claim, validity in the domain of consciousness studies, the metaphysics of individuality, and perhaps even holistic systems theories. Any domain where the integrity and interiority of beings are central may find monadism a welcome bedfellow. The relational attitude of this worldview is outward from the individual unit, seeking to understand the whole by starting from its self-contained constituents. Rudolf Steiner, in his lectures, highlighted Leibniz’s concept as a bridge between the dynamic universe, and how it necessitates individualized centers of being. However, monadism’s one-sided limitation is found in the fact of its disconnection. A pure focus on discrete units struggles to account for the actual existence of palpable relationships and interactions, of which these bind the cosmos into a coherent system and totality. Mathematism steps in here in an effort to alleviate this problem of disconnection. It grasps the formal, universal “language” which relates all individual entities, and fashions from it it’s claim on the construction of the world. This discloses to the soul that the world is constructed by mathematical form and structure. The diverse phenomena of the world are but contingent expressions of the eternal, necessary mathematical truths. For the mathematician, the concrete monad is definitively less real than the abstract geometrical and numerical laws that govern its behavior and relations. The strength and validity here are essentially absolute in the domains of theoretical physics (which is a tragedy, given its one-sided limitation) and mathematics. Its failure is, as plain as day, its bloodless abstraction. Sure, it may instantiate a perfect, intelligible order. But divorcing this schema from the world of living substance risks presenting a reality of all syntax, and no semantics.

I doubt there are “monadists” among the readers of this essay, however, since I have established it as tradition, I will include a possible path that the monadist might take to mathematism, in hopes that the spiritual scientist will guide a dogmatic worldview warrior into the light. Let’s say, for example, that a monadist is contemplating a universe of individual metaphysical/spiritual units. To understand how these coexist, one must consider the underlying spiritual reality (spiritualism) that they all share. This spirit, in an effort to not be accused of formal universality, is cognized as the animating breath (pneumatism) that actively organizes and connects all monads. From there, the connection to the universal forms and ideas (idealism) of the spiritual world and the subject, and the logic and reason inherent in the ideas (rationalism) make themselves known. The monadist then discovers that the most unambiguous expression of these rational relationships is found in mathematical structure and form.

Leibniz is discussed in Steiner’s lectures really to illustrate how monadism, properly understood, is more of a way station, rather than an endpoint. Leibniz’s monads, while spiritual and individual, were conceived as obeying a pre-established harmony (a fundamentally mathematical rational order set by God). This shows how monadism, by its own concession, points toward the rational and mathematical harmony (rationalism and mathematism) which orchestrate the relations between monads, making them a coherent universe in Leibniz’s system. Recontextualized this way, it might make more sense how one can find themselves as a mathematics or rationalist, and make their way to monadism. Mathematical structure lives and becomes manifest somewhere, and so this demand is met with monadism, where a concrete, specified existence populates the world.

Sagittarius is monadism. This constellation is of the centaur archer, and it’s influence on man is found via the thighs and femur (which happens to be the strongest bone in the body). One must ask themselves, why is the archer figure aiming at a distant target? The trajectory suggests a distinctly sovereign being with a purposeful direction. Monadism reflects this self-contained sovereignty by having each monad follow its own developmental trajectory towards its goal. The centaur figure has been discussed on this website before here, but as a brief summary, it is representative of a half earthly, half heavenly being. The centaur is a bridge between the lower and the higher, and each monad is precisely such a bridge, engaged with and reflecting the entire cosmos from its position. The being aimed by the centaur is aimed at the heavens. What this tells us about monadism is that it is about individual centers of perception/consciousness that are embedded in the material world, striving toward greater realization and truth. Mathematism is the sign of Gemini, the twins. Everything in man as it relates to symmetry is thought (or to be thanked) by Gemini and taken into himself. Namely, these are the arms, hands, lings, and nervous system. The arms and hands are the primary tools we have for manipulating and relating objects to each other. The activity of the lungs as they exchange air, and the nervous system as it exchanges signals, are also the mark of Gemini. This is all to say that the Twins represent communication and duality; they are the principle of relationship itself. Being an air sign, concerned by the space between things, such as ratios and dialogues, mathematism naturally takes this to its conclusion. Reality is these abstract relationships and ratios. The “things” related are actually secondary to the perfected form which relates and unifies them. We can view the arms and hands here as the herald of mathematical Gemini- they are tools of the intellect. Consider the hands’ almost alien design. They are tools specifically made to manipulate and tinker. Gemini is the force behind this archetypal aspect of man, as a concrete mind that discerns differences and patterns. Mathematism then employs this faculty to abstract from all concrete phenomena, looking for the underlying “grammar” which connects them all together.

The interplay of the thighs and symmetrical aspects of our form gives us the capacity to be distinct, and also the capacity to relate, connect, and understand the world through interacting with its formal unity. This final pair of zodiacal signs brings us to the penultimate step of circularity, expressly that the cycle begins all over again. With the completion of the cycle, we have fully travelled through all twelve worldviews, and explored the fact that each worldview is a one-sided truth which finds its completion in its opposite across the circle, and its full context in the whole of development.

The 7 Soul Moods

If the worldviews ask of what (such as what is ultimately real), then the “soul moods” ask how (as in, how does my soul actively engage with reality to know it?). To really understand the full extent of the capacity to internally conjure life in the soul, a distinction between the conclusions we reach about the world, and the character of the inner activity by which we reach them, must be made. Such a distinction is important, since it can help us explain how two thinkers can share the same worldview, yet produce radically different philosophies. If not for the fact that truth becomes liberated from the dogmatic attachment to any single conclusion, then for the fact that soul moods speak to the diversity of engagement that all people exhibit a modality, we must explore the various soul moods. For example, both Hegel and a mystical philosopher might operate within the worldview of idealism, the view of the world which finds ideas shaping reality. However, Hegel’s mode of engagement, his soul mood, was logicism. His cognition was driven to connect every concept into a network, forming one giant logical organism. The mystic’s mood might be mysticism, which looks for an immediate felt union with the divine Idea beyond all logical chains. They share a “what”, but differ in the “show”. Comparably, a materialist with a voluntarist mood would see the material world as a sandbox of clashing blind forces, while a materialist with an empiricist mood would see it as a collection of neutral sense-data to be catalogued. The mood colors the worldview (and the soul “tones” likewise will depict the “lightness” of a given configuration), transforming the character of the worldview and its implications. What this tells us about thinking is that, like the capacity of the human spirit, there is a great diversity of thought in each person. Additionally, the solar parallel does not end with the worldviews. The twelve worldviews presented us with the “fixed” form of the zodiac, and now, the seven moods will present us with planets moving through them, creating the various possible cognitive configurations brought to us by thought.

These planets differ from the planets we know today- they are called the occult order of the planets. The reason for the usage of this ordering is considered by anthroposophy to be a correction of a spiritually significant historical error. Steiner claims that medieval esotericists deliberately concealed true knowledge by swapping the positions of Mercury and Venus, as a “mask” meant to protect spiritual truths from misuse. By returning to the ancient geocentric order based on sidereal orbital rhythms (Moon, Mercury, Venus, Sun, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn), Steiner claims to restore the correct spiritual framework. Mercury here is re-established as the true “Morning Star”, framing its influence as representing the principle of conscious, individualized spiritual enlightenment fulfilled through the Christ impulse. Furthermore, Steiner clarifies that this system is based on their qualitative, rhythmic influences, compared to modern “science’s” external spatial arrangement. The sequence reflects the increasing sidereal periods (which is the time it takes for a planet to return to the same point, relative to the fixed stars). Because of this, the seven soul moods are the direct cognitive and experiential expressions of these specific cosmic rhythms within the human soul. Using this “corrected” order guarantees that the system of soul moods is built upon an objective spiritual-scientific understanding of how cosmic evolution and the influences in and on the world (including rhythmic forces) are decidedly wedded.

Gnosis

Gnosis comes to us as the first soul mood. It’s defining characteristic is that it seeks knowledge through the internal cognitive forces of the soul. A person in this mood strives for a clear, intimate grasp of their subject. We can explore a few concrete examples of what is meant. Take the worldview of spiritism, in the soul mood of gnosis. Such a person would have a “deeply illuminated insight into the relationships of the spiritual worlds”. A gnostic of idealism would be someone who can picture the ideas they speak about, with a sharp, concrete clarity in their soul, “as intensely as a man grasps external things with his hand”. A gnostic of realism would find a person finely tuning perception for external reality. Steiner contrast them with some theosophists, noting that a “Gnostic of Realism” might walk through a picture gallery, or nature, and make abundant comments based on a deep connection with what is seen, while others without this mood might understand nothing. There is another example Steiner uses in the gnostic of materialism. Admittedly, he calls this one strange, but possible. It describes a person whose feeling and perception are only for the material. They try to know matter by coming into direct, intimate contact with it. He likens it to a dog sniffing substances to know them intimately.

The connection of this mood to the planet Saturn has both historical precedence, and a spiritual-evolutionary significance. The ancient, esoteric Gnosis that flourished from the 4th to 1st millennium BC was based on the direct, intimate soul experiences, and vivid “picture ideas” that arose from within, made possible by the developmental stage of the Sentient Soul.

“The Gods, who hitherto revealed themselves to him in a garment of sense, reveal themselves now in the garment of the Spirit. This was the age when the Gnosis really originated and had its life.”

Saturn, or Old Saturn, represents the first primordial stage of our planet’s evolution. The gnosis soul mood harkens back to this primordial form of spiritual knowledge, to a knowledge that came from within, from the soul’s direct resonance with cosmic ideas, before the intellectual soul became dominant.

Saturn, in reflecting the quality of consciousness that gnosis entails, finds its archetypal role as a kind of boundary definer. True gnosis is defined as the disciplined effort to delineate spiritual realities with precision, defining the contours of supersensible knowledge with as airtight a certainty as mathematics. In essence, this mood creates a structured “inner space” for knowing.

Logism

Logism is the second soul mood. Its defining characteristic is that it wants to connect thoughts and ideas into an organic system. A person in this mood sees ideas as interrelated members of a coherent whole, and strives to build a logical system of thought.

If we take the previously mentioned example of Hegel, who is in the worldview of idealism, but the soul mood of logism, then it can help us reflect on how his philosophy provided an immanently arranged system of all possible concepts into a great “concept-organism”. A logician of psychism would likely approach the realm of the soul differently; Fichte can be cited as an example, constructing a logical system that originates from the active perceiving psyche “I”. One may also be a logician of monadism, systematically relating the logical interactions between the monads. In any case, the core principle remains. Regardless of the worldview constellation, the logism mood manifests as the drive to unify all thoughts into a rational whole.

The connection of this mood to Jupiter (or Jove) is found today. Logism is linked to our modern stage of the Consciousness Soul, which is under Jupiter’s dominion. Jupiter represents the next great evolutionary stage following our current Earth era (if this doesn’t make sense, familiarize yourself with the evolution of the Earth in spiritual scientist). Logism, with its capacity for systematic, all encompassing thought, prefigures and actively prepares the human mind for this future state of consciousness. It may be considered a cognitive forerunner of a more unified, spiritually conscious way of knowing.

The archetypical role is that of systematizing unifier. In mythology, Jupiter is the sovereign king who brings order and harmony to the cosmos. This corresponds to the logicians task of bringing the same order and harmony to the realm of concepts. Likewise, its activity is to build these expansive conceptual bridges between all kinds of knowledge, integrating all ideas into a single logical system.

Voluntarism

Voluntarism’s defining characteristic is that it perceives and interprets the whole of reality as a manifestation of will. For a person in this mood, the active principle behind all existence is will.

Arthur Schopenhauer is perhaps the most infamous example of this soul mood, in the worldview of psychism. His entire metaphysics is described as “voluntarism in the spiritual constellation of psychism” by Steiner. For him, the unifying world-soul is itself will; all the forces of nature are expressions of this singular cosmic will. A voluntarist of monadism would approach this in a way similar to the Austrian poet-philosopher Robert Hamerling, as found in his work “Atomistics of the Will”. Instead of the one world-will, he bases reality on many individual monads, each of which is a distinct will-being.

The connection of this mood to Mars is most easily understood with reference to the planet as a sign of impetus and energy. Mars is the god of active, driving power. Of course he is the god of war, but “war” is the manifestation of forces behind what we call “war” in the first place. This force is the primal energy that initiates movement, perfectly corresponding to the Voluntarist perception, which identifies the power that brings structures and thoughts into being, and sets them into motion.

Empiricism

Empiricism seeks knowledge in the simple action of accepting and incorporating what the world presents, without searching for hidden connections behind any given appearance. A person with this soul mood observes and accepts experience as it offers itself. Steiner provides us two examples to distinguish it:

A materialist in the mood of empiricism would accept only what appears externally through the senses, taking the material world at face value. A spiritualist in the mood of empiricism would do the same, except their experience would include spiritual phenomena that present themselves. The important point in this protocol is to not try to find a special connection behind what is presented, letting things approach and wait on its term.

The Sun is the great illuminator, and likewise is what represents the soul mood of Empiricism. Its primary function is to shine light, making things visible and known. Given that this is the soul mood most directly concerned with the bare fact of “seeing” things, it does not, in its pure form, add interpretation from within (such as with gnosis), or form a systematic logic (logism) from what is revealed. It strives to let the phenomena themselves be revealed in under the light of the sun. It’s not a coincidence that this soul mood is directly in the middle of all the other soul moods, it has a unique position as a centrality, in the same way that the Sun is a central body by which the plants all revolve around. The act of being aware of what you experience is the hallmark of sentience and the starting point for any form of knowledge. This knowledge may be sensory or super sensory. Even anthroposophical spiritual science is described as a form of “clairvoyant empiricism”, based on a systematic observation of the Akashic record. It makes sense, then, that the Sun, as it relates to empiricism, signifies illumination and the now.

Mysticism

At first glance, mysticism might seem indistinguishable from gnosis. However, they are polar opposites. If gnosis is the clear, boundary-limiting activity which clarifies, mysticism can be considered knowledge through feeling and merging within the individual soul. Its characteristic is through the inward deepening of the soul’s inner life, mystics are not concerned with re-articulating, only with building interiority with a fiery passion.

Steiner provides a clear contrast. He states that a person in the mystical mood believes that the world unveils its secrets only within the inner realm of the soul. Such a person might say:

“Of what help to me is the Gnosis that takes pains to struggle up to a kind of vision? The things of the external world that one can look upon—they cannot show me the truth. How does Logicism help me to a world-picture? … In Logicism the natural connections of thought are spun out, but the world is not grasped by that. And of what use is all Voluntarism? … The will can reveal itself to me only in my own soul. When I sink down into my own soul, when I deepen my inner life, when I bring to life within me all that lives in the soul as love, devotion, veneration, then I gain an understanding of the world.”

Venus, or Aphrodite, is the planet of love and union. Her power is that of attraction, dissolving the limits between entities. Mysticism is the soul’s endeavor to achieve this kind of union, that of the human soul and the world-spirit, or divine source. The mystic seeks to merge with the object of knowledge through their love and inner devotion, transcending the subject-object split that characterizes other forms of cognition, albeit subjectively. Since Venus represents the principle of love, for the mystic, love becomes the primary organ of perception. The path follows the postulates of inner refinement/communion, and the cultivation of a loving relationship with the universe. As a byproduct of this relationship, the mysteries of the world are disclosed.

Transcendentalism

Ascending swiftly, the mood of transcendentalism believes that the essence of all things, in their true reality, exists in a beyond. Persuaded by the conception that direct, everyday experience cannot grasp ultimate truth, a person in this mood seeks knowledge by transcending the limits of normal human faculties, to access a higher, purely “spiritual” plane of existence. Perhaps an example of this soul mood relevant to today is found in the transcendentalist materialist, who does doses of psychedelics in order to transport themselves into a “higher” plane of existence. The soul is surely satisfied by such experiences, which is why entire subcultures are developed around this activity.

Transcendentalism is linked to Mercury. Mercury (or Hermes) is the messenger of the gods who travels between Olympus (here to refer to the transcendent, divine realm) and Earth (the immediate human world). The transcendentalist soul mood conveys this mediating function between the two worlds. It is perpetually in motion, striving to leave the earthly realm of appearances behind, and bring back knowledge from a transcendent “beyond”. Mercury is associated with intellect, and an agility of thought. In a higher form, this becomes the capacity for an abstract thinking that attempts to connect the domain of thought to the realm of direct experience. A mercurial faculty is employed in order to theorize such a path.

Occultism

Occultism, nowadays synonymous with “esotericism”, is a soul mood concerned with uncovering the hidden connections and laws that operate behind the outward appearances of things. People in this mood will never be satisfied with surface-level experience, or conventional explanations; they actively investigate the veiled forces or relationships that constitute reality.

Something interesting to note, that the same thinker can be analyzed as a combination of different moods, much like we find ourselves in situations where multiple different emotions and moods bubble up within us simultaneously. For example, the poet-philosopher Hamerling was mentioned as an example of a voluntarist monadist. By the same token, he may be considered an occultist monadist, since the method present in his work seeks to uncover the hidden forces within that the will of the monad.

The Moon is traditionally associated with that which is hidden, subconscious, reflective, and often times feminine, but that is a topic which deserves its own post. The Moon reflects the light of the Sun, instead of generating its own light, in the same way that occult forces may not be immediately invisible, but are in actuality reflections of concealed spiritual activities. In more occult physiology and cosmology, the Moon is often linked to hidden formative forces that shape growth and life processes. The occultist mood is similarly concerned with unveiling these hidden formative laws.

The 3 Soul Tones

The soul tones describe the orientation of the soul in its search for truth, or the divine. They are theism, intuitive, and naturalism. A fourth, more radical stance is anthropomorphism. The soul tones deserve a distinction because they define the primary relationship between the soul and the source of meaning.

Theism

Theism corresponds to the Sun. The theist soul looks out to find that “the God who fills the world reveals Himself to me in all this”. He likens this to the “earthling who rises up when he comes into the rays of the Sun”. The Sun is the source of light and life for our system. Theism orients the soul outward and upward towards a divine source that stands beyond the immediate world, illuminating from without, just as the Sun illuminates the Earth.

Intuitism

Here the soul seeks truth “through what shines intuitively within him” is “like the intuitionist poet who sings to the Moon and is stimulated in his soul by the silvery, mild moonlight”. Just as the Moon reflects the Sun’s light, intuitism orients the soul inward to its own reflective capacity. What it does not do is look directly at the external divine “source”, nor at earthly phenomena, for it is most content in receiving and transforming higher light into an inner, intuitive knowing.

Naturalism

Naturalism corresponds to the Earth. For Steiner, the naturalist is one who “never raises his eyes to the Sun, but only looks at what the Sun brings forth for him on Earth”. This soul stops at the phenomena and processes of nature. The Earth, being the plane of manifestation where the Sun’s effects are realized, fits perfectly as this soul tone. Thus, naturalism fixes its gaze on the interrelationships within creation, without seeking some higher cause.

Anthropomorphism

Anthropomorphism is actually quite an exception. Here, this stance corresponds planetary to an Earth in isolation. Steiner describes it as adhering “entirely only to that which [man] can experience in or around or in himself”, corresponding to “the Earth, if we consider it as such, apart from whether it is surrounded by the Sun, the Moon or something else”. This represents the extreme of self-enclosure, as a complete contraction of truth into the human sphere, imagining a world removed from any spiritual context.

Configurations, configurations.

It’s possible with this array of 12 worldviews, seven soul moods, and three soul tones to describe and account for all possible attitudes. Each respective layer addresses a dimension of spiritual life by which a given philosophical stance can express itself. The most important aspect to be intuited from this overview, is that thought freely grasps all these attitudes at one point or another. If it is possible, it is already done. Additionally, transitory states also exist on multiple levels. Within the 12 worldviews, one person may be in the middle of materialism and spiritualism, or transitioning from materialism over to psychism. The mixture of moods can also play a part in a possible configuration.

Using the tool above, we can take a modern few examples and situate them within it. Take “nihilism” for example, particularly the active, cosmic nihilist. The probable worldview may be a dynamism or materialism, seeing the world as blind force or matter. The definite soul tone can be naturalism or intuitive, claiming that the universe has no intrinsic divine meaning, and so all that is present for us is here on the world as nature. Because of this, the individual must create their own meaning through will or art (hence intuitism). We may also take Sartre’s “existentialism” as a case study. The worldview is most readily a kind of psychism, or idealism, defined by a “being-for-itself”. This world gains structure (and meaning) only through the projects and choices of this human subjectivity, making the realm of the psyche, or its ideal projects, fundamental. The soul mood gives off a strong voluntarist impression, since Sartre’s philosophy claims that the core human reality is freedom and choice. We are “condemned to be free”, as he says. Our will to choose then defines us. One may also pick up on the strong element of empiricism found in his focus on the concrete appraisal of the facts laid bare. The soul tone appears to be an intuitive with a basis in naturalism. Sartre rejects the transcendent god of theism, so that soul mood is out of the question. What he claims is that the truth if found in the internal, intuitive grasp of one’s freedom and anguish, within a godless universe. Sartre’s claim that “existence precedes essence” is nothing if not a Voluntarist principle. Finally, we may take Kojeve, who represents a difficult analysis, since he mixes Hegel, Marx, and Heidegger. His primary worldview is materialism, but it is a highly sophisticated one which secularizes Geist into human labor and struggle (particularly, the struggle for recognition). To him, history is the product of man’s activity, as a being who actively and collectively produces his own being through “material” transformation. The soul mood comes off as both gnostic, and logicist. Kojeve seeks a total, intellectually coherent system in the form of a “gnostic” grasp of history’s logic. Following this, he constructs a logical dialectic of desire and struggle which forms his unitary logical concept- one that explains all historical motion toward its end. The soul tone is naturally, naturalism. His system is rigorously secular and humanist. Lacking a transcendent source, and sourcing the object from the world, theism and intuitism do not ring true as strongly resonating with where Kojeve’s philosophy says the soul should look.

The examples will stop there, with the exception of a reflexive attitude that wants to create a repository of the different philosophies and frameworks. Such an attitude would be a realist one, with it’s a soul mood being logicism or empiricism, and with its soul tone being naturalism or anthropomorphism. The reason for this is that such an attitude assumes the worldviews and stances it seeks to catalogue have an objective, discernable essence that can be correctly identified and classified. It treats philosophical positions as real forms to be discovered, and so is described best as realist. Alternatively, this could learn towards mathematism, if one were to imply that the relationship between these stances can be mapped into a system of relations, or are best described as a geometric morphism. The soul mood being logicism is self-explanatory, since the drive here is to connect all thoughts (or in this case philosophies) into one single logical organism. The reason I say it could be empiricism, is because the connective substance of a logical organism is not exactly implied! It could be the case that the intention is to merely lay out the “given” facts (the conceivable and historical stances) without incorporating judgment. Confining this search to the world lends the soul tone to naturalism, however since the project is human focused, with its scope being limited to “stances” as human thought-attitudes, then this may create a closed system of human self-reflection.

This does not stand as a condemnation or provide any normative judgments at all. As said previously, the point of detailing the possible configurations thought provides us is for the purpose of comprehension. The enemy of truth is one-sidedness. Because of this, the goal of any anthroposophist, or practitioner of a future spiritual scientist, is the ability to freely inhabit each of the seven soul moods. The moods can be said to represent distinct spiritual “senses”, and conjuring these worlds in the soul is of utmost importance for apprehending the different layers of reality. To fixate on a single mood, as if it were merely “personal preference” or a subjective stylistic choice, is akin to trying to perceive the full spectrum of color with a lens that only permits one hue. If this is the case, the anthroposophist is rendered spiritually myopic, capable of producing partially valid half-truths within a narrow band of possible experience, and, at the same time, declaring the rest of reality nonexistent or false. Such a mobility is practically a moral imperative for spiritual science, because each mood decisively explains a specific domain of knowledge. It is not a coincidence that Rudolf Steiner’s 14 chapters in The Philosophy of Freedom goes through the 7 soul moods twice.

On Forgiveness

It has taken me, personally, a long time to accept people for how they view the world. Implicit in me and you, is the premise I am to share here: The incarnation of Christ in the world is the divine Logos, God, fully entering and comprehending the human condition from within. When God became man, it was for the purpose of fostering love, and I mean this literally. The structure of the world, consciousness, and the subsequent markings of these things in history, literature, art, and politics has not been the same since the Christ has left his signature. The love to be fostered was, yes, from the divine towards man, however this has always existed, even before Christ. The purpose wasn’t a lawful worship of Christ’s command, although this would be wise to internalize as well. Nor was it a call to believe in Christ. Even demons believe in Christ and his power. Christ’s significance was a transformative event for the world and for man, to live like he did, to love like he did, and to facilitate the flowering of all beings capable of freedom. Those who keep this teaching alive are to be called Christians.

If all humans are created in the image of God, then the entire spectrum of human thought and internal activity reflects, however fragmented, facets of the divine mind and act of creation. Dismissing this or that worldview is a concession that one is dismissing an obscured reflection of God. Christian doctrine holds that Christ’s sacrifice was for all humanity. All includes the full range of human spiritual and intellectual conditions, each deepening the appreciation of redemption’s breadth and necessity. For if redemption only came to those who could already see spirit on the horizon, what use would it be for the majority, who find themselves entrenched materially in the evils of the world? God, in coming down to our level, is meeting us where we are at. All of us. So too shall we perpetuate this conviction.

Spiritual growth requires breaking out of one’s initial worldview. We may find this or that native worldview more comfortable and reasonable. We may even try to encompass the entirety of the world through one specific configuration, seeing it as impossible to disprove. However, a Christian faith must engage other perspectives to avoid a dogmatic view of the world, and in order to easily reach out to another when outreached hands invite company or companionship. One cannot effectively minister to, or heal, the human condition without an accurate diagnosis. Understanding the places where the soul can go helps provide us a precise diagnostic tool for understanding the intellectual and spiritual state of individuals and cultures. Moreover, the given system helps teach an important lesson regarding individuality. A person’s worldview is often a result of their spiritual-psychological constitution and cognitive mood. There is a separation from the person, and the limiting form of though they inhabit. I can only speak to America, who has the most propagandized, illiterate population on the Earth. This is meant in a descriptive way, however I would be a liar if I did not resent the state our culture is in now. Many Americans do not actually disagree on the principles that matter, but tend to get caught up in ideology and culture war battles. The correct approach to have is the Christian approach; we are to awaken the individual soul from its somnambulant state and let it freely come to embrace positions it may have vehemently disagreed with in the past. As a matter of course, comprehending the internal logic and valid domain of each worldview fosters an intellectual charity in one. It allows one to see why someone of one political or philosophical persuasion thinks as they do, recognizing the partial truth they grasp, rather than simply labelling them wrong.

Comprehension being the ground of forgiveness has quite the peculiar wordplay. The words literally mean to grasp and to give up, respectively. One can only conclude that forgiveness involves an active achievement, requiring action from the soul. You must first grasp together the full context of the other, holding their conditioning and fragmentary truth in the understanding, before you forgive. Only with the solid ground of comprehension can one then give up completely the personal claims of resentment and retaliation. Recognizing how a soul may be captive to a one-sided worldview frames sin partly as a form of spiritual bondage, although this is surely not the full story. The spiritual logic of grasping does not end with understanding the person; it must extend to grasping the serious nature of the transgression. If forgiveness means giving up a personal claim, it definitely does not mean giving up on truth. The comprehension that enables mercy must therefore inform a sober recognition of sin’s cost, ensuring forgiveness does not turn into a passive endorsement. The command is to “love in truth”, and the discernment provided by an anthroposophically informed framework identifies more than that one is sinful or erroneous, but in how they miss the mark.

A dissimilar notion has been communicated by Blaise Pascal. Our differing eyes create incommensurate realities, and the refusal to see them as alike is the root of a cleaving judgment. True comprehension is the work of seeing through these different eyes, dissolving the false certainty that fuels condemnation from another. Likewise, full comprehension opens one up to the beatific, greater reality that they may be rejecting.

“We view things not only from different sides, but with different eyes; we have no wish to find them alike.”
