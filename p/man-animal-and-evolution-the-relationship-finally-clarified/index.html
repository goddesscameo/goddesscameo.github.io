Man, Animal, and Evolution: The Relationship Finally Clarified

The form of man and its future.

28 minute read

Intro

After writing my recent article Epistle to Polygenism, a few insights came to mind regarding the human form and it’s arrangement, going hand in hand with an elaboration of the relationship between man and animals. This article is on this consideration. In this undertaking, the process describing the generation of embodied form these beings take is necessary, namely, an explication of “evolution”, which is the name for this process. In its conventional usage, “evolution” refers generally to the gradual physical transformation of species over geological time, driven by variations and natural selection (which is a catch-all term employed to describe a failure for an organism to reach biotic potential). This framework has provided the world with a few insights, surely, yet it leaves more basic questions unanswered. Why did self-consciousness emerge only in one lineage? What is the inner relationship between the human form and the animal forms that so closely resemble it? Why does the fossil record show long periods of status punctuated by sudden appearances, rather than the smooth continuum the theory might predict?

To address these, we must expand our understanding of evolution to include dimensions that the physical sciences, by their very methodology, cannot access. Spiritual-scientific research offers precisely such an expansion. In this account, evolution is not merely the history of bodily forms. It involves the evolution of the planet, of spirits, or the ideas and concepts by which perceptions are generated by, the whole of nature, and so on and so forth. Thus far, the only theory in materialistic sciences approaching such a conception is the Expanding Earth hypothesis, which can explain everything modern plate tectonics does except for not taking as an assumption the fixed radius of the Earth over time. In addition, it also answers questions such as the relatively young age of the ocean floor, and a few others. Besides this theory, modern materialist science treats nature as dead, and if not dead, then it is dead at the moment of observation, put into hypostasis for the thinking subject. Evolution as properly understood involves the spiritual hierarchies who guide cosmic and earthly development, dimensions of evolution such as Conditions of Life, Form, and Consciousness, planetary stages, and involution and sacrifice wherein higher spiritual beings descend into lower states to create conditions for humanity’s ascent. Evolution can be said to be sustained by a cosmic sacrifice. Within this framework, the relationship between humanity and the natural kingdoms, for instance, does not appear to us as one of linear emergence. It is a stratified separation. Telos is also involved. If something is developing, there is something it develops towards, and so the spiritual science’s understanding of evolution does not end with the present, nor does it end with biological organisms. Everything in nature is alive, as Schelling states. However, this will be elaborated on at a further date. The question concerning us today is along the following: Why do we look like apes?

Monkey

Humans and great apes have a strikingly similar skeletal structure. The arrangement of limbs, the opposable thumbs, the facial musculature, and so on, have long been held up as the crowning evidence for Darwinian evolution. When Huxley, Darin’s “bulldog”, famously toured the British Isles with a chimpanzee skeleton, his message was very clear. The human body is merely a modified ape body according to him. The resemblance is so close that comparative anatomy leaves little room for doubt. We share a common ancestor, the ape form is our past, and the human form is a more refined variation of the same basic blueprint, problem solved. This argument has become the bedrock of modern evolutionary biology.

Note: The entire field of evolution tends to be pseudo-science, lacking all forms of explanatory value or normativity. Whether evolutionary psychology or biology, it does not matter. What a trait in one organism is said to be an adaptation, the respective opposite trait in another is also said to be a beneficial adaptation. For instance, a dull color in a bird may be beneficial in order for it to survive and reach biotic potential (reproduce). Within the same species bright-colored feathers is explained away as beneficial for attracting mates and reaching biotic potential. If any trait can be rationalized post hoc, its explanatory power becomes null. More on this: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Just-so_story. While the counterargument to this statement is that an emphasis on different selective pressures resulting in fitness is the main point might seem attractive, further probing reveals that fitness (survival and reproduction) becomes a tautological loop of reproduction. “What reproduces is more fit, and the fittest survive” becomes “those reproduce more reproduce more”. Evolutionary psychology is similarly a fruitless domain parading itself as normative theory.

The resemblance of monkey to man is elegant, parsimonious, and seemingly supported by a wealth of genetic and fossil data. Yet for all its explanatory power, it still rests on the assumption that morphological similarity necessarily implies genealogical descent. In other words, because we look like apes, we must have evolved from them. This is an unjustified assumption that will find no solution in the materialistic sciences. Modern neo-Darwinian synthesis is ultimately a theory of form. It explains how populations adapt to environments through variation and natural selection, supposedly. There are disagreements, but we can grant this for the sake of argument. We may also grant that it successfully accounts for the diversification of species, the distribution of traits, and the patterns of genetic relatedness. The human-ape similarity fits into this framework, that we share a primate body because we inherited it from a primate ancestor, with modifications that accumulated along separated linages. However, the limit is reached precisely at the threshold of what makes us human. The aforementioned theory cannot explain why, among all primates, only one lineage developed self-consciousness, the capacity for abstract thought, moral reflection, language, art, and so on. It treats these as emergent epiphenomena of increased brain size, yet it has no account of why the brain expanded in this lineage alone, or how mere quantity of neurons could ever produce the qualitative leap to the reflexive “I”. The evolutionary biologist is left with what Thomas Nagel coined “the hard problem” of consciousness. This is a problem that neo-Darwinian synthesis and natural selection was never equipped to solve.

Additionally, Darwinism (as shorthand) treats the animal kingdom as a series of steps leading *coincidentally to man. The ape is positioned as our nearest relative, a kind of unfinished version of ourselves. This narrative implicitly carries a hierarchy, but it cannot ground that hierarchy in anything beyond differential reproductive success. Morality, art, philosophy, and language all become byproducts of a brain that happened to get larger, with an unclear object in mind, and in some cases denies the object is there at all. The human form is reduced to a contingent outcome of mutation and selection, with no intrinsic meaning or direction.

Spiritual science offers a fundamentally different reading of the same anatomical facts. In this view, the human being is the original form. Not in the sense of a physical ancestor, but as an archetypal spiritual reality that preceded the physical separation of the kingdoms of nature. Long ago, during periods of times named the “Lemurian” and “Atlantean” epochs, the human physical body was still malleable and had not yet solidified into the fixed skeletal form we know today. At various stages of this long process of solidification, certain beings that guide species from the astral plane (called group souls) took hold of physical bodies that had prematurely hardened. These bodies, which had once been part of the human stream, became the foundations for the animal kingdom. The apes represent the last such separation, occurring when the human form was already quite close to its final shape. This is why their anatomy so closely resembles ours. They are a kind of photograph of the human form at the moment it branched away. In this conception, a portion of developing humanity “lags behind” and becomes the basis for a new natural kingdom. The animals, plants, and minerals are not our ancestors; they are our own former possibilities frozen in time.

The ape, therefore, is not a primitive ancestor, but a side branch. It is a being that took on the physical form that the human stream had at a particular stage, but then stopped developing further. The anatomical similarity cannot be evidence that we evolved from apes, it is instead evidence that apes and humans share a common origin in the plastic proto-human form which existed before the final hardening of the human body.

If this reading is correct, then the fossil hominins (australopithecines, Homo habilis, Homo erectus, and even earlier forms) take on a different significance, representing various side branches or transitional forms that either preceded the full descent of the human “I” into physical bodies, or were left behind as the human form continued to refine and harden. As mentioned in my Polygenism article, the fact that stone tools appear with australopithecines shows that even before the “I” fully incarnated, beings with a degree of intelligence and will were interacting with the physical world. These beings, in Steiner’s account, were ensouled by group souls (or by individual souls not yet fully independent) who were preparing the physical “body” for the eventual incarnation of the self-conscious, reflexive “I”. The appearance of anatomically modern Homo sapiens in the Quaternary period (precisely when the Out of Africa population emerges) marks the completion of the hardening process. The physical body is stably mineralized and capable of hosting the “I”.

Conventional evolutionary theory, exactly because it limits itself to physical form and a vague notion of genetic inheritance, cannot accommodate the above picture. It has no language for telos, ideality, freedom, self-determination, etc. Yet these are precisely the dimensions needed to answer the questions that Darwinism leaves open. Why does consciousness exist? Why only in humans? Ultimately, what is the relationship between the human form and the animal form that surrounds us? Spiritual science certainly does not reject the empirical findings of paleontology, genetics, taxonomy studies, or comparative anatomy. Instead, it offers a broader view for these findings, one that interprets the same data in a fuller way and recontextualizes the information accordingly in a unified way. The ape-human similarity then transforms from an animal origin to a human origin.

Influence and Development

In order to understand the human-animal relationship, let us set aside the image of evolution as a linear chain from simple to complex, of ape to man. In its place, we must envision a process. Rationalize this process however you want; the Hegelian abstract to concrete, the alchemical process, separation to hardening to inward transformation, etc. are ways to understand the procedural moments here. This is a process that spans beyond the history of the Earth as we currently understand it into the period of time known as the middle Lemurian epoch (roughly 359–299 million years ago). If the “archetype” of man was abstract (spiritual, so to say) at some point, then this is the period it began to “harden” and manifest a body in the world for itself (in addition to the beginning of the sex distinction, among others). The human form was not yet the fixed structure we know today. Steiner describes it as “plastic” often, “soft”, and still directly shaped by the will. Bones had not yet fully mineralized, the body was more fluid, more responsive to the inner life of the soul, as a necessary condition for the incarnation of the human “I” in the world. During this epoch, the human being moved through the world with a body that was, in essence, a sculpture in process. The forces that would later become the distinct animal, plant, and mineral kingdoms were still contained within the human form. The human being was, as Steiner puts it, a kind of “potential totality” from which all other terrestrial forms would eventually be separated. It is worth noting that the Earth and man concurrently “evolve”, however, we are focused on man and animals for now.

The Atlantean epoch continued this process. The body remained more fluid than today. For example the skeletal system was still not fully solid. Yet a progressive hardening was underway. The first really durable artifacts appear only at the very end of Atlantis, marking the threshold where human will began to encounter a world that was itself becoming mineralized. The great disaster and flood that ended Atlantis finalized this transition. The human form, now sufficiently hardened, could enter the current post-Atlantean epoch as a stable vessel for the fully incarnated “I”. Why did the human form need to harden exactly? Because the “I” requires a respective stable instrument. A body that is too plastic cannot reliably support the kind of self-conscious reflection that defines true humanity. The hardening of the skeleton, the mineralization of the bones, the stabilization of the brain structure, etc. are the invented culmination of a long spiritual preparation. The fossil record, when read this way, reflects this gradual solidification. The reflexive, self-conscious human “I” did not always dwell in a physical body though. During earlier planetary stages of Earth’s development, the human soul moved among the spiritual hierarchies, gradually preparing for embodiment. The descent into a physical form in one sense is considered a sacrificial act. The higher “I” bound itself to a body that would limit it, in order for it to gain the capacity for freedom, self-knowledge, love, etc. This descent did not happen all at once. In the Lemurian epoch, the “I” began to inhabit the still plastic human form, but was not yet fully integrated. During the Atlantean epoch, the “I” deepened its connection, but still the body was not entirely stable. Only in our epoch did the “I” achieve full-self consciousness within a fully hardened body.

This brings us to today. If the animal kingdom consists of forms that separated from the human stream at various stages, then the human being accurately is more than the synthesis of animal traits as a being who inwardly transformed what animals externalized. This is the principle of metamorphosis which Goethe recognized in plants where leaf transforms into sepal, petal, stamen, etc., and in animals, where the vertebrae transformed into skull bones. This extends into the whole of evolution. The human form is a metamorphosed unity of what appears in the animal kingdom as separate, specialized organs.

Consider the fact that what in animals is an external organ becomes an internal organ in humans. The fish’s swimming bladder, which regulates buoyancy, is metamorphosed into the human eardrum. The fish’s gills become the tiny bones of the ear. What was once spread out in water becomes concentrated and internalized. What in animals is a specialized function becomes a unified capacity in humans. Steiner talks in depth on the siphonophore, a colony of distinct individuals each performing a single function such as balance, feeding, movement, reproduction, or defense. This is a kind of deconstructed human. In the human being, all these functions are drawn together into a single self-conscious organism, held together by the “I”. When the “I” fails to hold them together, we see the fragmentations of mental illness, a kind of return, in distorted form, to the condition of the siphonophore. One may also consider that a one-sided development becomes a balanced threefoldness in humans. Animals are, as Steiner says, “human beings which have remained stationary in the one-sided development of their characteristics”. The ant has developed social organization to an extreme, but lacks individuality. The beaver has developed engineering wisdom, but cannot innovate. The human being, in contrast, holds thinking, feeling, and willing, in a balance that requires the insecurity and openness that the one-sided animal lacks.

This inward transformation certainly didn’t happen automatically. It was guided by spiritual beings working from the astral plane. Since animals don’t have an “I”, they are guided by animal group-souls, beings on the astral plane who direct the collective intelligence of the species (think the beaver’s dam, the bird’s nest or migration, and the bee’s hive). The third elementary Kingdom works directly on animal forms, shaping them according to instincts and desires. In the human being, these formative forces are internalized. The same forces that shape the animal from without become, in the human, the inner forces of conscience and self-direction. In other words, the human “I” learns to do consciously what the group soul does instinctively.

The ape occupies a special place in this picture. It separated from the human stream last, when the human form was already close to its final shape. Its anatomy is therefore most similar to ours for this reason, because it froze at the stage just before the human form achieved its full uprightness, the capacity to receive the “I”, and so on. Moreover, Steiner notes that the ape “brings too much from the group soul down into its own individual existence”. Unlike lower animals, which fully return to the group soul at death, the ape retains something in its physical organization that cannot go back. This gives it a quality that appears almost individual- a something that resembles the human “I”, but is in fact a stalled potentiality. This residue becomes, in the spiritual world, the elementals called salamanders, beings that are neither fully human, nor fully animal, lingering as a kind of unfinished creation.

Everything is for a purpose or, the form of man

The animal kingdom consisting of potentials separated from man is on the one hand. The other hand focuses on what the animal kingdom is a derivation of: the human form. Then human form can thus be understood as the living synthesis of these potentials, more than the sum of the parts. It contains these potentials by having turned them inside out, under the sovereignty of the “I”. Among all features that distinguish the human body, three stand out as especially revealing. First is the upright posture, then the articulate larynx, and finally, the free hand with its opposable thumb. Each of these is a metamorphosed animal organ, an inward transformation of what in the animal kingdom appears as an external structure.

The most visible difference between humans and the higher animals is the vertical orientation of the spine. In the animal, the spine runs horizontally. The head is attached at an angle, its weight being carried by the forelimbs. In the human being, the spine stands upright, and the head balances freely atop it. For conventional biology, this is a simple and mere addendum to bipedalism, one that freed the hands for tool use. For spiritual science, that which subsumes the material sciences, it is the physical expression of the “I”. Steiner explains that the upright posture serves as a necessary condition for self-consciousness, in addition to it being a mechanical advantage. When the spine is horizontal, the astral body flows predominantly in the direction of the Earth’s surface, anchoring consciousness in instinct. But when the spine becomes vertical, the astral body can orient itself toward the cosmos, allowing the “I” to descend into the physical body. The upright human being stands as a column connecting heaven and earth, as a microcosmic counterpart to the cosmic order.

The uprightness is also the basis for the threefold division of the human organism into the nervous system, rhythmic system, and metabolic limb-system mentioned in a previous article on the development of human physiology and spirituality. Each of these three systems have their own relation to the animal kingdom, meaning in the animal, these functions are often spatially separated into distinct organs, or even distinct creatures. In the human being, they are drawn together into a single upright form, integrated by the “I”. The upright posture makes possible a second transformation via the metamorphosis of the air-organism into the organ of speech.

Consider a bird. Its entire organization is oriented toward the air, with its hollow bones, its lungs connected to air sacs extending throughout the body. The bird’s song is produced by a syrinx that vibrates with the breath. It is, in a way, an instrument of the air elementals, being shaped by the element it moves through. The song can be considered an instinctive yet direct expression of the group soul. During the Lemurian epoch, when the human form was still plastic, one possible evolutionary path was to become a bird-like being. Had the human stream talent that direction, we would have become creatures of the air, our whole organization lightened and specialized for flight, and our consciousness, merged with the astral currents of the atmosphere. But that path was not taken. Instead, the human being concentrated the air element into the organ of the larynx.

What in the bird is spread throughout the whole human body as the air sacs, pneumatic skeleton, and functional song, is in the human being inwardly concentrated. The larynx is, as Steiner describes, an inverted or “transformed” bird, it is an inverted air organ. It is the place where the air is shaped, and where breath becomes articulated sound, and subsequently where that sound becomes another instrument of the “I”. This inversion explains why the upright posture is so intimately connected with speech. Only when the spine is vertical and the head is balanced can the larynx develop the flexibility necessary for articulate language. The ape, which walks only partially upright, has a larynx that remains a call organ. Capable of signalling emotion, surely, but incapable of the nuanced articulation that goes hand in hand with thought.

The last metamorphosis we will concern ourselves with is the hand. In animals, the forelimb is specialized for a single function. The hoof is used for running, the wing for flight, the paw for grasping, the fin for swimming, and so on. Each is a perfect adaptation to a specific environment, but each is also one-sided. In the human being, the forelimb is notably unspecialized, it is the universal organ that can become a toolmaker, a painter, a gesture of blessing a caress, and similarly a strike, an insult, a warrior, etc. The universality here is a result of a double metamorphosis. Steiner explains that the human hand is not simply an unmodified animal paw or this or that, recall that the human archetype takes precedence. The specialized forelimbs are in actuality the one-sided determinations of the human hand. It is in reality the inward integration of what in the animal kingdom is distributed across different forms. The strength of the hoof, dexterity of the paw, lightness of the wing- these are all potentials concentrated into the hand. The human hand is free because it has not been forced into any single function. It can learn any function because it is guided by the “I”. The opposable thumb is the physical sign of this freedom. In the animal, the digits are often locked into a fixed pattern. In the human, they are arranged to meet one another, to create a circuit of touch and manipulation that unironically mirrors the circuit of self-consciousness.

Evolution is a constant process of turning structures inside out, and concentrating and diluting functions. “Human” is thus the category of being which holds as its characteristic positionality the metamorphosed unity of all animal potentials. As Goethe intuited, the human being is the “sum” of the animal kingdom, although this should not be read literally. It is through a transformation of each animal quality into a higher human faculty, and a gradual falling away and one-sidedness of these same qualities in man that we should view the animal kingdom in relation to us. We should come to re-cognize the lion’s courage as moral strength, the eagle’s vision as piercing insight, and the serpent’s cunning as wisdom.

Possible forms, roads untaken, and form clarified

Mentioned previously was how we could have taken an evolutionary path resulting in a bird-like form. As an avid lover of sci-fi, a question arises for me. What other forms could have emerged? The body we inhabit today is the successful result of a process of differentiation and avoidance. Guided by higher spiritual being, the human stream was consciously sculpted from many potential animal-like outcomes. Each of these potentials, had it had been allowed to harden, would have procured a creature as distinct from us as the ape is, yet each would have carried within it a parietal reflection of what the human being was meant to become. These alternative forms remain in their separated kingdoms, as reminds of the choices embedded in the process of evolution. Steiner explicitly mentions several such potentialities.

In the early Lemurian epoch, before the head had fully differentiated as the organ of self-consciousness, the human form was structured very differently, that is to say that the metabolic-limb system was dominant. The head, by contrast, was relatively undeveloped, more like an appendage than any sort of sovereign centrality. If this configuration had hardened, the result would have been a being of immense physical strength but dull consciousness; a giant. This potential is associated with the cyclopean beings of old, creatures of immense power who lacked the refinement of individual thought, with their singular eye representing a symbol of a unified and undifferentiated consciousness. This potential was avoided through the development of the larynx and the eventual specialization of the nervous system, centered in the head. The human being, instead of becoming a brute giant, concentrated the forces of will and movement into the limbs, while developing the head as the organ of self-reflection. The giant today remains a memory of what could have been.

At another stage, a different influence predominated. Beings associated with the air element (sylphs and their spiritual guides) worked strongly on the human form, offering a potential path toward the description previous of lightness and flight. Had the human stream taken this direction, our whole organization would have become quite pneumatic. We would have hollow bones, air sacs throughout the body, sing rather than speak, and have an instinctive and dispersed consciousness, fully merged with the group soul. We would have become beings of air. This potential was separated off and became the bird kingdom. The human being did not become a bird, as you can tell. Instead, the air element was concentrated into the aforementioned larynx, resulting in a spiritualized version of the air potential.

Another major potential is the one that concerns us most directly, since it produced the mammals, and among them, the ape! At the stage when the human form was already approaching its final shape, a hardening occurred. Certain beings (influenced by the Luciferic and Ahrimanic forces) could not wait for the full transformation. Their physical organization solidified prematurely, producing a form that mimics the human structure in a fixed, specialized way. Why did the ape not complete the journey? Precisely because of the temptation of Luciferic and Ahrimanic beings. During the Lemurian epoch, Luciferic beings aimed to bestow self-awareness prematurely, while Ahrimanic beings aimed to harden and calcify form before the “I” was ready to descend. The poor ape was grabbed these forces and solidified at the threshold. Its body reflects a state of a soul that is *trapped. Imitative and cunning, but tied to the physical-etheric world, carrying within it the outlines of an individuality never attained. This is why the ape has always held a strange fascination. When one gazes upon the face of an ape, they witness trapped intelligence.

The human form was guided away from these potentials by the hierarchy of spiritual beings working to consciously sculpt the human form. The Lords of Form (Exusiai) held the human archetype in the spiritual worlds, preventing it from solidifying prematurely. The Spirits of Motion (Dynamis) directed the formative forces away from one-sided developments. Even the human “I”, before its full descent, exerted a longing for a form that could house self-consciousness. The result is a body that is uncompromising in its need for freedom. The animal kingdom, likewise, is the family of forms that could have been us, had we not been guided toward something higher.

Implications and the future

The relationship posed before us has implications that extends beyond the past and into the future. Mention was made of the evolution of the Earth, matter, nature (including physics), and spiritual beings, however, we will still focus on man and animal. But it is worth noting that these will change in the future as well.

The principle of animal forms arising as separated potentials does not end with the Lemurian Atlantean, or current post-Atlantean epoch. It is a recurring dynamic, one that will be simultaneous with the evolution of humanity through successive planetary stages. The Earth is destined to undergo further transformations, with the next being called “Jupiter”, “Venus”, and then “Vulcan”. At each stage, the category of being “human”, one who is the central being of Earth evolution, will develop new faculties, and new kingdoms will branch off from the human stream. For example on the future Jupiter condition, Steiner indicates that what is currently the animal kingdom will have evolved into a kind of intermediate kingdom, reflection the separated potential of a humanity with a highly realized “I”. Meanwhile, new animal-like beings will separate from humanity. These will be beings who, during our current stage of Earth evolution, did not fully integrate the “I” into their physical and etheric bodies. The group souls that now ensoul animals will have advanced, while some human souls (or perhaps aspects of human souls) will devolve into group-soul existence, forming new natural kingdoms. Ponder this: the fact that supersensible human faculties will be developed, and that the principle of animal engendering is still occurring, suggests to us an animal kingdom in the future that will reflect an animal kingdom comprising the potentiality of supersensible perception. Psychic-apes residing in an intermediate stage of “man” and “animal” is not a discounted possibility in the future.

We may also take into consideration that the extinction of a species is not only an ecological event. It may play a part, but it is largely a reflection, and sometimes a consequence of, the general spiritual posture of humanity. When a species disappears, its group soul withdraws from physical manifestation. In some cases, this withdrawal is part of the natural rhythm of evolution. Maybe the group soul has completed its task and moves on to new forms. In other cases, extinction is accelerated, or even caused by human activity. This carries spiritual significance.

Take the passenger pigeon, which was once the most numerous bird in North America, driven to extinction in the early 20th century by industrial scale hunting and habitat destruction. Or the great auk, the thylacine, or the great American buffalo (which is not extinct just yet!), or any of the countless species lost in the wake of human expansion. From the perspective of spiritual science, these extinctions reflect a spiritual posture that treats the natural world as a standing reserve, one that severs the human being from the kingdoms that were once contained within him.

When a species goes extinct, its group soul does not perish. It does however withdrawal from the physical plane, and continues in the spiritual world, sometimes waiting for a future epoch when conditions are right for re-embodiment. But the manner of its withdrawal is shaped by humanity’s relationship to it. A species treated with indifference or hatred carries those qualities into its spiritual afterlife. A species that is honored and loved leaves behind a different seedling. This places a huge responsibility on humanity. Extinction defined as the loss of biodiversity is a bankrupt definition. The true meaning is that it is a spiritual event which reflects our own inner state. When allowing a species to vanish, we are, in a sense, amputating part of ourselves, that part of ourselves which is a potential that we have refused to recognize or redeem. The current wave of extinctions driven by human activity can be considered a mirror, showing us a humanity that has forgotten kinship with the natural world and hardened itself against the very kingdoms that once made its own evolution possible.

Now, one might object that if animals are only “separated potentials”, why do we see a clear progression from simpler to more complex forms in the fossil record? Why do later animals appear to possess more developed consciousness (sentience) than earlier ones? The direction of the process is important here. The materialist standpoint sees complexity and apparent consciousness as proof of evolution’s power, as if the process itself were gradually producing mind. From the spiritual scientific perspective, which encompasses the materialistic sciences and includes philosophy (hence the grounding of any possible deductive knowledge), the direction is reversed. Matter is the sediment of consciousness, and the increasing complexity of animal forms over geological time is the vestigial remnants of a gradual revelation of potentials that were always present in the spiritual world.

When we see a modern animal with complex social behavior or problem-solving skills, we’re witnessing the descending arc of group-soul consciousness, which was always there, now becoming more visible as the physical forms that expresses it become higher in definition. The animal does not approach humanity, rather, the group soul, over vast periods, expresses itself through forms that are increasingly capable of reflecting its wisdom. Materialist evolution, lacking this framework, will naturally interpret the same facts as evidence of a continuous and blind upward-driving process. It will bring up dolphins, the empathy of elephants, crows which solve problems, monkey which use grunts and body language to communicate, and so on as proof that consciousness is an emergent property of complex nervous systems. But this interpretation mistakes the how for the why. Still, there are those who would, in the future, see the new kingdoms engendered by man as proof of evolution. If they still believe in materialist nonsense by then, they will be relegated to the 8th sphere, becoming the animals they so desperately want to see as approaching humanity.

The human form in the future will also evolve and metamorphose further. I will bring up the larynx just one more time. This organ, which currently serves speech and song, is destined for a further transformation. Steiner describes that on the future Jupiter condition (a stage of Earth’s evolution), the human larynx will evolve into an organ of generation/creation in a much higher sense than it currently is. The word will become directly creative, as it was in olden times, only now unified. What is now spoken will then be, carrying with it the power of actualization. This future capacity will not be automatically given to all, however. Those human beings who do not properly develop the necessary faculties, such as misusing speech and hardening their hearts, will have this potential “hardened” or “split off”. They will become the seed for a new kingdom, just as the apes and other animals arose from those who could not wait for the full descent of the “I”. Yet again, future animal kingdoms will be crystallized human potentials.

Evolution is presented to us as a spiral. What happened in the past on the physical level will happen in the future on a more spiritual level. The animals of the future will not be identical to today’s animals, but they will be externalized group soul expressions of human capacities that were not individually developed. Likewise, we have before us an ethical responsibility. By developing our higher faculties, especially the “I” in freedom and love, we are also preventing the unnecessary fragmentation of the human form into new separated kingdoms. Conversely, what we neglect or pervert (or refuse to transform) can become, in future ages, a new animal-like race. The choices we make today are shaping the forms that will surround our posterity beyond.
