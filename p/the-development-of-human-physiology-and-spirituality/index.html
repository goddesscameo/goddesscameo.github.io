The Development of Human Physiology and Spirituality

Broad notes on human development.

27 minute read


Note: This is a first draft of this article, probably [not] to be updated in the future. As a result, some of the writing is not up to par, repetitive, unclear. For now, it is informative enough to post.

Intro

Human development is among the first order concerns of humanity. After all, self-knowledge is the beginning of all true knowledge, regardless of if this self is a broad and abstract notion of spirit/source, or if this “self” is the immediate ground by which the possibilities for the conditions of freedom may realize themselves. As it follows then, a brief and high level elementary note on what “man”, or “humanity”, is constituted by is necessary. Man is ultimately a category of being which is capable of self-determination. As a consequence, we are defined much less by what we are than what we can become. It’s quite a paradoxical position we find ourselves in, since in order to realize freedom and determine ourselves, certain conditions need to be in place. Namely, the possibility of failure, non-being or a gradual falling-away from the kind of category we are (which comes hand in hand with failure. It is entirely possible to do deeds which would eventually have us ceasing to be human), and most importantly, a concretization of this being in the world. These conditions demonstrate for us the fact that freedom is not given, just as the saying goes: you can lead a horse to water, but you cannot make it drink. Just as the horse needs to want the water in order for it to drink, freedom needs to be realized by the soul of the individual as a free act wholeheartedly, otherwise it is not freedom.

The capacity for freedom does not appear fully formed. In the aforementioned conditions, the unfolding of this category of being through time, such as organic, mechanic, chemical, and spiritual structures, are of key importance to actualize the decisions man makes. The precise workings and detailing of this process will occupy us subsequently, but first we must establish the sobering fact that the complex organism “man” has many perspectives by which we may view ourselves. Body, soul, and spirit are all interrelated, and likewise the temptation must be resisted to view ourselves through a singular lens. The life and development of each and every person is contrapuntal; each narrative framing of an individual is equally true as the next, forming a totality. The same can be said for the abstract sense of development to be described. Further, the human being reveals itself only through multiple, overlapping perspectives. It is through this framework that we can begin to comprehend ourselves.

Were it not for the need to make itself real in a specific kind of way, the human being would never have achieved the conditions of freedom. In other words, if the human being were to remain purely spiritual, the exposure to external impulses and influences that sway them from a realized self-determination, and likewise freedom would not be realized. As a result, the human needs an environment to both give them the opportunity to fail (be swayed by externality), and provide some kind of conditionality which acclimates it to this externality while preserving the possibility for freedom. It is necessary that the human being is posited into nature and fashions itself a body with help from the higher spirits. More will be said in another article on this topic.

Threefold Man (Or is it four?)

Throughout various cultures and history, the differing perspectives on the totality of man have a consistent through line. Each schema emphasizes a different dimension of the same human being. For instance, the trichotomy of body, soul, and spirit is the most ancient and widespread. It makes note of the difference of the physical side, the inner life of feeling and consciousness (soul), and the eternal, self‑conscious ideality (spirit). This is the broadest division. In Plato’s Phaedrus, the soul is depicted as a charioteer driving two horses, a division that echoes into Neoplatonism and early Christian theology, where Paul’s distinction of “spirit, soul, and body” became the foundation for later trichotomist thought. When Steiner speaks of a threefold functional system (to be clarified later), he is extending this old intuition into a physiological and spiritual scientific language, carefully distinguishing the living organism from the metaphysical. The fourfold enumeration finds its classic expression in the Pythagorean Tetractys. For those unfamiliar, the Tetractys is the sacred decad (meaning 10. It is 10 because 10 contains within it the enumerations of 4: 4 + 3 + 2 + 1 = 10) arranged as a triangle of ten points, symbolizing the four elements, the fourfold structure of number, and the cosmos itself. Much can be said on the Tetractys. In Jewish Kabbalah, the four worlds (Atziluth, Beriah, Yetzirah, Assiah) describe a layered descent from spirit to matter, analogous to the fourfold human constitution of physical, etheric, astral, and “I.” Steiner explicitly references the Tetractys as a prefiguring of his own fourfold model. Sevenfold schemas permeate world traditions. We have the seven chakras of yogic philosophy, the seven metals of alchemy, the seven days of creation in Genesis, and the seven planets of antiquity all point to a structuring of the cosmos and the human being into seven interrelated and interpenetrating levels, to name a few. Ninefold enumerations appear most notably in the Christian angelic hierarchies, for instance where Dionysius the Areopagite arranged the nine choirs of angels into three triads. Similarly, the ninefold Enneagram, though modern, draws on ancient roots of threefold and sevenfold patterns. For Steiner, the ninefold view emerges when we distinguish the soul’s three distinct activities, each of which can be progressively transformed by the “I.” Each of these numbers - whether three, four, seven, nine - represents a different lens through which the various spiritual traditions of man have glimpsed the same object. I’d hate to sound like a broken record, but much more can be said on this, and on the totality of this development. Among these lenses, one of the more comprehensive ways of understanding the living human being is as so: firstly a description of the more functional natural distinctions which condition freedom, then how exactly they encourage a development of the threefold soul and I, and finally, a few notes on what I call the “rhythms of life”. This will present us with a schema that unveils how the human being expresses thinking, feeling, and willing in a much more concrete way than the enumerations above. Please keep in mind that this is not comprehensive, as it does not take into account things like the relationship man has to these components during incarnated life vs. the time from death and new birth, the influence of the hierarchies, zodiacal influence, and so on and so forth.

Threefold Being

The three functional subsystems of nervous, rhythm, and metabolic-limb pervade the whole organization of man. Through each system’s “center of gravity” so to say, each encourages a relationship to consciousness and spiritual influence. Firstly is the head organization. The nerve-sense subsystem has its center of gravity in the “head” and is the most spiritualized of the three systems because, ironically, it is the most physical and the one most subject to death. It is the oldest evolutionary inheritance, formed during Old Saturn, Sun, and Moon stages, and is therefore the “dying” part of the human organism. It’s physical foundation is the nervous system, which Steiner describes as the “lyre of Apollo”- an instrument through which cosmic harmonies are translated into individual soul experience. Furthermore, it is also the bearer of thinking and conceptual life. The distinctive feature is an operation through death. What this means is that the brain requires a continuous process of mineral deposition and subtle decay to serve as the instrument of thought. The nervous system constantly “dies” in order to create the space for spirit to enter. The head is emptier than empty, a bony vault which allows spiritual rays to penetrate directly from the cosmos, unmediated by the rest of the organism. In terms of the human constitution, the nervous system is essentially physical body only. The etheric and astral bodies recede here, allowing the “I” to experience the external world with the greatest degree of separation and objectivity. This is why thinking can achieve the clarity and detachment characteristic of conceptual life.

The rhythmic subsystem centers in the chest and consists of the breathing rhythm, blood circulation, and the circulation of other fluids like lymph and chyle. It is the mediating figure between the head and the metabolic-limb system. Evolutionarily, it corresponds to the Old Sun stage and is the bearer of the life of feeling. Two primary rhythms entwine here. First is the breath (approximately 18 cycles per minute) and the pulse (approximately 72 beats per minute), maintaining a ratio of roughly 1:4. The Greek hexameter, the four pulse beats structured within two breaths, producing the characteristic “long, short, short” pattern, was understood as an artistic expression of this inner harmony. All genuine poetry and music is thus ground in the rhythmic system. Unlike the nervous system, the rhythmic system is not primarily physical. It is the domain where the etheric and astral bodies work in balance, functioning as a symbolic diaphragm and holding apart the telluric forces of the upper human being (that is to say, the tendency toward mineralization) from the cosmic forces of the lower human being (tendency towards animalization). Because of this, the rhythmic system is the seat of health. Any time rhythm is disturbed, illness ensues. It operates with a dreamlike awareness. Certainly lower definition than thinking, but more fluid and interpenetrating, which is the very character of feeling.

The metabolic-limb system is the newest evolutionary development, belonging to the Earth stage of planetary development, and looking toward the future planetary stages (Jupiter, Venus, and Vulcan). It is the “coming-into-being” part of the human organism. The most spiritual in potential, yet in its current manifestation, the most bound to earthly substance. It is the bearer of will and has a unique relationship to physical substance. What appears as the metabolic-limb system is in actuality deposit. It is substance that is continuously being excreted or shed. The real metabolic-limb system is spiritual. The physical arms, legs, and organs of digestion are the sediment left behind by the activity of the will. We can think of this in a few ways. Firstly, let us consider the “hands”. No other animal in the world, despite having fingers and opposable thumbs, can make use of the hands in order to tinker with and manipulate the world how they see fit. We may also think of the example of activities which brings the soul delight. When one reads a book which broadens and enlivens the inner life of the soul, it is in essence a kind of digestion for the spirit. This is why will operates largely below the threshold of consciousness (namely sleep), its true throne is in the spiritual forces that constantly build up and tear down the body. The metabolic-limb is also where all four principles of physical, astral, etheric, and “I” work the most intensely, while at the same time where the “I” leaves only an imprint. The will, which this system serves, is the most future oriented faculty, the one through which the human being shapes their own destiny across future incarnations.

The Threefold Soul and I

A distinction must be made between the threefold soul and the bodies of astral, etheric, and physical, or what we may call bodily principles. The bodily principles can be said to describe the “structure” and organic condition by which the threefold soul’s inner activity and “I” can express themselves. Roughly, one can say the “I” lives within the bodily principles, and expresses itself through the threefold soul. They align as so: the sentient soul uses the astral body, the intellectual soul uses the etheric, and the consciousness soul uses the physical. The “I” itself is active in all three, although its presence becomes increasingly conscious as we “ascend” from sentient to intellectual to consciousness.

The sentient soul is the lowest member of the inner human being. It is the seat of the passions, desires, antipathy, instinctive reactions, sympathy, etc. The “I” broods dully here, hardly aware of itself. It receives impressions directly from the world and responds with attraction or aversion. It is also the most ancient soul layer, connected with the astral body and with the formative forces of sound (Mars forces). The astral bodily principle (or astral body) is the structure of sensation, desire, instinct, feeling, etc., as previously mentioned. It corresponds to the element of air, since it is stimulated mainly by breathing, which draws the airy element into the organism and carries it to the head in an attenuated state. In waking life, the astral body interpenetrates the physical and etheric bodies. Its shape is said to be egg-like, surrounding the physical body like a luminous cloud, extending about 2.5 times the head’s length beyond it. Its colors vary according to individual characteristics, with a dark violet spot in the region of the brain. It also has three energy centers being the brain, liver, and heart, which in the spiritual world form a six pointed figure, and in the astral world a triangle. Worth noting is that the astral body has a dual nature as a result of its evolution, consisting of a lower part inherited from the Old Moon stage, and the higher part which merged with the lower during the Lemurian epoch. Unlike the physical body, which exists in space, the astral body primarily exists in time. Steiner explains that the astral body does not fully descend into earthly life, instead opting to remain in the spiritual world, “throwing its beam” into the physical. When we observe a person’s astral body, we are actually looking into the spiritual world and perceiving what that individual underwent before birth. The human being is thus “like a comet stretching its tail far back into the past”.

During sleep, the astral body flies out of the others, in a sense “de-syncing” from the others. This is what enables feeling impressions upon the body when dreaming. When observed clairvoyantly, it appears as two intertwining spirals. One connects to the body, and the other extends far into the cosmos. Also occurring during sleep the astral body is “poured out over the whole of our solar system,” uniting with planetary beings and absorbing strengthening forces. It is from this cosmic union that the term “astral” (from astrum, star) derives. Regarding health, the astral body continuously produces arsenic within itself. This inner production is essential for consciousness and feeling. If the astral body is too weak in its connection to the organs, the etheric body proliferates, leading to conditions like diarrhea. If the astral body is too strong, it “arsenizes” too intensely, causing a tendency toward mummification and sclerosis. According to Steiner, therapeutic use of minute amounts of arsenic can strengthen an astral body that is too weak, although take this with a grain of caution. Additionally, the use of magnesium can counter an overactive astral body.

The astral world (or soul land) is the environment of the Earth corresponding to the element air, interpenetrating the physical world, in the same way that the human astral body interpenetrates the physical body. The astral world is populated by a hierarchy of beings, known in ancient Persian teachings as Amshaspands (beings with human bodies and animal heads, working as regents over forces of nature), Izards (beings of varied forms which assist the previous group in daily shifts), and Farohards (an innumerable amount of beings with forms combining plants and fish bodies with human heads, populating the astral world in countless variety). There are in effect two astral worlds: a higher “good” one and a lower “evil” one, both with laws operating in fundamentally different ways than the physical. For example, beings can occupy the same space; there is no physical impenetrability. There is also a unity of thought and being such that opinions and ideas cannot be debated and stored as thoughts, these ideas become deed immediately. When two opposing intentions meet, they must fight, and the more fruitful one annihilates the lesser. This is why tolerance is natural to the astral world. Access to the higher astral world requires renunciation. In ordinary experience after death (or initiation), a person encounters the images of their unfulfilled desires through painful symbology or frightful beasts and such. A transformation of these images occurs when one develops the capacity for renunciation in life. We may be getting too far out of the bounds of this article, so let us return to the threefold soul.

The intellectual soul (sometimes called the mind soul or emotional soul) is the middle, mediating member. It is where the “I” becomes higher definition, emerging to form thoughts, judgments, and concepts about the world in a clearer way. Operationally, it transforms sense impressions into an ordered experience, or inner life. The intellectual soul is aligned with the etheric body and is associated with light forces (Mercury). As the seat of courage, it holds the capacity for inner pictures and the beginning of self-reflection.

The consciousness soul (or spiritual soul) is the highest member. The “I” becomes fully self-aware. It is through the consciousness soul that man comes to know more than the physical and immediate world. We begin to know the spiritual world. Steiner states: “The consciousness-soul is in touch with the self-existent truth which is independent of all antipathy and sympathy”. The consciousness soul uses the physical body as its instrument and is associated with electrical forces (Jupiter).

So what relation does the “I” have to the threefold soul? The “I” is the concept of the human being, it is the centrality by which the peripheral and “subordinated” (hesitate to use this term since this might imply the wrong kind of relationship) threefold soul follows. It does not reside in any one soul member, instead residing in all three, progressively mastering them across incarnations, across the stages of a single life, or if you don’t make it in this round of being, in future developmental stages (and if you really don’t make it in the whole “cycle”, then you’re fucked for a while). Steiner uses a remarkable image. The “I” “plays on the instrument of the soul and the different strings of human soul life”. What emerges from this “playing” is what we call human character. This relationship is developmental. In early life, the “I” is most active in the sentient soul, working through instincts and impulses. As the child grows, the “I” increasingly takes hold of the intellectual soul, learning to form judgments and moral feelings. In full adulthood (around year 35. Remember that Jesus was baptized/initiated at 33), the “I” can fully awaken in the consciousness soul, attaining self-consciousness and the capacity for spiritual perception.

Importantly, the “I” never abandons the lower soul members as it transcends, it transforms them. When the “I” masters the intellectual soul, the sentient soul is elevated and refined. This may be reformulated as the power of thinking/feeling/willing over immediate instincts; mind over matter. It then follows that when the consciousness soul is awakened, it fructifies both the intellectual and sentient souls. This is why a truly mature human being does not lose passion, nor does not lose feeling, but transforms and spiritualizes them respectively.

Note: It must also be said that each soul member (sentient, intellectual, consciousness) expresses itself through all three functional biological systems, just as each functional system serves all three soul activities. The alignments mentioned only go as far as to indicate the bias (for lack of better term) of each soul member to system.

Human character is the expression of how the “I” “plays” upon the threefold soul. Recall the metaphor Steiner uses of music. Three factors shape this music. First, the innate disposition brought from previous incarnations, or the “definite attributes and characteristics” the “I” carries with it across lives. Next, the quality of the bodily principles, which involves a bit of spiritual economy re; karma, as well as care in this life. The physical, etheric, and astral bodies inherited may come one way or another, but they must also be maintained in this life. Following the music metaphor, a great pianist cannot play well on a broken piano. Lastly, the “I”’s own development plays a part, that is, the degree to which it has awakened in each soul member through conscious effort, spiritual practice, life experience, and so on. Two sculptures are highlighted in demonstrating these dynamics. The Laocoön group depicts a moment when suffering drives the human ‘I’ out of the body. The limbs writhe independently, no longer unified by a central “I”. Laocoön represents the fragmentation of the soul. The Niobe group shows the opposite condition, where the physical and etheric bodies become quite rigid under external attack/determination, freezing into form without the animating presence of the “I”. These sculptures reveal that the true character is neither fully chaotic fragmentation, nor rigidity.

Understanding the threefold soul provides us a practical framework for self-development, both spiritual and physical. The more apt among you might have noticed I have given a tacit justification for going to the gym and exercising in the previous paragraph. But we can make this prescription even more universal. We begin by observing the interplay of the three soul members in daily life. Be conscious of when you are acting from mere sentience (impulse, desire), from intellectual soul (judgment, feeling), or from consciousness soul (clear self-awareness, moral intuition). What do you notice? Is there a partiality? The goal here is to not suppress any member, but to strengthen the “I” as the unifying center which commands them harmoniously. One-sided development, such as an overly strong sentient soul without intellectual balance, produces the fragmentation symbolized by the Laocoön. Such work also requires caring for the bodily principles. The sentient soul needs a healthy astral body, the intellectual a spry etheric body, and the consciousness soul a well-ordered physical body (particularly the nervous system). Cultivating the consciousness soul would look like specifically practicing independent thinking, developing moral imagination, and nurturing a particular kind of reverence. One which does not require subservience. It also involves recognizing that this soul member does not mature fully until the 35th year or so. There is also the karmic dimension, this is not to be forgotten. The character we bring into this life is the result of work in previous incarnations, meaning what we develop now shapes future ones, giving self-development a non-immediate significance. It sanctifies daily efforts into an enduring spiritual substance.

Rhythms of Life

The “I” does not arrive fully transformed, not in this life, nor fully in this development. That which we call the “I”, that centrality, does not spring forth armed and masterful but slowly comes to know what it inhabits and manages. This growth follows a rhythm, unfolding across the years of a life in predictable cycles. Freedom is the fruit of this development. The “I” becomes free only by consciously taking hold of each stage and mastering what it offers. For now, we will only consider the current stage of human development with a consideration of what the future holds at the end. The following section traces the following rhythms: seven-year cycles, the 18-year rhythm, and the overarching arc of human life.

The most familiar pattern of development is the septennium, which marks the gradual incarnation of the “I” into the bodily principles. At birth, only the physical body is fully “born”; the etheric body is born around the seventh year (near the change of teeth), the astral body around the 14th year (puberty), and the sentient soul begins its independent activity around the 21st year. The intellectual soul follows around the 28th year, and the consciousness soul reaches its full awakening around the 35th year. Of note to the misguided materialist is that the biological milestones here will be mistaken as the determining factor (such as the full development of the brain being attributed to the emergence of the sentient soul). The relationship is quite the opposite. The spiritual guides the material. It is only by virtue of the concept of human being (this particular stage) that these events happen when they do. When confronted with finding a materialist explanation for why puberty happens when it does, the materialist is hard-pressed, often retorting some tautology of evolution (check out other articles on evolution).

Note: The reason most “reincarnation” cases have such prominence in early ages (e.g., children reporting past life memories) is precisely because the only bodily principle settled in the newborn baby is the physical body.

From birth to age seven, the child is a being of imitation, absorbing the environment through the physical senses. From seven to 14, the child lives in a natural relationship of authority; the healthy development of this period requires figures worthy of reverence, as the capacity formed here becomes the foundation for later equality and social responsibility. From 14 to 21, the young person awakens to independent judgment and the capacity for love, shaped by ideals encountered during these years. This is why childhood development is of utmost importance in the generation and reproduction of peoples. Moreso, from the ages of seven to 14, children want authority, they often look forward to structure imposed on them, mirroring (among other things) the astral body’s structuring quality of sensation. A desire for clarity and grounding due to tangential exposure to the astral world demands itself.

A second, longer rhythm runs parallel to the seven-year cycles. The 18-year rhythm, tied to the moon’s nodal cycle (~18 years and 7 months), corresponds to the nutation of the Earth’s axis and the moon’s return to the same position relative to the sun and fixed stars as at one’s birth. At these intervals - roughly ages 18–19, 37–38, 55–56, and 74–75 - a “spiritual window” opens. During these periods, the higher self (the “I” which descended at birth) may renew its impulses for the incarnation. Biographies of figures like Goethe, Schubert, and Swedenborg show pronounced creative crises or breakthroughs at these ages.

The 35th year is highly important for reasons already mentioned. Up to this age, the human being is occupied with building up the physical, etheric, and astral sheaths. After 35, these sheaths begin to recede. The forces that previously built the body become available for spiritual work, and wisdom becomes possible. In ancient Mystery schools, one was not permitted to teach before this age. Judgment given earlier was understood to belong more to the world than to the individual. After 35, the “I” can begin to give back to the world what it has received.

In the second half of life, development continues but becomes much more individual. The etheric body begins recession around age 42, and the astral body earlier. What can be called shadowy metamorphoses of soul occur around the end of the forties, again in the mid-fifties, and at the end of the fifties. These roughly correspond to what the human race as a whole experienced in earlier historical epochs. Old age recapitulates, in rudimentary form, the developmental stages of ancient humanity, just as during development from a young age, the child goes through the development of all ages of humanity before it.

Throughout life, these rhythms find resonance with each other. It is worth noting that these are not hardline rules, but more general tendencies of development. There is normativity, but let normativity have flexibility to it.

Final note: Humanity is getting younger.

One of the most counterintuitive findings of spiritual science is that humanity as a whole is not aging as an individual, it is growing younger. Perhaps the words of Christ to become like little children is fitting (don’t throw the tomatoes, no more jokes). The mainstream materialistic view assumes that ancient peoples thought and felt much as we do today, only with less scientific knowledge. This is extremely wrong on multiple levels. The relationship between the physical body and the soul/spiritual being has shifted drastically across cultural epochs, and this shift lets us know something regarding human freedom and the future of civilization.

In every human life, there comes a point when the physical body reaches its peak of growth and beings its slow decline. In ancient times, the soul and spirit remained dependent on the physical body for their development far into old age. A person in his fifties or sixties could still undergo significant inner transformation simply through the natural forces of the body. As the physical body declined, the soul blossomed. The patriarchs of old were not merely old in years; they were old in wisdom precisely because the body’s decline brought forth spiritual fruit. But this process has been shifting. Across the post-Atlantean [cultural] epochs, the age at which the soul becomes independent of the body has been steadily decreasing. It makes sense to think of it this way: spirit is becoming stronger and less dependent on the enabling conditions which provided its freedom. These conditions are being sublimated as spirit develops.

At the start of the Greco-Latin age, a person could still develop naturally into his mid-thirties. However by the time of the Mystery of Golgotha, humanity’s “age” had receded to approximately 33 years. Christ Jesus lived to the age of 33, meeting humanity at the very point where its natural development was faltering. Without the Christ Impulse, man would have been unable to access the spiritual world after this age threshold passed. Today, in the fifth post-Atlantean age (the age of the consciousness soul), natural development carries the soul only until approximately the 27th year. After that, the physical body continues to age, but the soul and spirit do not naturally develop further. Key word on naturally. They do not develop as a result of mere being-there. A person may live to be eighty, but unless he takes conscious initiative, his inner being remains at the age of 27.

There is a spiritual-physical basis for this. As the physical body grows older, the etheric body (the body of formative forces) does the opposite. It is old at birth and grows progressively younger throughout life. In ancient times, the declining physical body naturally released the etheric body, allowing spiritual experiences to arise in old age. In modern day, this release no longer happens automatically, meaning the etheric body still grows younger, but its youthful forces remain bound unless consciously cultivated. This is why a person can have gray hair and wrinkles, yet possess a soul that is becoming more flexible and more creative, more “young”. Spiritual science itself acts as a kind of rejuvenating force. One can study it at any age and find that it continually offers new insights, keeping the soul plastic and receptive.

So what are the implications of modern humanity? Firstly it is the end of natural wisdom. In older times, old age brought wisdom automatically. Today, wisdom must be actively sought out. One cannot simply rely on growing old to become wise. One needs to take inner development in hand. Another thing to consider is that since natural development ceases in the late twenties, young people often feel prematurely “finished”. The demand to lower the age of majority, to thrust the young into public life before they have truly matured, reflects this shift. If left unchecked, this tendency could lead to a society governed by the views of youth alone, without the counterbalance of elder wisdom. Although in America we are kind of stuck behind a rock and a hard place: geriatric Israeli worshiping pedophiles, or gen-z/gen-alpha brain rot. Another thing to keep an eye out for is the danger of spiritual stagnation. A person who does not consciously develop after the age of 27 carries the same inner configuration for the rest of life. Steiner warns that if humanity does not take up spiritual impulses, the consequence will be a form of spiritual schizophrenia. There is a disconnect between the physical age of the body, and the arrested development of the soul. Previously, my diagnosis for the problem of “man-children” was much more societal, that we had no “coming of age” ceremonies in the US. However, the problem is much deeper. Steiner gives us a couple of personalities as an example. Woodrow Wilson, although old on the outside, remind inwardly at the age of 27. Lloyd George also exhibited the same trait. He was brilliant and effective, but operating entirely within the horizon of what can be called the “27-year-old soul”. Conversely, people like Goethe or Schelling retained the capacity for inner transformation into later life because they drew from spiritual wellsprings. Goethe’s concept of metamorphosis and his continual self-revision highlight the consciously developed soul that grows younger, even as the body ages.

Ultimately, an emphasis on the necessity of conscious self-cultivation should be promoted. The waning of natural development is a challenge, one that makes sense as a stage of development, forcing man to take freedom seriously. What was once given by nature must now be won over through conscious effort. This serves as another reason why spiritual science is a necessity for the future, providing the means to continue developing the soul after natural development has ceased. If this trend continues, the age of natural development will continue to recede. In the sixth cultural age, it will fall to between 14 and 21 years. In the seventh millennium, it will drop to the 14th year. At that point, physical humanity will no longer be able to develop. Women will cease to bear children. The physical human race as we know it will come to an end. Supposedly. The main thing to keep in mind is that the “younger” humanity becomes physically, the more it must mature spiritually through its own free will.
