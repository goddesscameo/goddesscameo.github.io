Hamann and the Non-Euclidean Revolution of Faith

A confession must be made…

21 minute read

Non-Euclidean Revolution

In a recent unfruitful conversation with Hegelians, I was made to defend the honor of non-Euclidean geometry, despite not being all that invested in the enterprise. The conditions of the argument sought to clarify the character of Non-Euclidean geometry, such that its very possibility was to be established as a whole. The proving grounds for such a position has to come from the conceptual determinations of space. Reason being that geometry is what is known as a science of the Understanding, it is a science with its chief concern being formal validity and symbolic coherence, namely, an avoidance of formal contradiction. No more in geometry does the Euclidean serve as proof that space is Euclidean than Non-Euclidean does. All it does is prove that geometry does not come from observing physical space; it shows that geometry originates in pure thinking rather than empirical observation. Therefore, geometry as a domain exists at a level abstracted beyond the determinations of space. Mathematical systems as a whole are products of the structuring activity of thought, the mind can generate multiple internally consistent “spatial” logics, and so it can be said that mathematics creates conceptual structures by which we engage with (as do all worldviews; see: Mathematism). “Science” then applies these structures to nature, and thus we have the judgment that mathematics models nature, but does not necessarily disclose its essence. It can be said that mathematics is thought grasping pure form independent of sensory experience. Truly, math alone does not penetrate deeper into the spiritual reality of the world, although we will return to this later.

It is evident then, that the possibility of a Non-Euclidean space lies within the determinations of space, which geometry takes as its supposition. Here is where the argument has taken place. Ultimately, the claim I was presented with was that inherent in the immanent development of space was Euclid’s five, yes you heard that right, five postulates, in the form of the claim that dimensions imply orthogonality. Now, I tend to be open to how meaning is disclosed to me. However, the only way in which orthogonality can be bound with dimensionality such that it would prove Euclid’s 5th postulate, is if the argument collapses the distinction between opposed as distinct, or dependent, into orthogonal, meaning at right angles. Orthogonality requires a metric, dimensions are tangent vectors to one another.

The argument leaps from the claim that dimensions are distinct, working at the manifold level, to “therefore perpendiculars exist globally”, working at the metric level. This very assumption is what needs proving, and the reason Euclid could not draw out the fifth postulate from the first four. For Hegel with respect to space, one begins with an immediate and indifferent self-externality. The only interruption of this self-externality can be ideal negations of it. For the purposes of brevity, we may consider point, although it is possible to begin with plane. Points of space are ideal insofar as their negation of extension cannot rest upon any thing in space, because this would be taking the space of that thing for granted. Each point is indifferent and indistinguishable to another, thereby uniting each indifferently with the next. The negative content of both moments is expelled, generating line. Because line is just as indifferent as space, space follows the same pattern from point and line and generates plane. In the words of Richard Dien Winfield, “space’s internal differentiation into point and line entails planes, whose own analogous similitude fills the continuity of space with the unbounded extension of spacial dimensions, which are equally indistinguishable from one another. It makes little difference whether height, length, and breadth be construed in Euclidean space or in a non-Euclidean space, whose use of curvilinear axes already presupposes the two and three-dimensionality defined by straight lines. In each case, what a dimension is identified as is a matter of indifference, since every one can substitute for the others without altering its internal or external relations.” Nothing in the manifold being of space, be it the indifferent distinctions and coexistence of dimensionality, or the for itself indifference of time (it’s ideal logical commencement), implies that a construction of the dimensions involves more than linear independence, not orthogonality. Any more would be an overreach. In my position, the conditions under which one would concede the opposing side would be a proof of the logical necessity of the fifth postulate within the determinations of space. However, as it stands, the conflation between a conceptual opposition, orthogonality, and the parallel postulate is never justified, only asserted. Even Hegel would not make such a bold claim, given that he was highly critical of everything. It also has to be said that in the domain of geometry, the proof has been all but been successful, particularly with Gödel’s incompleteness theorem problematizing, in part, first order axioms and formalist systems, and the explorations of Riemann, Bolyai, and Lobachevsky establish geometries which do not have the fifth postulate holding. Additionally, a position which holds that space as a whole is Euclidean, is a presumptuous position stating that all the work done which is non-Euclidean is either some fluke, intentional misdirection, or worthless. In any case, there is a very prideful and overreaching Moloch behind this claim.

Consequently, the distinction should be made, and has been made by Lawrence S Stepelevich, between what is known as “pure” geometry, and “physical” geometry. Hegel recognized this very distinction, comprehending “what the logic of the understanding has sundered into two exclusive geometries, one developed from an abstract logical content, the other deriving its content from the concrete contingencies of sensuous intuition”. Stepelevich argues that Hegel’s theory of geometry provides speculative support for both Euclidean and non-Euclidean geometry. His critique of Euclid’s congruency postulate in the Science of Logic organizes the distinction:

Thus Euclid’s first propositions about triangles deal only with congruence, that is, how many parts in a triangle must be determined, in order that the remaining parts of one and the same triangle, or the whole of it, shall be altogether determined. The comparison of two triangles with one another, and the basing of congruence on coincidence is a detour necessary to a method that is forced to employ sensuous coincidence instead of the thought, namely, the determinateness of the triangles.

There is a geometry (that, remember, is a level abstracted away from the determinations of space!) which involves the relation between the certainty of the understanding travelling through sensuousness. There is also a geometry which remains in the pure thought of the understanding. The former Stepelevich calls “physical” geometry, and the latter “pure” geometry. What the geometry of the pure understanding provides us with is the fact that the naturality of tridimensional space is taken to hold only for concrete apprehension, for image-thought, and not for the abstract reflections of pure geometry, nor for the determinations of space. An objection might occur here that one expects Hegel to celebrate the intuited three dimensions of space found in “physical geometry” as proof of his method, however, in actuality he rejects this, arguing that geometry is always a level abstracted away from the determinations of space, and, as a science, three-dimensionality itself is not derived from the concept (read: conceptual determinations) of space.

In so far as it is not a philosophical science, geometry may assume the universal determinations of space as its object, and it is not to be demanded of it that it should deduce the necessity of the three dimensions of space.

The differences we perceive between weight, length, and width, are themselves superficial and empty. We cannot define how they differ qualitatively because, at the level of pure thought, they are not yet actual and actualized differences, they are merely supposed to be different. From here, we may return to the establishment of a geometry with this in mind. Pure geometry is the science of abstract conceptual determinations. It deals with the “pure point” (akin to Hilbert’s formal points) which is ens rationis- an entity of reason. The point is once again the negation of space, that in its abstractness is not yet in intuitive space. This pure geometry then continues to describe the internal, necessary articulation of the concept of space as concept. Physical geometry is geometry as it relates to the natural world, to “actual physical mass”. The physical point is a here, a place. The point enters the realm of externality and is thus the “natural concretization” of the pure point. The dimensions (height, length, breadth) appear as the expression of physical mass occupying space. It is not the case that one is true, and the other is false. The culmination of the method in the enclosing surface and the restoration of the spatial totality corresponds to the space of physical bodies. This is a space that has returned to itself, a self-contained whole. This is also the space of “congruence” geometries (such as Euclidean, Spherical, Hyperbolic). These geometries, as Felix Klein systematized them, are concerned with spaces that are homogenous and have properties (such as invariance under translation and rotation), that make them suitable for describing a totality, a world-sphere in which figures can be moved without distortion. The pure mathematics are reflective of a geometry which takes the stages of point, line, and plane as pure moments, representing a space which has not yet achieved totality. They are abstract, one-sided determinations. The “pure point” is not yet in this space or that space. It is the abstract negation that could, in principle, generate any kind of spatial manifold. Both geometries are describing spatiality. Both are, in a sense, necessary. The error would be to mistake the science generated from the abstract moment as being inferior or superior to the science generated from the concrete moment.

Eva Brann’s monumental book, The World of the Imagination: Sum and Substance, offers a historical look at the domain of Euclidean space through an overview of imaginational reality. She inadvertently proves precisely what Hegel claims, that intuitive three-dimensionality is the form of apprehension, and not the content of the concept. Brann merely states it in the form of Euclidean geometry being the imagination’s “native idiom”, yet that theoretical cognition can and does transcend it. Both thinkers agree that Euclidean geometry is how we naturally perceive and imagine space, but it is not a logical necessity and does not exhaust the possibilities of spatiality, thought, and nature. The denial of the possibility of non-Euclidean geometry results from non-speculative thought; such a denial involves the philosophical failure to distinguish between intuition, or immediate imagination, and the concept’s free self-determination. When one asserts that Euclidean space is the only possible geometry, one is unconsciously elevating a report on the imagination’s structure into a limitation on being itself. This is, in essence, a Kantian position, albeit one Kant himself might not have fully endorsed, and one that Hegel’s speculative method definitively overcomes. Euler circles, Venn diagrams, Galilean parabolas, the armillary sphere of the Timaeus, all these are the imagination at work, rendering intelligible structures in intuitively accessible form.

I would like to return to the idea that I am not all invested in the enterprise of non-Euclidean geometry. However, truth-seeker that I am, it is hard to let statements which are blatantly false or mistaken stand, when appropriate to correct them. What I actually believe has very high overlap with Steiner— different geometries correspond to different modes of cognition/perceptions of space. Geometries all describe spatiality by very character of what they are. Geometry provides the quantitative structure of objects furnished from the Understanding’s position as the begetter of thought. As such, Euclidean geometry describes the space we perceive through the senses, it corresponds to waking sensory consciousness, being very accurate for the dead objects of the world; a world of fixed distances, rigid shapes, and so on and so forth. With mathematical thinking, in a geometric proof, Steiner states that “what is present in the mathematical as a conscious fact right up to the lines of reasoning is manageable and accompanied by inner experience from beginning to end”. This is the source of its certainty. The mathematical object is an image which geometry creates, a “pictorial” reality, and in relation to external nature, it is “unreality”. This is also to say that geometry and the determinations of space can never be wholly commensurate. The pure geometry of the understanding is precisely this realm of pictorial unreality. It is thought grasping pure form. So the Euclidean axioms, when modified by a Bolyai or ignored by a Lobachevsky, generate other geometries, they are generating other pictorial worlds from thought. This act of modification is the factual proof of the certainty one feels in the comprehensibility of mathematization. The mind can dwell in these different pictorial spaces with equal rigor and inner consistency.

If Euclidean geometry corresponds to our waking sensory consciousness, being the language for the physical world of mineral bodies, of forces that push and pull centrifugally, it is the geometry of the dead. For this domain, it is perfectly true. However, a “geometry of the living” is necessary. I propose projective geometry. The objects of projective geometry are not abstract objects, or dead objects. Its objects and mathematics describes the formative forces in the world; it is a geometry of process. Steiner himself pointed to the path from analytical to synthetic geometry as a path that leads out of the purely mathematical and towards an encounter with a differentiated, qualitative reality. As he argues, when you follow the implications of projective constructions, you are “forced out of the free formation of the mathematical and into something where the objective passes over into an inner determination, into an inner being”. You are, in essence, beginning to think into the living thing. It is for this reason that I do not put much stock into Euclidean or non-Euclidean geometry. Both have their place and purpose. I much prefer projective geometry for what it can help us visualize and intuit about the world. While more traditional exhibitions of projective geometry exist, such as Lawrence Edwards in The Vortex of Life: Nature’s Patterns in Space and Time, who creates computer generated models of plants, embryos, and organs, like the heart, Annie Besant and Charles Leadbeater’s Thought Forms offers the perfect opportunity for a spiritual-scientific geometry. In the book, they provide numerous illustrations and a clear typology of different forms. If Steiner is right that the respective geometry reflects different modes of cognition/perception of space, then projective geometry, with its focus on mobile, metamorphosing forms (like the conic sections found in Thought Forms), provides the mathematical language for these forces. A growing plant can be understood for the spiritual scientist as a metamorphosis of form that can be modeled by the continuous transformation of a projective curve. Additionally, Steiner’s “intuitive thinking” can help us comprehend the geometry of thought-forms as the visible trace of the etheric formative forces having been impressed upon by a soul quality. The qualitative, inner experience, and the living world of formative, spatial dynamics may provide us with a geometric phenomenon of the soul.

Any further arguments on the intrinsic character of space as “Euclidean” will be met by me with a demand to read Point and Line and Plane by Kandinsky. It is with a great disheartening mood that I must confess: arguments like these are not all that atypical for me when talking with Hegelians. In trying to convey meaning, one will be met with incredulity regarding structures and actualities outside the dogmatic form of the concept. The very argument mentioned has ceded ground to the dogma of the concept- I have not been a Hegelian for a while- and yet in all my efforts to explain why, using analogies and alternative ways of approach, the very approach of analogies and alternatives was the issue. Unless one already has doubts, which are the seeds of escape from the solipsism of the concept, communication will be rendered impossible with the interlocutor. Both thinking and being are restricted, disabling one from accepting that ultimately, we are all analogical. Everything we do and think is in an analogical relationship to the ultimate goodness, of Christ. Rejecting this reality of being and holding onto false Gods is nothing short of fanaticism. The possibility of self-criticism surely always needs to be present, and so my own position in these circumstances must be established. Words and ideas leave psychic imprints on me, such that I can almost “feel” the relationality of what is presented to me through thought. Responsibility must be taken in conveying what is “felt” or “seen” in this structure. However, the limit of accountability is the actions done by other people. In communication, if there is an inability to see what is there, you cannot force someone to see it, just as you cannot force a drug addict to see the good life. Likewise, an inability to afford grace in communication, that the other person is trying to communicate something, something more than their own failure of comprehension, denies the inner life present in all of us, through the grace of God.

Hamann’s Faith

A confession must be made. The debates above reveal an archetype of sorts, of a person sealed inside a circle that has no outside, unable to receive what does not fit the predetermined form. What becomes clear in such moments is that argument alone cannot open what has been closed. Only a burgeoning from the inside may begin to crack the hardened shell of systems-thinking formalism. Something supplementary is needed, a therapy that targets the very aspiration to systemic totality which Hegel inherits from the German Idealist tradition and radicalizes. The person to offer such a therapy is the “Magus of the North”, Johann Georg Hamann.

I have spent years myself inside this circle of circles, I know it’s language, it’s satisfactions, I know it’s Euclidean centrality. And I know what it costs to leave, to slowly recognize that the circle, for all its solidarity, cannot contain what God provides. Relationality is felt, but grace arrives unbidden, a confession must be made:

There is no pure reason. Reason is never abstract, and never universal, nor self-standing. It always belongs to a particular planet, a particular zeitgeist, shaped by a particular language, and tradition. The Hegelian system, for example, for all its claims to absolute knowing, remains immensely humanist: it can only ever be a relationality that is intelligible to us, not to a relationality to the object itself. What sense would it make to speak of a relationality we have no relationship with? The very question lends us the answer.

We approach this limit constantly through analogy. The relationship between God and humanity, the divine and the human, is an analogical one, based on the free act of God in the Incarnation. The union of two natures in Christ is a mystery to be believed and adored, rather than something to be reconciled by speculative reason. The relationship is open because the kind of operative protocol the concept undergoes is incapable of getting from the human to the divine. There is a gap that can only be crossed by a gift from the other side. The attempt to expunge reason of its contingency is the proton pseudos of systematicity and the beginning of the neglect of faith. Reason’s purpose is to reveal its own limits. Like the law in Pauline theology, reason is a pedagogue. It leads us to the end of our own resources, exhausting its own resources until what is left is what is. Surely, Hegel’s methodology can be described as a series of self-exhaustions of the system itself. But for Hegel, this exhaustion leads to an ever higher integration, an apotheosis of reason. For Hamann, it leads elsewhere entirely. Reasons self-critique is meant to show us our ignorance and need for something beyond ourselves. When reason claims to be sufficient, it becomes idolatrous. Reason itself is a fallen creature, a fallen human capacity. It is given sensations, given a tradition, given a language, and ultimately given revelation. Its job is to acknowledge this dependence, not to spin everything out of itself. Thus, reason’s ultimate purpose is to lead us to the end of our own resources. It is meant to fail, to show us the abyss. This is the exact opposite of Hegel’s project, where reason’s self-examination leads to its own apotheosis. For Hamann, reason’s examination leads only to its own limit. What lies beyond that limit is not for reason to say.

Perhaps what lies beyond that limit, what is given is language. In the beginning was the Word, after all. Hamann has the view that language is prior to reason. To an Idealist, this would seem like a crude, empirical assertion which the concept would sublate (yes I will use that word). They would almost immediately assert: “Prior? prior in what sense? Chronologically? Empirically? This would not make sense, given it would be anthropological, rather than philosophical. Spirit sublates such empirical conditions into its own self-conscious logical development. The truth of language is the Concept!”.

Hamann is certainly not making a claim about cavemen grunting before being able to think. He is making a humbler claim about the nature of all thought- that language is the inescapable medium of all thought. You cannot get behind it or beyond it to a purer realm of thought, whether that realm is Kant’s transcendental unity of apperception, or Hegel’s pure self-thinking thought. The idea of a thought that has fully sublated its linguistic expression into a pure, self-present meaning is an illusion. Steiner himself has commented on how a spiritual person like Hamann would be quick to jump against the position that meaning is not immanent, or located within some “outside”. He would likely say to Hegel’s Concept, if one were to hold the position that the form is “sublated” (and in some sense contingent to the word, whether it be the rational natural contingency of form, or the formalism of the Understanding which only values symbolic coherence) that for all its claimed concreteness it is just another attempt to vault over the abyss of language’s being, which is itself being.

Hamann often uses the term “hysteron proteron” to accuse both Kant, and by extension, any idealist system of getting things backwards. Such a position would treat the a priori (the logical, conceptual) as if it were the foundation, when in fact it is derived from and always dependent upon the a posteriori. The genealogical priority of language means that what philosophy claims is first (the Concept) is actually “last”. Or as scripture puts it: so the last shall be first, and the first last. The very possibility of an abstract logical determination purifying itself of its linguistic and embodied skin is denied by Hamann. There is no thought before or outside language. There is only thought in language, shaped by it and carried by it. Consider a practical example. What is gender? One approach might attempt a rational determination: gender is logically rooted in the sexed distinction, a clean conceptual definition which admits no variability (although it certainly leaves room for it). This approach, for all its claim to necessity, tells you nothing about what gender actually is! It is indistinguishable from a dogmatic traditionalism that simply asserts the union of gender and sex. The alternative is to attend to how gender appears through particular cultures, how it is lived, and spoken, and named. This second approach does not begin with the concept and apply it to the world. It begins with the world, with language and tradition, and lets the concept emerge from there. The concept, in this case, is not the foundation, but the result. It is last, not first. This is essentially Hamann’s point. Language is not conceived of as the clothing of the thought, it is its body. You cannot strip it away to find the pure concept underneath, because there is nothing underneath. The concept is not behind the word, it is in the word, inseparable from it, given with it. And this is why reason, which thought itself sovereign, must confess its dependence. Reason does not invent language for itself, it is born into language, it relates the word to itself. It does not master language, it is mastered by it. Reason is pedagogue to Christ. The ultimate purpose of reason and its self-critique is to lead us to the end of our own resources. It is meant to fail, to show us the “abyss,” so that we might stop trusting in it and instead trust in the Word that is given to us. This is the exact opposite of Hegel’s project, where reason’s self-examination leads to its own apotheosis, what Steiner calls logical fetishism (for more reasons than this).

There are some relations you have to take on faith, relations which reason itself cannot immediately show you. For example, it is inconceivable to see karmic links between people within a framework like Kant’s or Hegel’s. The Incarnation is another example. In Christ, divine and human natures unite without fusion, without one side being reduced to the other. You cannot arrive at the self-relationality of Christ by reasoning, because this system of relationality is not exhaustive of Christ’s self-relationality, it is only exhaustive of the intelligibility reason has. This is why faith is not a substitute for reason, nor a moment to be subsumed within reason’s development. Faith is the mode of relating to what exceeds us- to the God who gives himself, to the Word that speaks to us, and to the relations that hold us together, the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit. Faith is the trust that the gift is good, and that the giver is faithful. In the future this mode of relation will develop itself into a new faculty. Nevertheless, this will be for a future time, brought along by a revolution as paradigm shifting as non-Euclidean geometry was for the Kantians.
