Post-Scripts to the Racialism in Anthroposophy

Against 'Steinerism'

13 minute read

Previously I’ve written on anthroposophy, philosophy, and race. I made an error insofar as I did not treat the accusation of racism in Rudolf Steiner’s anthroposophy seriously enough, instead opting for a formulation of the structure and telos of race that I thought was correct, albeit one that was fully compatible with the framework of anthroposophy as proposed by Steiner. I did not highlight the inflammatory statements made by Steiner, nor did I feel it ever made sense given his other statements on the dissolution of race in the future. However, it is evident that Steiner thought the “white race” was the best, or at the very least held a patronizing racism where members of the “white race” have the responsibility of guiding the others. There is a glaring contradiction with Steiner wherein there are beautiful universalist statements that foretell that the emergence of the Christ impulse during the Mystery of Golgotha unites all humanity beyond race, blood, and outer appearance. At the same time, statements on the mission of the white race, dark skin as a block to the Christ impulse (particularly in the context of America where the Black American community are stronger bearers of the Christ impulse in modern day than “White” Americans), and the moral/evolutionary decadence of non-white races demonstrate that at the very least, Steiner was struggling with this tension if not completely wrong and shortsighted in this regard. Even absent hatred, Steiner’s paternalistic framing of racial hierarchy and mission constitutes a form of racism that is incompatible with spiritual progress. It also goes to show that any future “Spiritual Science” should not be contingent on one individual, and should include the participation of all participants in order for it to be a living tradition and practice. Likewise, my goal here is to disentangle a valuable spiritual-scientific impulse from the uncritical dogmatism of “Steinerism” and from Rudolf Steiner’s own incoherent racial statements by providing various thoughts on the matter.

To move beyond a simple catalogue of offensive quotes (of which you can find here), we must examine the underlying structures of Rudolf Steiner’s racial thought in his system of anthroposophy. When subjected to scrutiny, these structures reveal profound contradictions, both within Anthroposophy’s own metaphysical framework and against the record of history. This internal critique is necessary to separate the system’s enduring insights from its untrue statements. For example, a central pillar of anthroposophy is the concept of evolving consciousness, mirrored in the evolution of spiritual beings themselves. The hierarchies are guides whose relationship to humanity changes. Their “goal” as Steiner often implied, is to foster human independence, to render their own tutelage obsolete. Forms of “authority” are transitional and initiatory, preparing the ground for a future of free, conscious collaboration between humanity and the spiritual world, and subsequent recognition of humanity as being constituted, at the very least in part by, the spiritual world.

Steiner’s racial taxonomy contradicts this core principle. He presents 5 races as a rigid hierarchical ladder of spiritual-moral evolution, with the White race as its contemporary apex. This model is fundamentally static in its ranking. While souls are said to incarnate through different races karmically, the racial categories themselves bear an immutable value judgment where “White” equals advanced, and “Black” equals backward. What absurdity! A cosmos where divine beings and human consciousness are in constant purposeful evolution, but the vessel of that consciousness (racial humanity) is locked in a fixed caste system. If the spiritual world is a teacher that seeks its own obsolescence, why is the racial classroom eternally graded? In no way can this stand for spiritual science, it sounds more like the projection of 19th century colonial anthropology onto the cosmos, putting temporal prejudices into hypostasis as a spiritual law, and subsequently betraying anthroposophy’s insights into being.

Steiner’s entire edifice of racial mission depends on the coherence of the “White race” (he never actually defines race, staying vague) as a historical and spiritual actor. This construct collapses under historical examination. What we call “European” consciousness - the very cradle of the Intellectual/Consciousness Soul - was never a self-contained sui generis phenomenon. It is the product of continuous cross-pollination. Consider the foundational elements. Greek philosophy was undoubtedly shaped by exchanges with Egyptian and Persian thought. The Renaissance, Europe’s “great awakening”, was fueled by the recovery of classical texts preserved and elaborated upon by Arab and Islamic scholars. Even Steiner could not contest this. The very concept of Christ entered the European sphere through Jewish and Near Eastern traditions, later intertwining with Greco-Roman philosophy. To speak of a “white race mission” is to perform an act of historical revisionism and erasure, claiming the collective product of Mediterranean, African, and Asian intellect as the exclusive inheritance of a fabricated racial category. The “mission” is exposed as a narrative made to justify contemporary power dynamics and narratives, spiritualizing a colonial worldview by assigning it an incoherent cosmic purpose.

This historical fallacy leads to a theological contradiction. Steiner rightfully speaks of the Christ Event as a pivotal turning point in Earth’s evolution, an impulse of love that entered the stream of humanity as a whole (via Christ leaving his mark on the etheric body of the Earth and so on). He states clearly that the Christ impulse is available to all, irrespective of race or creed. Yet, buy tethering the maturation of this impulse to the specific “mission” of the “white race”, he effectively makes Christ the property of the West. This ignores the fact that ancient Christianities took root and flourished entirely outside the European sphere, often before many “white” nations were even converted. The Coptic Church in Egypt, the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church (which traces its lineage to the 1st century, before Constantine adopted Christianity for Rome), the Church of the East which spread to India and China, all bear witness to a Christ who speaks to, and is incarnated within, every human experience. To suggest that these traditions are merely waiting for, or require impregnation by, a European spiritual consciousness is not only a racial insult, but a Christological heresy. It replaces the universal, unifying Logos with some tribal deity serving a specific racial-geographic group which has yet to be precisely delineated.

Perhaps the most telling contradiction lies in Anthroposophy’s explanation for race itself. Steiner describes racial differentiation as a consequence of the Luciferic and Ahrimanic influences. The racial condition is a fall away from an original, more unified, human state. In this view, racial characteristics are akin to spiritual scars or adaptive shells, results of a cosmic accident and descent into materiality. Yet in countless lectures, Steiner abandons this neutral diagnostic tone and assigns moral and evolutionary value to these very conditions. The “Mongols” are “decadent Atlanteans”; the “Negro race” is at a child-like stage; “white” skin allows fuller access to the Christ impulse. This leap from description to hierarchy is logically and ethically unjustified and unsound. It’s the equivalent of saying that a person born with a disability due to a historical accident is inherently less morally or spiritually evolved than others, and practically an example of what one finds in the religion of India. In India the prevalence of the caste system can be, in part, attributed to the belief in their form of karma. Because you had sinned in a past life, your destiny in this life is to be a member of the Dalit (untouchable) class and to be treated accordingly. This in effect functions as a legitimating authority for status quo and domination of lower classes, transforming a shared condition of fallen humanity into a system of spiritual apartheid. The Gospel of John provides the antidote to this kind of prejudice:

“And as Jesus passed by, he saw a man which was blind from his birth. And his disciples asked him, saying, Master, who did sin, this man, or his parents, that he was born blind? Jesus answered, Neither hath this man sinned, nor his parents: but that the works of God should be made manifest in him.”
—John 9:1-3

What this passage conveys is that outer conditions are not verdicts on the inner self. They are conditions for the revelation of Spirit, arenas for the self to find itself. Such racist and prejudiced statements forget this foundational Christian truth. What we have before us are foundational errors in the intelligibility of Steiner’s racialism.

Let us take for granted that this group of people called “white” have a mission which is defined as the spiritual subjugation, education, or impregnation of all other peoples. Then Rudolf Steiner is not describing a Christian, Christ-driven impulse. He is articulating a blueprint for spiritual colonialism. Such a doctrine of inherent superiority and divinely mandated dominion does not serve the unifying, love-filled Christ impulse he elsewhere championed. Instead, it perfectly serves the Ahrimanic forces. Ahriman, in Steiner’s own cosmology, seeks to harden, materialize, and divide consciousness into rigid hierarchies and external forms of power. A racial “mission” to dominate global spiritual evolution is definitely not Christian. It is the “Black Sun” of a cosmic racism, an anti-Christian force parading itself around as divinely decreed. What great a gift to Ahriman! An Ahrimanic perversion of spirituality into a tool for hierarchical control, making a gift of grace into a possession of power, what more can be asked?

If we are to categorize white supremacy accurately, we must recognize it not as a doctrine of excellence, but as the institutionalization of white mediocrity. It is the claim to inherent spiritual, intellectual, or cultural authority based solely on the biological accident of pigmentation. This claim circumvents the need for demonstrable effort or mastery, making a temporary collective condition an individual accomplishment. Does being born a woman inherently bestow the skills of motherhood? Of course not! True motherhood, as any true mastery, is a proving ground that must be validated through action and result. In this light, Steiner’s framework of racial missions and hierarchies tands exposed. By assigning cosmic purpose and superior creative capacity to the “white race” by virtue of its mere existence, he is spiritually legitimizing the White Man’s Burden. He transposes a colonial-political ideology onto the spiritual work, revealing a failure to transcend the most pernicious and unearned prejudice of his era. It’s quite shameful that he dressed up the mundane arrogance of imperialism in the language of cosmic destiny.

The defense of Steiner’s racial theories often rests on a single claim: that these are not opinions, but objective facts derived from direct spiritual perception as a “reading of the Akashic Record”. Most anthroposophists, when confronted with these claims, will try to talk their way out of it, rather than defending the claim. However, for the purposes of this writing piece, I will treat the response as defense against what it clearly claims. This defense places criticism beyond the realm of ordinary debate and into the realm of spiritual authority. It is precisely here, at the heart of methodology that the most necessary work must occur. To declare Steiner’s clairvoyant findings unimpeachable is to enact a contradiction within a system ostensibly dedicated to human spiritual self-determination. Anthroposophy proposes that the development of conscious, independent thought (Imagination), feeling (Inspiration), and will (Intuition) is the evolutionary goal of the modern “I”. However, the demand to accept Steiner’s pronouncements without critical experiential verification is an anti-initiation. It asks the student to surrender the faculties of discernment, personal validation, and questioning, that spiritual development is meant to awaken. The greatest obstacle to Anthroposophy’s future is internal idolatry that transforms a method of knowing into a catalog of dogma, sinning against the individual spiritual capacities it claims to cultivate.

The principle of spiritual science asserts that clairvoyant perception, like any observation, must be trained to be objective. But no consciousness, however heightened, exists in a historical or cultural vacuum. Steiner’s mind was unmistakably that of a late 19th century Central European intellectual. When turning his gaze to the Akashic Record of human evolution, to what extent did this record pass through the filter of his own preconditioning? It is evident that the truth ran into a contradiction he never ended up reconciling. His specific assertions about race provide the answer. The notion that melanin physically blocks the “Christ impulse”, that blond hair correlates with intellectual vigor, or that “Negro novels” could cause racial degeneration, in no way are these timeless spiritual truths. They are artifacts of a contingent and specific historical moment, echoing then-prevalent pseudoscientific theories of racial biology and decadence. They bear the unmistakable stamp of a European gaze interpreting spiritual realities through a lens of racial hierarchy. A true spiritual methodology would require us to consider that while Steiner’s faculty of perception may have been advanced, the interpretation of what he perceived could be, and in many these cases clearly was, distorted by the unexamined prejudices of his earthly persona.

The most potent empirical refutation of Steiner’s racial framework comes from the exceptions Steiner himself notes. He occasionally noted individuals from so-called “backward” races who displayed advanced spiritual or intellectual qualities, often explaining them as “old souls” from more advanced races incarnating elsewhere. That’s quite the stretch, isn’t it? If the racial container is so spiritually determinant, such exceptions should be impossible. Their very existence demonstrates the opposite fact, that the sovereign individual “I”, with its unique karmic trajectory and spiritual agency, can and does utterly transcend any generalized “racial” characteristic.

All in all, we must rigorously distinguish between Anthroposophy, which we may define as the broad field of spiritual-scientific inquiry into the human being and the cosmos, and Steinerism, the uncritical veneration of Rudolf Steiner as an exclusive and final authority. In the hard sciences, one studies physics, not “Newtonism” or “Einsteinism” (if one can forget the fact that they were wrong on many points). These founders provided powerful tools and models, but models meant to be tested, expanded, corrected, verified, and in some cases, superseded. Likewise, a living spiritual science requires the same ethos. To freeze the work in its early 20th century form, defending every statement as holy writ, is to kill the very spirit of inquiry that gave it life in the first place.

In no way is this a dismissal of the entire system of anthroposophy. It is actually a necessary precondition for any truly novel advance of it. A reworked understanding concedes that these diverse forms serve, and in their fading way, still serve a pedagogical function for the human spirit. They can be seen as collective indications, particularized schools of experience through which specific soul qualities could be intensified and brought to global consciousness. The purpose of the “races” is to provide a diverse schooling with their telos having their garnered wisdom absorbed, overcome, and integrated by the free and individual “I”. The goal is a humanity that carries the harvested fruits of all its journeys, no longer confined to the immediacy from which it began.

Thus, we can accept that differences have existed for a reason, without making that reason some chain of determinacy. We may be able to study the morphology of human experience without enshrining it as a static destiny forever. The future spiritual science must honor this past development, while detailing its future as a conscious dissolution of differentiation into a new unity in the form of a free, shared humanity.
