What is Natural?

Musings on nature.

35 minute read

To speak truly, few adult persons can see nature. Most persons do not see the sun. At least they have a very superficial seeing. The sun illuminates only the eye of the man, but shines into the eye and the heart of the child. The lover of nature is he whose inward and outward senses are still truly adjusted to each other; who has retained the spirit of infancy even into the era of manhood. His intercourse with heaven and earth, becomes part of his daily food. In the presence of nature, a wild delight runs through the man, in spite of real sorrows. Nature says,—he is my creature, and maugre all his impertinent griefs, he shall be glad with me. Not the sun or the summer alone, but every hour and season yields its tribute of delight; for every hour and change corresponds to and authorizes a different state of the mind, from breathless noon to grimmest midnight. Nature is a setting that fits equally well a comic or a mourning piece. In good health, the air is a cordial of incredible virtue. Crossing a bare common, in snow puddles, at twilight, under a clouded sky, without having in my thoughts any occurrence of special good fortune, I have enjoyed a perfect exhilaration. I am glad to the brink of fear. In the woods too, a man casts off his years, as the snake his slough, and at what period soever of life, is always a child. In the woods, is perpetual youth. Within these plantations of God, a decorum and sanctity reign, a perennial festival is dressed, and the guest sees not how he should tire of them in a thousand years. In the woods, we return to reason and faith. There I feel that nothing can befall me in life,—no disgrace, no calamity, (leaving me my eyes,) which nature cannot repair. Standing on the bare ground,—my head bathed by the blithe air, and uplifted into infinite space,—all mean egotism vanishes. I become a transparent eye-ball; I am nothing; I see all; the currents of the Universal Being circulate through me; I am part or particle of God. The name of the nearest friend sounds then foreign and accidental: to be brothers, to be acquaintances,—master or servant, is then a trifle and a disturbance. I am the lover of uncontained and immortal beauty. In the wilderness, I find something more dear and connate than in streets or villages. In the tranquil landscape, and especially in the distant line of the horizon, man beholds somewhat as beautiful as his own nature.

The greatest delight which the fields and woods minister, is the suggestion of an occult relation between man and the vegetable. I am not alone and unacknowledged. They nod to me, and I to them. The waving of the boughs in the storm, is new to me and old. It takes me by surprise, and yet is not unknown. Its effect is like that of a higher thought or a better emotion coming over me, when I deemed I was thinking justly or doing right. Yet it is certain that the power to produce this delight, does not reside in nature, but in man, or in a harmony of both. It is necessary to use these pleasures with great temperance. For, nature is not always tricked in holiday attire, but the same scene which yesterday breathed perfume and glittered as for the frolic of the nymphs, is overspread with melancholy today. Nature always wears the colors of the spirit. To a man laboring under calamity, the heat of his own fire hath sadness in it. Then, there is a kind of contempt of the landscape felt by him who has just lost by death a dear friend. The sky is less grand as it shuts down over less worth in the population.

—Nature, Ralph Waldo Emerson

Nature Is An Imposition On Man

Hydra poison and angel tears, sifting through the veins of a lonely man. Nature, red in tooth and claw gnaws at the independent will, placing an obstacle before the suffrage due to him. Self-determination, the ability to act from a possessed principle instead of being pushed from the outside, requires an outside to push upon it. Nature is this imposition, a cold, material, temporal, and indifferent, yet highly beautiful constraint, which is essential for the emergence of genuine freedom. Without this imposition, self-determination would be a mere dream to be played in and with, in the face of any real accomplishment. Externality commences the first overcoming of freedom by establishing a context for organization- a rock lies where it lies and not where I will it, the river flows where it flows and not where I’d prefer it. The immediate being of nature confronts freedom with the possibility of alternation; in the quest for self-determination, things I did not determine exist. My body is in nature, and I do not immediately will it how I please. It imposes upon me hunger, fatigue, illness, and death. No act of freedom cancels the need to breathe or the fact of aging. Consequently, nature operates by blind and lawful regularity. A stone does not remain suspended in the air simply because I wish it. I must either work with or against gravity to have my will be done.

Consider all that imposes. Social custom claps a yoke upon the will, logic constrains our thinking, and our own habits and self-discipline limits from within. If Nature were but another such constable, another adder to the list of our limiters, her philosophical interest would be a thin and meager thing. The imposition of Nature is not like these at all. No, no. There is a separation all its own that sets it as far from social rule or logical bound as wide as the Grand Canyon. To know Nature, we must quit the shallow question of whether she restricts and swerve into the question of what kind of restriction is hers. The import of this change in questioning will influence whether nature is some alien to overcome, or perhaps a neutral resource to be used. And because the quality of the imposition changes what freedom means in relation to nature and vice versa, this second question is the more important one.

Uniqueness lies in the fact that nature’s imposition is at the same time impersonal and aesthetic. Unlike a tyrant or a social norm, nature does not act from malice or care. Floods wash away livelihoods and histories without hatred, and drought dehydrates all the same. This impersonality means no negotiation is possible; there is no bribery in falling from a great height, as if one can promise something to nature in exchange for arbitrarily halting her rightful law. In all cases, the one with the upper hand is nature. Yet the same impersonal forces often produce such breathtaking beauty in the world. The mountain’s sublimity or the flower’s delicate form, or the snowflakes’ symmetry- the combination of indifferent splendor is found nowhere else in human experience (barre natural disposition in spirit). Another uniqueness is in the fact that nature’s imposition is at the same time restrictive and generative. The very laws of nature that block one aspect are enablers of another as a result of indifference. While gravity may prevent one from floating, it also holds the atmosphere that lets one walk. A species’ morphological type limits variation, but that limit is what makes organized diversity possible. Without it, there is complete chaos and Ahriman’s imagination runs wild. Social and self-imposed rules typically function as negations imposed upon a subject, but nature’s negation is intrinsic to the very conditions that make the subject possible. The former places the onus on the subject to create alternation, and as such, the prohibition of speech, for instance, generates coded language and satire, but this generativity depends upon subjective receptivity and response. Within nature, a displacement of the negation occurs immediately and immanently within the domain of nature itself; the force of gravity’s forbidding of “free” movement, is what fosters the very field within which movement becomes possible in the first place. Nature’s imposition is then understood to be highly material. It has a lawful duality to it, as it works in and through bodies and energies. The constraints within nature leave very real traces that cannot be cancelled by thought alone. Unlike a mistaken belief, which can be corrected by reasoning, nature’s imposition demands action in the world. As such, the thinking of nature has immediate consequences: nature thinks herself into being, and if she is mistaken, then she dies. When the plant requiring sustenance is watered by an acidic substance, that is, ironically, nature’s own produce, then the plants being cannot be debated because its thought has failed. Because of this, nature becomes a great teacher of lawfulness in the world. The child learns to not touch the hot stove either by heeding the warning of adults or by the singing of their new flesh, and a fear of falling from tall heights stems from the impact of broken bones on concrete. Such an imposition is quite reliable.

Nature As Such

Any genuine inquiry into nature must begin with a singular and unwavering rule: look for the reason something happens within that thing itself, not in any outside source. Two common habits of thought are dispensed with in this approach. The first is the habit of saying that a natural object is designed according to a premade plan or telos (for example, saying that an eye is made the way it is because a “designer” wanted the eye to see). The second habit is a reductionism towards mechanism, as if a tree were no different from a pile of rocks simply because common forces of pushing, pulling, collision, and so on work upon them both. Both habits fail because they bring in a law from outside the object, rather than letting the object reveal its own inner lawfulness. What this means concretely is that this principle requires that when one studies a stone or plant, they refuse to ask “what externality determined this”, opting instead for the positionality that seeks what must be true within this thing to make it appear exactly as it does. The investigator’s vocation is to arrange the object or event (in this case nature) so that its own behavior becomes self-explanatory. No picture of the world, no matter how detailed a listing, yields comprehension. Comprehension arrives when the mind creates a fertile concept that makes sense of and coheres a set of percepts. Such a disciplined act of thinking stays entirely within what is given, and in one swift movement lifts the given into a higher ideality. For example, watching a lamp swing back and forth does not by itself teach the law of the pendulum. That law appears only when someone thinks the swinging motion as a relation between length and time, stripping all accidentals like air or imperfect suspension. Uncovering the intelligibility which was always there, but hidden behind the surface constitutes any real advance in knowledge. Otherwise, lacking this vocation results in a heap of unrelated facts.

Nurturing a worldview that carries its own justification is the goal of any true inquiry. It should not need to lean on outside supports such as an external, guiding divine plan, or notions like “common sense”. Explaining each part by its place in the relational whole and that whole is nothing more than an unfolding of its own starting points is what is known as self-sustaining. Without this, knowledge remains arbitrary, and our explanations risk the sin of overstepping their post. For instance if in an explanation of a living being we borrow principles from non-living matter, we have not understood the living being. If an inquiry into nature would embrace something such as a presumed external designer, it will have stepped outside nature altogether. Only when the explanation arises from within the thing itself have we achieved a conclusion that is both true to the object and satisfying to reason. Furthermore, immediate everyday experience is full of disorder and contingency. Things happen because of accidental circumstance: this stone fell here, not there; this leaf grew crooked because a bug bit into it at this spot, and so on and so forth. Raw reality often disappoints our deeper need for comprehension, and so inquiry should not be in accordance with this chaos, it should raise us to a higher kind of perception. In this way, inquiry may give us a world that is known.

Verily, nature is not a machine. A machine is assembled from the outside and follows a designed conceived from elsewhere, having no inner principle of self-organization or self-repair. Nature, by contrast, certainly does not arrive prepackaged from a foreign intelligence onto dead matter. Her laws are the very way she unfolds from within. No doubt, organisms exhibit an admirable ordering. That order need not be traced back to a planner standing outside nature, no reason connects this to reality beyond a convenience, or rather laziness, of thinking. To do so would also be to explain away the living by something quite dead, placing the source of intelligibility beyond the thing we seek to understand. Nature’s order is immanent on the first count.

As understood most comprehensively, nature is best described as the dynamic, self-organizing whole of spatiotemporal existence. Every thing in nature is a temporary expression of underlying powers beyond the natural world, but make their existence known in it. What these powers are is where the thinker plays a part, whether this thinking is done by the nature spirits who think rocks and stones, leaves and trees, and storms and hurricanes into being, or on part of the human subject seeking to connect with the world and broadly think as it does. Being itself characterized by expressing underlying powers, nature is not exempt from its reflexive self-characterization. Nature itself is a living process that conditions and participates in the emergence of spirit. This is the underlying power behind nature, a spirit in its own right which hosts the concretization of spirit in the world. Now, there is a more subtle denial of nature’s freedom that comes from turning nature into a waypoint for spirit. Some philosophies (most famously the grand systems that see all reality as the journey of spirit) treat nature as a foreign land that Spirit must begrudgingly pass through, suffer in, and ultimately abandon. In this view, nature is real only as a moment of self-estrangement. This should be rejected, not on the denial that spirits, angels, or even God having created nature for its pregnancy, this much should be acknowledged. It should be on the fact that creation is always meaningful. A painter who gives a painting its own inner light does not reduce the painting to a footnote of the painter, and likewise a composer who writes a fugue does not make each note a slave to the harmony, let alone the composer’s biography. So too, if nature is created, she may be created as a genuine other, as a real presence and domain with her own kind of truth. The positivism just described only serves to veil what nature herself veils, forbidding us from getting to the truth of the matter.

The Spiritual Intuition of Nature

At some point or another, most people have looked at a forest, a starry night sky, a newborn animal, or even in some cases a natural disaster, and felt something that goes beyond the physical fact of description, passed the sublimity of awe and reverence, and into the intuition that what is being looked at carries a sense of something alive in a way that a pile of bricks or a machine isn’t. Say we watch a spider rebuilding its web after a storm or a wounded animal limp to safety. Even something as “basic” as a seedling pushing through soil is truly anything but basic, and one senses something that purely chemical descriptions miss. The chemistry is not wrong, mechanics and chemistry play a part and are true on their own level. It is the phenomenon that exceeds the sum of these parts, a persistence implying a unity of some kind. Meaning the language of physics and chemistry applies on one level, to the degree that a seed responds to temperature and moisture and gravity, these are not false, but are insufficient. The seedling at the same time does not choose a direction, it grows toward light as if it knows where the sun is, presenting a self-maintenance in the face of the entropic principle. The ability to repair damage and coordinate the sometimes thousands of chemical and mechanical processes simultaneously are signatures of an inner impulse. Often it is said that life wants to continue, as if life itself had a will. In one sense this is a kind of anthropomorphism, in the sense that the same quality of life present in planets, the sun and moon, animals, and plants, is also present in people. However, this thing called “life” cannot truly be called anthropomorphic on this account since it is an honest report of how the phenomenon of life presents itself. There is an inwardness that even a single cell exhibits, a kind of intention without a brain. It cannot be considered a conscious intention, but it is certainly not random either. Goethe would call this the Bildungstrieb, or formative drive; Schelling saw nature’s productivity as the world soul’s first stirring. These names are not necessary, you only need to have held a living thing in your hands and felt that it is doing something. Nature’s intuition is doing something and not just happening. If the natural is alive in any kind of way, it is constituted in part by an excess of chemism and mechanism, which are so often unified as one deterministic explanatory power. In this case, the death of a lifelong family pet or the felling of an old tree feels like the loss of presence because it is. Nihilistic attitudes would accuse subjective feeling of experiencing lament over the loss of a mere function. We can only pray that the same indifference felt toward nature by such individuals is not mirrored by others as a fulfillment of their world picture.

There are places that feel different. One may be in an unremarkable forest in one instant and in the next step into a clearing of some kind and have the quality of air change. Perhaps the light seems older, and the silence becomes deafening. Places like these may be unmarked my human hands but carry a significance more impactful than religion. Truly, an encounter in nature may itself be a religious experience. The intuition here is that the land itself has a kind of character beyond geological oddities. Places that feel chosen somehow, that the Earth has arranged for itself a temple or holy site as integral of its own being. Across cultures, such places become sites of pilgrimage and ritual because people cannot ignore the feeling. “Ley lines” are a more structured attempt to name the various church sites, pyramids, alignments of ancient standing stones, wells, and natural features that seem to follow energy pathways invisible to the naked eye. Whether one believes in their literal existence or not, the underlying experience is real. Some paths across the land feel alive, as if you are walking along a current. Farmers speak of earth energies that affect plant growth and animal behavior. Even so-called skeptics admit that some places are uncannily calming or unsettling, although mechanical and chemical explanations will furnish their justifications in feeling.

The world itself undergoes repetition with difference. Spring brings with it a kind of return through the story of emergence and the break of the dormancy of winter. Autumn is a release of beauty and mourn. The tides breathe alongside the moon, they do not just blindly repeat the motion of water. Each phase transforms into the next while preserving something essential, and by this very fact alone, cycles not only feel but are larger than their physical causes. What Goethe calls the Urphänomen (primal phenomenon) tries to capture this sensory experience of a law that governs the entire domain of nature. Most famously, an example can be found in the metamorphosis of the plant. Looking at countless plants, he saw that all their parts (the petals, fruit, stamens, sepals, etc.) are in reality the same organ transformed. That organ is the leaf. This leaf expands, contracts, refines, and specializes. The cotyledon may be considered a leaf with stored food, and the stem leaf a leaf that breathes. The petal is a leaf having become delicate and bright, the stamen a leaf coiled to hold pollen, and the fruit a leaf swollen around seeds. The whole life of the land is the leaf’s journey thrush a series of gesturing. Such an archetype is present and visible right now in every [healthy] plant. All it takes to see it is training. Once one does, the plant ceases in being a collection of parts and transforms into a performance of a single theme, found in the visible idea of the plant’s growth cycle. Goethe called this the Urpflanze (primal plant), claiming that with it, we can speak a language of forms that would apply to all plants, even ones never before seen. This approach also works for other natural phenomenon, such as color. Goethe identified the primal phenomenon of color as the appearance of colored edges where light and dark meet through a turbid medium (e.g., the sky at sunset or a prism held against a black and white boundary). From this singularity, the universal world of colors could be derived. The cycle of light and dark, yellow and blue, and so on and so forth are the metamorphosis of the primal contrast. When one learns to see this, they learn to see themselves in nature and their own growth; you begin to see your own growth and phases of expansion and contraction, your unfolding and fading, etc. Goethe claimed this is why the true scientist is like a poet, insofar as both read the living archetypes of nature.

The most intimate and at the same time most easily dismissed report by people across all cultures, social classes, and eras, from hermits to children, to hunters and to gatherers, is the experience of not being alone in a natural place. A forest or a meadow in which one feels seen or accompanied without seeing anyone can surely be explained away by the skeptic tools of chemical-material reductionism. Yet what cannot be explained away is the feeling of inhuman presence as a quiet attention sourced from the land itself, or rather, from the beings that dwell in and constitute it. Some may call this the genius loci, or the spirit of a place. Others, like Steiner, name specific orders of undines in flowing water, sylphs in air, gnomes in stone and earth, and salamanders in fire. The names are less important than the sense that the feeling is different from forms of social recognition. When a person looks at you, you feel judged, or maybe ignored, but definitely acknowledged in some way. When a forest looks at you, it is without expectation. It simply notes you, as one would note a ripple in a pond. Artists and poets have always known this. Urban gardeners speak at times of their plants “liking” a particular spot or “preferring” a certain neighbor. For those who have felt it, it changes how they walk in the world.

Quite the strange feeling may come across oneself when standing in a forest after rain, or watching a thunderhead build over a plain. This feeling returns to us in our consideration of nature because it is an expression of the notion that *this world is older and larger than the ‘I’ which I know now, yet I am made of it”. Rising from that, “I am also not that”. The rock does not wonder, and the cloud does not ask “why”. But people do. This intuition in the face of nature is in essence the fact that nature invites us to sanction nature by recognizing ourselves as in and of nature, in part.

The stone feels utterly other. It is cold and hard, yet you can hold it. Its weight is the weight of the Earth. Your bones and teeth are similarly made of the minerals present in the Earth, those of calcium, magnesium, and other substances that come from the Earth, just as the stone is formed. In the stone, the “hardness” about it can come off as a sturdiness as deep as the Earth goes. Just as the stone holds place in the Earth, the mineral kingdom is the foundation of our upright posture, making up the skeleton that has a shape which penetrates the downward push of gravity and holding our form in place. The mineral realm seems farthest from us, but it is the foundation of us as beings in the natural world. Our kinship with the stone is that it provides the resistance without which our “I” could never become free. Our bones are mineralized, teeth crystals, and the iron in our blood was forged in stars and carried in rocks before it ever entered a living cell. How awe filling is it that our posture depends on the calcium and phosphorus that once slept in ancient seabeds? To know the mineral world in nature is to know the sacrificial descent of spiritual beings who condensed themselves into matter so that we may have a body. When holding a quartz crystal, one holds a thought of Ahriman that has become tangible and enduring. And because ones own thinking can think that thought, you may comprehend the same spiritual activity that gave the stone its form.

Plants share a different order of kinship. We certainly do not photosynthesize, nor do we send roots into soil. But our body on one level depends on plants via the oxygen they release, the fruits and seeds we eat, the shelters, medicines, and clothes we make from their fibers and resins, and so on and so forth. More intimately, the rhythm of our own growth (particularly as responsive to seasons and light) mirrors the plant’s way of being. A child’s body grows like a seedling, and elderly person’s body withers like an autumn stem. The plant encourages us to recognize that our own bodies, in their lawful, rhythm, self-forming activity, is a continuation of the same formative forces that shape the leaf and the flower. The plant itself lives in the etheric realm, and so do we, with the addition of an “I” that has the ability to reflect on that living.

Weather is different yet again. Storms are events constituted by passing intensities of wind and water and light. One cannot hold a storm, only be within it or avoid it. The storm has no character the way an animal or plant does, yet it has mood. A sudden thunderstorm feels like anger or release, and on the other hand, the slow, gray, drizzle feels like melancholy. The storm invites us to recognize that it and you are alike, but that you can be more than it, because while the same forces that move the clouds also move your own inner atmosphere, the storm is not conscious of it. The weather itself does not think, but you who uses the same air, can think about weather. Storms in the natural world eventually pass, even the Great Red Storm on Jupiter, and so too will furious storms of emotions in the soul. In modern times, perhaps all should be prescribed time to routinely listen to the wind. It is different every day.

Animals also stand at a different threshold, and have been considered thoroughly elsewhere. Because of the animal kingdom’s proximity in form and being in the natural world to man, they express to us our own potentialities taken to the extreme, only to be taken up into ourselves again. In all cases, the intuition is the same. Nature is our own unfinished being, shown back to you in forms that cannot complete themselves. Man completes them by understanding them, living with them in harmony, and using consciousness to act freely in areas where nature can’t.

A brief consideration on the heavenly bodies: These also hold a special kind of kinship, touched upon briefly elsewhere in this blog. There is an older intuition, still present, that the planets and stars are not only objects, but beings. This is the same kind of recognition you might have when you meet a person. You see their body, but you also sense that something lives within that body, something that is looking out through the eyes. The stars and planets also have a kind of gaze. Not a human gaze with a face, although “A Trip To The Moon” makes a compelling point, but a presence that is intelligent in a way differently than Earth-intelligence, in the most literal of terms, with influences on us that have not gone unnoticed throughout various cultures in time.

Of course none of this requires a belief in spirits or the hierarchies, only that you take your own body seriously as a part of nature. Once you do, the boundary between self and world begins to soften just a bit. The pain of the animal is sympathized with in part as a version of your own vulnerability, and the plant’s growth is your own metabolism slowed down, for example. The boundaries we draw between the kinds of being are useful for scientific purposes, but do not capture the deeper comprehension that the whole of nature is alive and related. The nature of this kinship differs radically across the kingdoms, but the relationality remains. Ultimately, the kinship one feels with nature is recognition of the fact that nature was never separate from spirit.

The Shortsightedness of Absolute Idealism on Nature [WIP SECTION - UNFINISHED]

If animals are indeed separated potentialities of the human form, they carry within themselves the seed of what we have become, and what we may potentially overcome. What appears in the animal as instinctive and one-sided, bound to the collective existence of its animal nature, is the same force that in us becomes conscious and free. Following from this, the animal kingdom is not a static gallery of evolutionary “failures” or any other such nonsense. It is the field of future possibilities. People already sense this when they observe a dog’s loyalty, or a horse’s sensitivity, a monkey’s human-like movement, a crow’s problem-solving capability, and so on and so forth. In the natural world, these capabilities are the shining-forth of a future where the animals as the field of future possibilities themselves evolve in accordance with what they are the future of.

The group souls that now direct the bee’s hive, the spider’s web, the bird’s migration, and the beaver’s dam will begin to individualize. That same spiritual activity we call magnetism and somnambulism will, in those future stages, belong to animals as their own domain. Surely, they will not become human, however they will become what animals can become when the human potential that was frozen in them is finally relinquished. This stands in stark contrast to the current understanding of Spirit and Nature in Absolute Idealism. Parapsychological or various other spiritual capabilities are shown to be distinctly designated in the domain of “Nature”, yet the reasonable thinking man, who is trained to see beyond immediacy, can see that these capacities are already present in the animal kingdom. One needs only look towards the explicit experiments of Rupert Sheldrake on animal telepathy. If not this, then the seeds already present in the animal kingdom: migrating animals and homing pigeons are not tricks of chemistry, as chemistry is subordinated to them. These are forces in the spiritual world acting through nature, preparing the animal for a time when it will act from within its own dawning inner life, as a reflection of the immense universality present within the human inner life. Kinship of this kind is prompted by nature at every step of the way, for those that have the eyes to see.

Much of philosophy has missed what the point of kinship with nature illuminates. Logicist Idealism, for all its sophistication, has often treated nature as something merely to overcome in the form of a logical stage to be “sublated”, as a mirror that reflects only what spirit already knows of itself. Even if nature supposedly does not immediately display the full richness of spirit, or in other words, if nature hides the processes one finds in what can be (for the purposes of argument) called the spiritual world proper, dismissing nature would be like ignoring a newborn baby because it cannot yet speak or show its potential.

Essentially, proponents of logicist idealism do have a seed of truth. Forgiving an enemy is more true to spirit than a rock’s being heavy. The issue of spirit following or preceding nature is not the problem. In fact, the spiritual world is responsible for nature, and however one wants to put the organization (whether it is God responsible for nature, which God is), the ontological map is not in contention. Even if nature does not constitute what angels are, nature is an enabling condition for spirit to convey itself. To not recognize that we are constituted by nature, and that nature exists objectively and has a certain kind of independence is a form of undermining ourselves. Any true [Christian] and/or ethical impulse would preserve nature out of love and gratitude. For the retards and intentionally obtuse, this does not mean that nature is on the same level as spirit, for the umpteenth time, just as magnanimity towards animals prevents rationality from concluding in an equation of animality and humanity.

How Do We Overcome Nature’s Imposition?

The whole of physical being is nature. Living and non-living, beautiful and deadly alike. We are part of nature because we are in the world. At the same time, we may step back and abstract ourselves from nature, only because this is the hallmark of thought- the beginnings of freedom in a being through the capacity of developing an inner life. Nature imposes on our wills, and we overcome that imposition by working within it and transforming it. Nature is the given, it is a rock that falls, the hunger that wears us down, that stinging cold, and the blistering heat above. It imposes on us because we are of it and live inside nature.

Thinking is the first and primary, some might say only way we overcome this imposition. An animal feels hunger and seeks food, meanwhile a human can feel hunger and decide to fast for this reason or the next. Such a decision comes from an inner life, in other words the ability to form an idea that overrides the immediacy of natural impulse. This development of inner life is the beginning of any freedom in a being. Moral intuition as the direct sense of what is right, as opposed to what is “natural”, is bound upon and grows from this same capacity. Thinking in us meets nature’s push with a response outside of nature, compelling this cunning outside-of-nature-ness to take control.

This “taking control” opposes the imposition of nature, which is the given. Culture is what the human being contributes in this act of taking control. It begins the moment man takes something natural and does something with it that nature alone would not do. For instance a stone is natural. But a stone shaped into a hand axe is cultural. A handful of clay is nature. That same clay shaped into a bowl and fired into a kiln is culture. A cry of pain is natural, and a poem about pain is culture. The “opposition” in question is not a diametric opposition to nature, in fact culture grows out of and uses nature, never really escaping its laws. But it does add human willing and form. And so a bird building a nest from twigs is natural, with the bird following instinct. Every nest of that species looks nearly the same. When a human builds a house, that house is cultural. This house may be in a myriad of different styles, it can be a cabin, a yurt, perhaps a skyscraper, or even a church. The purpose may be for shelter, beauty, status, worship. There are many possibilities. This is the hallmark of culture. Culture is not always an improvement, all it does is choose. Nature supplies the materials by which culture transforms something into its own making. Therefore, the relation between culture and nature is a tension. This is why overcoming nature’s imposition does not mean leaving nature behind, it involves engaging nature fully and thoughtfully, and especially morally, that the result is neither purely natural nor purely spiritual. We may consider various impositions from nature upon the individual and how culture works to overcome these impositions. For example the most basic impositions are physical: poor eyesight, broken bones, infection, hunger, cold. We overcome these by taking nature into our own hands and reshaping it. Glasses take the natural law of refraction and bend light to correct what nature gives as a blurry image. The lens itself is nature transformed by the act of culture. Medicines come from plants, minerals, and in recent times are synthesized compounds, which are nature’s own substances but extracted, measured, and applied with human willing. We may also consider casts that use the hardness of plaster or fiberglass to immobilize a broken limb, working with the body’s own healing provided by nature, while overriding its natural tendency to move and worsen the injury.

Food and diet involve selecting and combining consumable materials into meals that no animal could possibly prepare. An animal feels hunger and seeks what is immediately available, eating raw and stopping when its hunger is gone. Man also feels hunger but does something no other animal does in the act of cooking. Cooking may be considered the original act of culture. Fire is nature. Forests burn when lighting strikes, but taking fire, keeping it, and putting food into it is a human decision. Raw meat, grains, and roots are nature. Cooked food is transformed nature. Heat breaks down fibers, it kills parasites, and releases nutrients that the raw body cannot access. A cooked meal takes time and planning, and a shared attention indicating a kind of being that has both a level of free development in internal life, and communal dependency. Once the hearth becomes the center of the house, people gather, and the meal becomes a cultural event. Cooking is only the beginning of what one does to food. Consider the preservation using various methods such as salt, the combination of foods in different manners, the spicing of foods, the ritualization of meals. Food, follows the arc of an overcoming of nature’s imposition, emphasized by the fact that food and food culture does not arrive ex nihilo. Food grows out of what grows where, that is to say, the environment sets the boundaries of possibility. Within these boundaries, human ingenuity invents dishes with their particular ingredient composition and techniques appropriate for the transformation of nature into culture through food. This is the first place where man takes what nature gives and returns something new.

After food, shelter is the first and most urgent transformation of nature. A body left exposed to wind, rain, snow, or sun, will sicken and/or die. The correct response is to build. However, what we build depends entirely on what nature gives us and what nature demands of us… at first. In a hot desert, the available materials are sun-dried mud brick, palm fronds, and the other appropriate environmental materials. Nature presents an imposition in the form of heat by day and cold by night. So the shelter may have thick walls that absorb heat slowly and release it slowly, small windows to keep the sun out while allowing for airflow, and a central courtyard that traps cooler air. In another environment, say a cold and wet first, the materials are wood and thatch. The problem presented by nature is rain and freezing temperatures. So the shelter has steeply pitched roofs to shed water, walls stuffed with moss or clay for insulation, a hearth for fire, and so on and so forth. The snowy region accordingly follows the same pattern. The point is that nature’s imposition is not always uniform, neither in particularity nor in individuality. It varies by climate, by available materials, animals, plant life, etc. Each shelter is a specific answer to a specific natural condition. That contingency shapes the whole of human culture. People do not invent architecture from pure thought, not at the beginning of time, that is. They respond to what is there, and in that response, they begin to overcome nature’s imposition. Shelter is not yet art. It only becomes architecture when the builder adds something that nature did not demand- perhaps a carved post, a symmetrical arrangement of stones, or a doorway that faces the sun for meaning, a particular design signifying some or other purpose, and so on and so forth. That extra step into deliberate form is what counts as entering the threshold into culture. Architecture only appears as an art when man says to himself that this wall is not only against the natural immediacy of weather and climate, it is also a potential creation/alteration which may serve as the gesture towards inner life. The same goes for art.

Art and technology are more abstracted from the immediacy of nature. Natural immediacies are not at first overcome through art, however a brief consideration of the natural immediacies of feeling and impulse disprove this initial position. Art works on the soul as an abstracted solution to the abstract problem of natural human impulse. Fear, desire, anger, grief, joy, and so on surge through the body in the form of basic modalities of feelings any sentient being experiences. An animal may feel fear and flee from what impelled upon it that emotion, and a human may also feel the effect of fear. However, man can feel the fear itself, manipulating its effect and subjectively conjuring itself onto the soul. The various phobias one may develop speak to this non-uniformity of natural feeling in nature, developed custom to one’s experiences. This level of abstraction marks the beginning of art’s territory. By giving feeling a form, the artist takes the raw materiality of inner life and shapes it into something sensible and tangible, forcibly changing the shape of the relationship to the feeling. Rather than being possessed by a feeling, rather, externally determined by it, the artist becomes the shaper of how this feeling is mediated, and the impulse ceases in being a wave that crashes over oneself. Art then, in part, produces capacities that carry over into life. The worst impositions of nature may be marginally fixed by a meal or diet, however the feelings of loss, illness, and death is given no voice by lower forms of culture, by and large. Art serves in the overcoming of such natural impulses in the soul.

Technology is the most extended form of this overcoming of nature. Simple tools help us lift and cut, taken to the extreme systems that take in nature seem to produce systems that operate on their own, perpetually adding more layers on top of natural laws. Consider the development of the ornithopter to the airplane. For centuries, man has tried to fly by imitating birds, the direct mimicry of natural form. And so he fashions wings and feathers for himself and flaps to no avail. Surely this is not a warning against learning from and copying nature. Far from it. True flight failed to be achieved until the emergence of the airplane, which was shaped by an understanding of the abstract laws in nature invisible in the observation of a single bird. For instance the wing of an airplane does not flap, yet it exists within the same domain of flight that the bird does, only overcome through its construction in nature. This leap from ornithopter to airplane is in essence the leap from copying nature, to comprehending and thinking beyond it, that same threshold where thought and inner life begin to urge culture to take part. That is to say, the airplane is not natural, but it could not exist without the natural world as a starting point.
