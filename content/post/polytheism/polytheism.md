## Exordium
It is the opinion of institutional religious studies that the vapid, simple observation of ideal multiplicity, that is to say, a diversity of forms presenting themselves with respect to the divine, warrants a label called “polytheism”. Unctuous religion provides content to bolster this opining up with defenses, claiming that millions of people today worship many divine beings, from Olympians to the Orishas, to the “pagan” gods. Understandably, man has always “believed” in and worshiped many gods. What experience and the accounting of these kinds proves only goes as far as to lay claim to an individuals individual interfacing with a manifestation of *something*. What that *something* is has yet to be evidence in the proposition. Polytheism is a term taken to mean belief in multiple divine beings, each with their own sphere of power; Poseidon over the seas, Osiris over the underworld, and so on, are the personalities bound up with their domains. A moment’s reflection shows that no such belief has ever existed in a pure and unqualified form. Even the most exuberantly “polytheistic” devotee pledges allegiance to a higher principle of unity. Zeus may not defy Moira, and Indra and Agni are nothing if not emanations of Brahman. We may call this unity “being”, order, law, or “the One”, but it does not matter. What matters is its presence and priority in the present. By extension, the follower who may call to Athena for wisdom surely does not deny that Athena’s power flows towards them, however they may not deny that it flows from a single cosmic source, just as the devotee of Kirshna who feels an overwhelming love for that form may not reject the unity of Brahman. Personal election of one face over another is the crux of what may be called “polytheism”. This surface level observation still does not threaten the integrity of the underlying structure of unity it relies upon, just as a citizen may favor one department of government without denying the state’s sovereignty. The supposed polytheist's devotion to a particular god implicitly reveals the presupposition of a single ground of being that sustains *all* particularities that the “polytheist” may choose from, even if this truth has been veiled by contemporary forms of understanding. Only a unified reality which expresses itself through a plurality of perspectives can hope to account for the ground of “polytheism”. 

This all only matters since the term “polytheism” has shaped damn near centuries of comparative religion as well as the popular imagination. In all works, say for example modern anthropological taxonomies of religion, the assumption has been that polytheism denotes a genuine metaphysical alternative to monotheism, that is to say, a genuine plurality of “ultimate” gods. This assumption has rarely been examined under scrutiny. What would happen if the category itself is coherent? What if every attempt to think many independent ultimates collapses by its own logic into a single ground of being? Irreducible multiplicity cannot survive this questioning, and so we must investigate what the true metaphysical status of the many gods of polytheism is. The argument, to be presented, will conclude in monadology, a system in which many are real, but none is ultimate, that the one is the very condition for their plurality. 

## Partitio and Confirmatio 

The argument unfolds in 6 steps. Each follows logically from the last, and together they form a chain, starting from the definition of hard polytheism, to the conclusion that only the monad remains.

> 1. Hard polytheism claims multiple ultimate gods exist independently.
> 2. A true god must be self-sufficient and absolutely simple
> 3. Simple substances cannot be distinguished from the inside. 
> 4. Hence, at most one simple substance can exist.
> 5. What polytheists call “gods” are actually monads.
> 6. Therefore, there is no such thing as polytheism, only the monad.

Suppose one takes multiplicity as the starting point as an emphasis of their worldview. After all, this is exactly what polytheism does, it begins with many gods and their distinct powers and their rivalries and alliances. For multiplicity to be coherent, the many require a unity that grounds and orders them in the first place. This is where monadology appears. Hegel captures the insight of Leibniz’s monadology quite well; the true idealism of the universe is one of intellectuality whose principle is individuality. Put differently, the universe is rational through and through intellectuality, and its rationality consists precisely in the fact that each individual being (each monad) is a unique perspective on the whole. Polytheism intuits this when it populates the universe with a collection of gods, each with their distinct personalities and domains of influence. But it does so in a confused manner, imagining the gods as independent agents who *happen* to coexist. Monadology thinks this same structure clearly. Each of the many monads are internally related, each mirroring the same universe from its angle, and their harmony constitutes an established order. To emphasize multiplicity is to already demand a unity that makes multiplicity intelligible. That unity is the monad, a living system of unique individuals reflecting a single totality. It is permissible in this case to say that polytheism leads to a monadology for the reason that the logic of the “many” naturally invokes the “one” that lets them be many in the first, coherent place.

Firstly, let us distinguish between an *ultimate* God, and a derivative divine being. For God to be *absolute*, such a good must be self-sufficient. This absolute depends on nothing else for its being and power. A derivative (lower case G) god, on the other hand, may be very powerful, perhaps even immortal. Yet it still owes its being to a higher principle or source. Most beings called “gods” in polytheistic traditions are not ultimate in this strong sense. In any case, a given pantheon rests on a deeper unity suggesting that even hard polytheists, when pressed, do not really believe in multiple *ultimates*. They believe in many powerful beings who are themselves embedded in a higher order. Suppose for argument’s sake that one insists on a true *hard* polytheism. They believe that several gods, each ultimate and independent, are coexistent with no higher principle above them. Then the Principle of Sufficient Reason applies. For any difference between them: why one rules the sky and another the sea, why one is male and another female, why twelve instead of five, why personified with an animal head or not, there must be a reason. The reason cannot lie within *one* god (since each is supposed to be self-sufficient and independent), nor can it lie in an external principle (since that would reintroduce a higher unity). Therefore, the differences would be entirely groundless and arbitrary, producing for us an *irrational cosmos*. Likewise, such a cosmos would be incoherent to us, hence the very *claim* of multiple independent ultimates collapses into irrationality. The only rational alternative is that what we call “gods” in polytheism are not ultimates. They are emanations grounded in a singular, self-sufficient and ultimate source.

Two conditions follow from the concept of an ultimate God. The first is self-sufficiency. An ultimate God must depend on nothing else for its being. If its being were borrowed from another, very clearly, *that* other would be greater. The next is absolute simplicity. An absolute God cannot have parts, compositions, or unactualized potentiality. Anything composed of parts depends on those parts and is thus not self-sufficient. Anything with unrealized potential depends on an externality to cause it. Therefore, a true God must be entirely simple. Consider what this entails. Most beings supposedly worshiped as gods in the polytheistic imagination are *clearly* composites. They have histories and limitations, and so cannot be ultimates. This composite might be literal, say Ganesha’s elephant head, or the composition of the Olympians pre-origins found in the Titans, and so on. Even if one insists that these beings have non-material forms, the very fact of composition means that they are dependent on their parts. All subsequently fail the test of self-sufficiency. Only a being with no parts whatsoever, that is no inner diversity and unrealized potentials, can be truly ultimate. That being, if nothing, is and must be absolutely simple. 

If a substance has no parts, then it cannot be distinguished from another substance by having *different* parts. The only possible source of difference between the two simple substances would be their internal states. A problem begins to arise in this case. The internal states of a simple substance are not self-contained, a perception is always a perception *of* something; an appetition is a tendency toward some perception. In other words, the internal states of a simple substance are essentially relational, they are perspectives of a common object, or world. Two simple substances with no external relations would have no way to differ, they would be numerically distinct but qualitatively identical, which is indistinguishable from being the same substance. If two simple substances genuinely differ (one perceives the world from point A, the other from point B), there must be a reason why each has its particular perspective. That reason cannot come from within either substance, for the reason that as simplexes, they have no internal resources to assign themselves a perspective. Nor can the reason come from their mutual interaction, since simple substances have no parts and likewise, no direct causal interaction is possible with other simplexes. The only remaining source is a higher, unifying principle that assigns to each its unique place in the order of perspectives. This principle is the supreme Monad, the highest substance that contains the reason for all others within itself. In consequence, multiple independent simple substances cannot exist. There can be at most one self-sufficient simple. All other simples are dependent, created, and assigned their perspectives by that One.

Could there be zero simple substances? Well, no. That would leave us with only composites which depend on simples, resulting in an infinite regress. Therefore, there must be exactly *one* self-sufficient simple substance, and it alone is God in the ultimate sense. This is the supreme Monad, the supreme being that is necessary and infinite, and the ground of its own existence. All other simple substances, if they exist that is, must be contingent and created. They are monads too, for they are simple centers. But they are not “gods” in any sense which evokes the conception of God most are familiar with. They owe their existence to the supreme monad, furnishing their unique perspectives from it, and are harmonized with one another through its pre-established order. Such finite monads include human souls, as well as the “gods” of what are called the polytheistic traditions. Surely they are real and conscious, but they are not ultimate. Only the supreme Monad meets the criteria of self-sufficiency and absolute simplicity without qualification. Likewise, there is at most one true God. Everything else that is divine is so by participation and limitation.

If only the supreme Monad is self-sufficient and ultimate, then what of the other gods of the world’s supposed polytheistic traditions? They are finite monads. They are certainly real beings in their own right. Like all monads, each perceives the universe from its distinctive positionality, with a degree of clarity far exceeding that of human souls, as it currently stands. They govern domains precisely because their particular perspectives have been assigned a specific sphere of influence within the pre-established harmony. Their conflicts and rivalries, as depicted in myth, are certainly not illusions. From the limited perspective of any finite monad, other monads appear as obstacles. But from the perspective of God, all is harmonious. Their “conflicts” are perspectival and relative to their level; they are how the pre-established harmony appears to beings who cannot see the whole, or their place in and of it. Polytheistic worship is certainly not an error, in this case. The error lies not in the worship of many gods, but in treating any of them as ultimate. They are dependent and contingent, harmonized from above. To mistake a finite monad for the highest is to let flow exactly the wrong kind of stream that an unexamined metaphysical polytheism invites. 

Polytheism henceforth is a phenomenal appearance, a way that finite monads (including mankind) perceive the hierarchy of spiritual beings from their limited vantage. It can not and will never stand as a genuine metaphysical category, because the only coherent ultimate reality is the supreme Monad expressing itself through an infinite plurality of finite monads. Perhaps the term may find usage as a sociological or descriptive convenience, but let it be known that there is no such thing as polytheism, there is only the monad. 

## Further consideration
The abstract identity of Spinoza lacks the moment of personality. Locke’s formulation of a world picture establishes a very mechanistic view of the world. Leibniz should be praised for overcoming both, however there are a few philosophical limitations with Leibnizian monadology. Firstly, God sets the harmony between monads, like a clockmaker would wind two clocks. The harmony is not *immanent* to the monads themselves, it is imposed from without. As a consequence, a dualism is (re)introduced between the finite monads and the infinite Monad. This pre-established harmony is *external* to itself. This would also mean that the supreme Monad (God) would stand *outside* the series of monads. Similarly, Leibniz describes monads as windowless. While this windowlessness preserves the inner activity of each monad, it also in the same move alienates each monad, making any actual intraspiritual interaction impossible. Everything that happens to a monad must spring from its own internal principle. At the same time, the harmony requires that its internal states *corresponds* to those of others. This correspondence is a guarantee by God, but not mediated by the monads themselves, it removes agency from the monad.

Leibniz’s monadology, for all its genius, suffers from some real problems. A conception of monadology from spiritual science keeps the original idea intact, but alleviates most of the aforementioned problems. From a spiritual scientific perspective, a few differences arise. Namely, that monads actually *have* windows. Direct perception between monads is achieved freely as monads learn to resonate with one another. Another difference is that the One is immanent. The supreme Monad is the actualized living totality of all monads as the ground present *within* every monad. The use of this reinscribed concept, is that it turns monadology from an externally derived system into an overview of spiritual development. It explains how individual “I”’s can grow and interact in and toward a shared harmony *without* losing their unique selfhood, but also without putting the individuality of each monad above the objective and immanent totality.
