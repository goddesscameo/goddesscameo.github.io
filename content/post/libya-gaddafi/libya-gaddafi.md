## To be or not to be (African)?
The recent AFCON 2025 win by Senegal (versus Morocco) has brought to mind an inflammatory question, or rather an observation, which casts doubt on the nature of “North Africa” in the context of being a part of Africa. North Africa’s identity is the site of a fundamental antagonism wherein objectively it is an inescapable part of the African continent by every material measure (such as geologically it is located on the continent, and it’s history, which includes its political economy and historical trade routes and such). At the same time subjectively, its people’s self-determined identity is overwhelmingly anchored in the MENA (Middle East and North Africa) civilizational sphere, often defined in explicit contrast to “Africa”. This contradiction doesn’t strike me as a mistake to be solved, but the central dogma of the region’s modern condition. The very passion by which it disidentifies itself is proof of an unsettled relationship, rather than lack thereof. I am speaking, of course, of the anti-Blackness present in North Africa. 

Ask a Moroccan or an Algerian if they are “African”, and see the varied response one will be met with. The answer, when and if it comes, is rarely a simple yes, barre the cases when conceding the very attachment of geography isn’t the framing of the question (so, answers like “of course it’s African, it is *in* Africa!”). “African”, if used at all, is often appended as a geographical afterthought- “we are in Africa, but we are not *of* it”. This is the crux of what can be called a painful schism. On the one side lies the objective and unchangeable fact of the being. On the other, lies the subjective force of self-conception which aligns itself historically, culturally, and religiously, with the Middle East and the Mediterranean; an alignment with the Arab-Islamic world of the MENA region. In a way, North Africa inherits Europe’s defining mode of metaphysics and interface with the cosmos as “a forgetting of Being”, while at the same time being rooted differently, or at least partially conditioned by, Africa. Clearly, this goes beyond syntactics and into semantics. To dismiss it with the pussyfooted stance some may call diplomatic, that North Africa is simply “both” is a cheap cop-out. It throws dirt on the actual self-identity of millions for whom “African” is not a badge of belonging, but an indication of a history where distance was constructed through centuries of civilization and trade. 

Before we can ask if North Africa is a part of Africa, we must first as: What do we even mean by Africa? The continent’s very name is an external imposition. To the ancient Greeks and Romans, the land south of the Mediterranean was not “Africa” but Libya, a coastal strip distinct from the interior. The term Africa likely stems from the Roman designation “Africa Proconsularis” for their province around Carthage, and was gradually expanded by European cartographers and colonizers. By the 19th century [Scramble for Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scramble_for_Africa), it solidified as the label for the continent. In this sense, it’s very self-conception is wrought with externality. However, the case can be made that the imposed frame was reclaimed from within via the anti-colonial and Pan-African movements of the 20th century, led by Ghana’s Kwame Nkrumah, and Tanzania’s Julius Nyerere as a rallying cry for solidarity and shared destiny. This was further institutionalized through the founding of the Organization of African Unity. Here, “Africa” has become an idea of unity against oppression (but still externally determined). Despite this, in popular vernacular a kind of reductionism persists. Globally, and importantly in some cases within North Africa itself, the term “African” has been racially narrowed to a metonym for sub-Saharan Africa. It carries a connotation of Blackness, and a perceived cultural sphere separate from the Mediterranean and Arab worlds. This reduction is a trap which instantly ensnares our initial question, making “Are you African?” feel, to many North Africans, like being asked “Are you Black, or are you from that *other* Africa?”. The question of “Africa’s” determination has yet to be proven, and has only been theorized as of thus far. The true test of it as a distinct being will only be proven in the actualized existence of its people. The more focused question one can ask then is “what is North Africa, such that it brings about distinction from Africa ‘proper’?”. To answer this question, it is necessary to point out a fundamental divide within the Arab-Islamic world itself: the Maghreb (الْمَغْرِب‎ - “the West,” encompassing Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia, Libya, and often Mauritania) versus the Mashriq (الْمَشْرِق‎ - “the East,” the Arab states of the Middle East). A North African’s primary civilizational orientation is often first to this Maghreb-Mashriq continuum, not to a pan-African one.

Beneath this level lies the substance of the Amazigh, or Berber, people. These are the indigenous peoples of North Africa, with languages and cultures stretching back millennia before Phoenician or Arab arrival, and are the uncontested “African” layer of the region. To acknowledge the Amazigh is to acknowledge that North Africa’s deepest roots are autonomously and uniquely *African*, and not imported from the Middle East. This complicates any simple Arab vs Africa binary, and is key to understanding the regions' character. Additionally, geography dictates an exception. The Nile Valley (primarily in Egypt and Sudan) forms a distinct corridor. Egypt’s history and psyche are fundamentally shaped by the river flowing from the African heartland. Sudan, even more explicitly, is a historical pivot point, a vast country torn by conflicts often framed as Arab versus African. This corridor ties North-Eastern Africa inextricably to the Sahel and the Horn, blurring any neat boundary and reminding us that the Sahara’s edges are zones of gradation and not distinct hard lined walls. Thus, we begin with contested definitions with “Africa” as an imposed label, a reclaimed project, and a racially narrowed colloquialism. “North Africa” is a modern geopolitical term masking a mixed reality of Arab-Islamic identity, Amazigh indigeneity, and the unifying exception of the Nile. It is within this tangled semantic field that the larger conflict plays out.

However fiercely identity may pull toward the Mediterranean and the Mashriq, North Africa remains wedded to the continent by chains of geography, history, and modern necessity. This is the objective, inarguable substrate upon which all subjective identities in the region have to contend with and integrate (sometimes unsuccessfully). North Africa rests upon the African tectonic plate; its mountains, faults, and mineral wealth are born of the same continental processes as the rest of Africa. The Sahara, so often imagined as a “barrier” is in fact a shared system which stretches from the Atlas foothills to the Sahelian shore. It is a connector that demands specialized adaptation, fostering trans-Saharan networks. Most definitively, the Nile River ( which we may call Egypt’s raison d'être) is an *African* river. Its lifeblood flows from the Ethiopian Highlands and the Great Lakes region. Egypt’s destiny is therefore irrevocably tied to the management, politics, and climate of the African interior. The historical tether cannot be ignored either, long predating the era of the Arab-Islamic. Ancient Egypt’s civilization cannot be understood outside its perpetual dialogue (whether through trade, warfare, and migration) with Nubia (aka modern Sudan), and cultures further South. The Kingdom of Kush conquered and ruled Egypt, a profound African reversal of power. Deeper in the desert, civilizations like the Garamantes (in modern Libya) built sophisticated subterranean irrigation systems and acted as crucial intermediaries, proving the Sahara was a space of commerce. Foundational to all is the Amazigh, the indigenous inhabitants whose presence for thousands of years constitutes the incontestable truth that the first “North Africans” were, and are, an African people. 

The final link to “Africa proper” is the political-economic necessity of the 21st century. The various crises of the Sahel, where ever they come from (looking at you France), do not respect colonial borders, and spill northward. Meaning the instability in Mali or Niger becomes a direct security threat to Algeria, Tunisia, and Libya. Membership in the African Union also acts as a strategic platform for conflict mediation and collective bargaining. To abandon it would be to forfeit influence and abdicate responsibility for the systems which directly impact North African security and prosperity. North Africa is, in effect, bound by the objective fact sovereignty, which ties it to Africa. Thus, while a North African might look eastward toward Mecca in prayer, they stand atop African history and the African future of shared crises and fortunes. Any complete disavowal of “Africa” is a flight from the world. The land one stands on insists upon the very connection to Africa which some North Africans intend to disavow.

It’s strange, then, that the anchor is so very clearly African, but identity decisively points east. The allegiance of the region is certainly not to a continental project, it is towards the civilizational sphere of the MENA world as an active daily substance. It becomes less strange when one familiarizes themselves with a few “pillars” if you will. The first is the religion. Islam is the primary communal bond, and the foundational layer of social and spiritual life. Its culture and moral framework orient the believer towards the holy cities of Mecca and Medina in the Arabian Peninsula, and not towards the African interior. The Ummah is the imagined polity whose heartlands lie in Asia. The language goes hand in hand with Islam. Arabic is the language of the sacred text of the Quran, media, and politics. The lingua franca that ties a Moroccan student to a Lebanese novel, or an Algerian politician to a Qatari news (Al Jazeera comes to mind) is Islam. This shared linguistic worlds creates a continuity with the Mashriq, making Cairo and Beirut feel culturally closer than Bamako or Abuja. The daily cultural diet does not dissuade the continuity, either. The dominance of Arab satellite television (like the aforementioned Al Jazeera, MBC, etc.), and the circulation of Arab music, or Ramadan television dramas, creates a social world through the digital which faces East. One final factor to take into account is the historical context of Ottoman rule or influence, which brought centuries of integration into an Eastern empire, and the later anti-colonial struggle framed through the potent ideology of Pan-Arabism. Gamal Abdel Nasser or Ba’athist parties are dreams of Arab unity, not of African unity. The political imagination stands as horizontally Arab, not African.

## Not African and not Arab? Not realistic.
This consolidation of MENA identity conditions an active dis-identification. “African”, often reductively coded as Black and sub-Saharan becomes the distinguishing other by which the social process that separates “us” from “them” takes place. This is heard in colloquial or casual speech and seen in social attitudes toward darker-skinned Africans. This attitude is reflected in media representations that subtly or overtly frame "Arab" North Africa as more civilized, developed, or historically significant than "Black" Africa. The distancing taking place here is hierarchical, and acts as a mechanism which maintains the coherence of the preferred self-conception as being solely in continuity with the Middle East, and coincidentally being placed in Africa. Thus, when asked about identity, the heart’s answer is drawn from this shared reality of politics, religion, culture, language, media, and so on and so forth, all flowing from the East. The African ground, for all its material weight, remains for many, a silent geographical contingency, overshadowed by the life of Arab-Islamic civilization. This is the powerful subjective truth that any honest analysis must confront. 

With respect to this, we have a contradiction at the crux of North Africa’s identity. The vehement distancing from “Africa” is not evidence by a lack of relationship, it is the loudest pronouncement of its unresolved relationship with it. One does not build an identity *against* something that is irrelevant. The force of the rejection mirrors the depth of the historical “entanglement”, which is putting it lightly. The primary source of this ordeal is found in the Trans-Saharan Slave Trade. For over a thousand years, this civilizational-scale process crafted a brutal social world where the “African”, particularly the Black African from south of the desert, became synonymous with “other” and “servile” in North African societies. This process embedded a structural and psychological anti-Blackness into the fabric of the social relations, establishing a hierarchy of human value that placed the Arabized or Amazigh North African above the “abeed” (slave, a term still used derogatorily). This is the origin of the separation, a hierarchy native to the nation, but certainly reinforced by European colonialism. French and Italian administrators, armed with the pseudoscience of racial anthropology, pursued explicit “Mediterraneanist” or “Berberist” policies. They argued that North Africans (particularly Amazigh populations) were a white and noble race, distinct from and superior to the Negroes of sub-Saharan Africa. This attitude, being internalized by segments of the local elite, added a modern European-sanctioned racial taxonomy to the older Arabo-Islamic one. Being offered a bargain which North Africans could not refuse, they were to be placed on a higher rung of the colonial racial ladder, contingent on accepting their own differentiation from the content below. It is not a coincidence that the term here is *bargain*, harkening back to the Faustian Bargain, and the Heideggerian ‘forgetting of being” mentioned that North Africa has inherited from Europe. This is just one avenue to be explored. 

In the modernist project of building independent nation-states, anti-Blackness became a functional “moment of differentiation”. For North African elites forging post-colonial identities, they required a “constitutive outside”, or an “other”, against which the modern national self could be defined. The constructed “Black African” serves this purpose perfectly. Defining the modern “Arab-Maghrebi” citizen involved a conscious (and in some cases unconscious) differentiation from this other, purifying the national-self image and aligning it with a perceived lineage of historic (Phoenician, Roman, Arab) Mediterranean civilization, in contrast to a stereotyped “primitive” and “tribal” Africa. We have now a contemporary performance of that centuries-old differentiation, a socio-political ritual that reinforces the borders of a non-African national identity. The scorn for the Black migrant is, tragically and ironically, a perverse reaffirmation of a separate self. North Africa’s “non-African” identity is, to a significant degree, built upon the history of its relationship with and by virtue of Africa. 

To view this struggle as between Arab and African, is to miss the signals originating from North Africa itself which problematize this binary. We can look to the Amazigh Revival as an indigenous corrective. The fight for Tamazight language and culture is a reclamation of an African identity which predates Arabization by a thousand years. In this assertion, what is to be found is an assertion of North African identity that is uniquely African, yet distinct from Arab political hegemony and sub-Saharan cultures. Further south is the Sahara-Sahel zone, where one can interpret this land as a land of synthesis and differentiation- one which purist ideologies will be forced to categorize incorrectly, or count as a deviation from the norm of a worldview. Nations like Mali, Niger, and Sudan are crucibles where Arab and Amazigh North Africa has blended with Songhai, Fulani, and Nubian cultures for millennium. Timbuktu’s manuscripts, are written in Arabic script but preserve the knowledge of the Malian empire. This is a good example of “fusion”. These societies render the harsh distinction between northern littoral and southern forests moot. 

## Gaddafi 

Gaddafi’s Libya stands apart. Isolated in the Arab world, he declared himself “an African, catalyzing a decade of objective institutional creativity aimed at nothing less than the political and economic unification of the African continent. His government became the primary architect and financier of the African Union's transformative leap from the loose OAU to a more integrated body. Libya funded over 15% of the AU’s budget, championed the creation of the African Investment Bank and the African Monetary Fund, and was the driving force behind RASCOM, the first satellite for African telecommunications, breaking a foreign monopoly. He leveraged state wealth to build infrastructure across the continent and positioned Libya as *the* patron for liberation movements and marginalized regions. Gaddafi represented a tangible, institutional answer to the question of continental unity that far exceeded the symbolic gestures of other North African states. Unfortunately, this objective reality being created existed in a stark parallel to a subjective social reality it failed to fundamentally alter, or rather, did not get to alter. While the state built African unity, Libyan society, which carried the historical baggage of the trans-Saharan slave trade and hierarchical social structures, continued to exhibit widespread anti-Black prejudice This was visible in the treatment of sub-Saharan migrants. Gaddafi’s Pan-Africanism did not constitute a national reckoning with racism or stem from a grassroots embrace of African identity. This paradox (which is in reality, the paradox of North African identity) reached its climax after the 2011 NATO-led intervention. The instant evaporation of the Pan-African project revealed its precarious foundation- that it was an edifice built on state power and not on broad objective spiritual conviction. The subsequent descent of Libya into chaos including the resurgence of open slave markets targeting Black Africans, and the collapse of living standards (from one of Africa’s highest literacy rates and GDP per capita to a failed state) demonstrated that Gaddafi’s noble project of unification was doomed to fail if it did not reconstitute North African identity, or reconcile what it actually means to be a North African. The very fact that slavery re-emerged tells you something intrinsically about the social substance of the Libyan people, and by extension North Africans as a whole. That the post-Gaddafi landscape did not revert to a neutral "Libyan" identity, instead regressing into the raw, unmediated societal pathologies and tribal factions that the state had previously contained and channeled, illustrates that this was a reversion to an older, unhealed spirit. In other words, the reappearance of slavery is the return of the repressed historical spirit, proof that the social substance of the people had not been transformed. Most, if not all countries (barre Congo and Sudan), have confronted what it means to be an African (particularly this or that kind of African- Angolan, South African, Ethiopian, Kenyan, etc.), and so this brings a sense of closure to sub-Saharan countries that is felt in contrast when dealing with the North African countries. This is part of why Africans south of the Sahara may find it easier to forget North Africa, because they have not dealt with this intrinsic anti-Blackness. After all, besides Gaddafi’s attempt and the practical establishment of the OAN (of which Morocco left in 1984 as a protest for the OAN recognizing the SADR. It must also be stated that Western Sahara was not part of historic Morocco in any meaningful sovereign sense, and so leaving the OAN in 1984, then applying for membership in the EEC is not a good look), the only interface with North Africans that sub-Saharans have is from North Africans arguing why they should be able to say the n-word. There is no real nationally recognized effort in reconstituting any kind of North African identity.

Gaddafi’s project, therefore, was not wrong in its vision, but premature in its historical moment. It demonstrated that objective institutions cannot survive if they outpace the zeitgeist. One can say this was a failure of synchronization. The projects' destruction demonstrates for us that the central unresolved problem for North Africa is existential. What does it mean to be North African? Until this question is answered in a way that reconciles body and spirit, heaven and earth, all grand political architectures in North Africa will remain vulnerable. The future of North Africa as an integral part of Africa does not hinge on more trade agreements or AU resolutions, though these are necessary. It hinges on an existential, spiritual reckoning that has yet to occur, one that is to be found in a collective and conscious confrontation with the historical that created its dis-identity. Until it does, North Africa will remain what it is today: the continent’s estranged shore, physically present but spiritually separated, building its identity upon the forgetting of its deepest and most intimate connection. The path belonging to North Africa is not eastward, it is inward, into a history it must remember down into the plains and forests across the desert, and inward into itself into a self it has yet to become.
