## Intro
The featured header image above depicts the figures of Tristan and Isolde. Their story is one that, on the surface, appears to be a medieval tale of romance and forbidden love. Tristan, a brave Cornish knight, is sent to Ireland to escort Isolde, an Irish princess, to marry his uncle, King Mark. During the journey, they accidentally drink a strong potion meant for Isolde and mark on their wedding night. The potion turns out to be a love potion, a potion which induces, or suggests, strong feelings of love toward another. As a result, it binds them in an irresistible passion. Despite marrying King Mark, Isolde and Tristan begin a secret affair, fraught with danger and suspenseful situations. Eventually they are discovered and forced to flee. The story ends in tragedy, with one version finding a wounded Tristan sending for Isolde, the only one who can hear him. He arranges for the ship carrying her to fly a white sail if she is aboard. His jealous wife lies, telling him the sail is back. Believing Isolde isn’t coming, Tristan dies of despair, Isolde arrives, finds him dead, and dies of grief beside him. In one sense, the story does tell of a forbidden love, in the sense that the true meaning behind the veil of this story is esoteric and not immediately accessible. To enter into this narrative is to observe an allure, drawing one into the familiar territory of the living forces within the human soul. Within Tristan one finds the consummate achiever, a master of sword and strategy. His world comprises action, conquest, and honor. However, beneath the armor resides a loneliness which no comrade’s praise can fill. When encountering Isolde, it is less a meeting of two people, and much more of a “completion”. He finds within her the embodiment of fulfillment. Likewise, his desire for her is a desperate, spiritual thirst mistaken for a worldly one. He is the masculine spirit, proficient in extending dominion over the shape of the external world, being encountered by a wave of unfamiliarity in feeling and soul. It would be correct to say that his tragedy is the belief that wholeness lies in another, turning love away from communion into fatalistic ends. Contrast this with Isolde, who begins as a figure defined by her function. She is the “other” to which Tristan remedies himself with. Archetypically, she can be considered a healer. And so, when ingesting the potion, both figures find themselves awakening to a different law in their hearts. In Tristan, Isolde finds the incarnation of the romantic, the opposite of the worldly ordered world of obligation (particularly since princess marriages were for political aims and not borne of true love). Her journey descends into the sovereignty of the heart, where all bonds and identities dissolve into a unitary connection. This is the mirror of the feminine dilemma; women find themselves resisting the urge to completely surrender themselves to Eros, where love becomes the totality of existence, in effect erasing the self it initially wanted to fulfill. Contained within these two figures is an immense magnetism, where each polarity contains one half of totality, and at once what the other needs. Yin-yang, being-non-being, day-night, sun-moon, and so on and so forth. Man exists as citizens of both regions, oscillating between each. To contemplate upon this fact of reality is to ask oneself, “what, essentially, are man and woman?”. Is their polarity merely some biological necessity, or social determination? Or does it point towards something more fundamental in the structure of human being? It is here we may begin an exploration with a distinct and, some might argue, provocative lens. Behind the veil or romance, this story points towards the dynamics foundational to man’s existence. Because the human spirit evolves through direct being and participation, existence itself is structured by the polarities of activity and receptivity, or any of the various polarities which define respective modes of being such as thought and feeling, or outward formation and inward space. To become “whole”, consciousness must fully understand (and inhabit, however this mechanism of reincarnation and spiritual economy is beyond the scope of this article) it’s opposite. The tension and attraction between what we call masculine and feminine principles is the primary schoolhouse for this education, and as such, Tristan and Isolde represent unconscious enactments of this law. Much time can be spent on any number of interpretations, dissecting this or that aspect of the story, etc., but our principal aim in this article is “male and female”. Correspondingly, we can define “male and female” or “man and woman” as the complementary modes through which a genderless human consciousness experiences earthly life in this period of time. Let this not be confused with static identities, for they are temporary orientations which themselves have been flipped and turned inside out at times.

Contemporary problems hold back our understandings of “man and woman”. Modern discourse on gender, for all its analytical rigor, operates within a framework of lacking. When it comes to sex, it can describe differences, whether through the lens of biological essentialism, or social constructivism, but it cannot ultimately answer why these differences should *matter* in any existential sense. At best, a conception can be developed which relies on the natural immediacy of the sexed distinction, out of which social institutions make use of this content and fashion what they will from it- typically the family structure which interfaces with civil society and the market. However, this lacks a freely determined *telos* for sex, let alone gender, which we can consider briefly as the configuration of polarities in the makeup of man (and of which we will deal with later). What *all* modern views lack is a coherent and unifying telos. 

First, modern conceptions lack a meaningful account of sexual difference itself. They can catalogue distinctions as either dictated by genes or culture, but what of the archetypal magnetism that arises from these distinctions? The lack of explanation for that animating force allows many “New Age” beliefs, or any belief which is subjective and lacks rigor, to fill in the blanks. Without a purpose, a reason why these dynamics exist, difference is fated to be reduced to either a mechanical fact (of which mechanistic and chemist modes of being are to be used by something higher. This includes the solipsistic mechanism of the Concept, Hegelians aren’t excluded!) or arbitrary construction, rendering the draw between masculine and feminine principles as a byproduct of biological reproduction, or social fiction. A second lack is a sustainable model for the integration of the body and consciousness. The materialist view, for example, makes the body a cage of determinism, worshiping its god “chance”. Constructivism likewise treats the problem of consciousness as incidental. Neither satisfactorily explains how our desires, drives, or identities interact with our *embodied* reality. We are left with a gap between what we are told we are as an organism, or merely as a social subject, and what we *experience* ourselves to be as conscious beings *inhabiting* a gendered form with personal (and spiritual) urgency. A third lack can be the lack of a framework which reconciles universal patterns and infinite personal variation. What destiny, to be caught between the false choice of rigid categories, and relativity so total that it obliterates the very possibility of a common ground which it is generated by? These failures result in an intelligibility. We are *unable* to speak meaningfully about a common human experience shaped by these two polarities, while *also* honoring the novelty, or unique journey, of every individual. 

Ultimately, and most importantly (for the philosophically minded), modern conceptions lack a *telos*. The most significant absence in our current understandings is what I initially mentioned- telos. By denying any inherent purpose or end goal to the masculine-feminine polarity, human relationships and gender identity are severed from any higher, ideal, cosmic, spiritual development. This can help describe the crisis of meaning we face today, or rather, this attitude is a byproduct of modernity. I surely don’t mean to imply that “solving gender roles” (read: resorting to dogmatic and blind traditionalism) will help us resolve the “crisis of meaning” in modern day. What I wish to underscore is that if this fundamental aspect of human being has no purpose beyond utility or subjective expression, then agonies and pleasures, such as those expressed in the tale of Tristan and Isolde, no matter how beautiful or sublime, are coincidences. Our longing for completion is dead on arrival, being rendered biochemical or social accidentals.

So, we will solve this problem. Not comprehensively, I can only provide suggestions and clarifications with the help of anthroposophy. For it to be a comprehensive solution, there is the implication that the reader takes it upon themselves to live the upcoming wisdom, in one form or another. ## The Story
To move beyond our current impasse, we can turn to the framework of spiritual science found in anthroposophy. Such an approach does not contradict empirical observation, but does seek to understand into the realm which gives meaning and context to what we observe in the sensible world. It helps provide exactly what is missing via a coherent teleological narrative of human development in which sexual polarity plays a part. 

Firstly, we will begin with “human”. To speak of “man” is to invoke more than a biological species; it is a designation of a universal category of being defined by the potential for self-conscious spirit and moral freedom in the context of a partnership with the cosmos. This potential is our shared *telos*, the inherent destination inscribed in our nature. The human being is thus a process of becoming, and describes the latent capacity to realize being-hood. This journey of course in not uniform. The very structure of a developmental process, where a universal potential seeks realization under conditions of freedom and through the resistance of matter, *necessitates* a spectrum of manifestation. Within the human family, this appears as a divergence in the pace and degree of awakening. Some individuals, through intensified activity and under certain cosmic and karmic conditions, crystallize the human telos earlier and more fully. They become the forerunners, those in whom the future form of humanity becomes partially visible today. They act as translators for higher possibilities into forms that can gradually be comprehended and integrated by the broader collective. On the other hand, the same freedom that allows for accelerated development implies the possibility of arrest. Others may remain more firmly embedded in earlier, more instinctual stages of the human archetype. They are the echoes of humanity’s past forms, persevering strengths and memories that the advancing vanguard party might otherwise lose in its forward striving. What is being described here is an intrinsic feature of a cosmos in evolution, and the feature of any class of being which undergoes true evolution (involving telos). There are those that are advanced, those that are on the right track, and those that lag behind. Between these three points exists a gradient, in the same way that a single seed produces a field of plants at different stages of bloom. The one human telos manifests across a field of consciousness in varying stages of realization. 

Since we have established our telos as the universal potential for spirit to become self-aware and free, it is appropriate to discuss the core of this being as the “I”, or the ego. The ego is the genderless seat of consciousness. Its telos is to awaken to its own spiritual nature and participate in the evolution of the world. This potential cannot be realized abstractly and must be realized in the world, and this requires a journey of individuation, and a descent into a kind of “limitation” which forces it to develop self-awareness and moral capacity. This is where the differentiation into masculine and feminine principles becomes essential. The creation of two distinct, but complementary, modes of embodied existence is the next topic.

The cosmology of anthroposophy is derived from Rudolf Steiner’s clairvoyant (which has a stricter definition than colloquially understood) investigation of the “Akashic Record”. If in the middle of reading this “story” stemming from anthroposophy, it starts to become unbelievable, you can skip to a section where I extrapolate the logical and structural takeaways as it relates to gender and sex, or, alternatively, treat this all as metaphor. This research into the “Akashic Record” describes, among many things, human evolution across vast epochs and stretches of time. In this view, the current physical form of humanity is the result of a long process of densification and specialization. He posits that in a remote past named the Lemurian epoch (which I argue should be called Thermian), presented a world alien to what we know now, characterized by a state of fluidity and malleability that permeated the Earth and its life forms. The Earth was described as a semi-fluid, thick substance, compared to a “fairly thick muddy sauce or soup”, or a “thickish liquid”. The atmosphere was much denser than it is today, water was also “much more fluid”. Physical malleability was key, seeing as the human body and other organisms consisted of “soft plastic matter” because the Earth’s crust was not yet hardened. Forms could be shaped and changed more easily by will and spiritual forces. Plant and animal life existed in archaic, often gigantic forms. What are now small ferns were then mighty trees, forming vast forests. The animal kingdom was dominated by creatures of an “amphibious” character. The most prominent descriptions provided are of giant, slime-like organisms. One source describes “giant slime oysters” or jelly-like creatures so vast that “all of France could have easily fit” on their backs (talk about escargot). Since we are focused on man for the time being, we shall turn our attention there and inward. I only wished to portray a kind of equilibrium between being and the environment that being resides in, or in logical form a kind of commensurability with global and local, revealing their unity.

The human being existed in an androgynous/bisexual state. The physical body was soft and plastic, malleable towards the self-determined forces of man and the environment (this, according to Steiner, helped in the formation of organs, which is important for later). Many world religions and mythologies preserve the memory of humanity’s original bisexual state. For example, the oldest Ancient Greek gods were described as having two sexes; in the mystery temples, only hermaphroditic figures were present. The Zoroastrian Myth portrays the memory even clearer through its narratives. So what of reproduction? Well, reproduction was a process of self-fructification, as opposed to the dualistic act we are familiar with. Self-fructification is an influential concept to understand, because prior to the sexed distinction, modes of reproduction up until the middle of the “Lemurian” period evolved through several distinct *non-sexual* stages, culminating in the birth of biological sexuality as we know it. The modes progressed from the immediacy of self-generation, or sprouting, where the “astral” human” incorporated etheric matter into its form, grew, and then split, similar to a bud sprouting. It then continued as humans were still unified with what would later become bird and lower mammal species. The early Lemurian period still saw a “spiritualistic” reproduction, where reproduction occurred through the forces of Kama (astral desire/feeling), which was still dissolving in the general environment, and not yet individualized. In spiritual science, the figures of Adam Kadmon, from Hebrew esotericism, and the Giant Ymir, from North mythology, are preserved clairvoyant memories of a literal, prior state of being. These figures represent a lost primal unity between humanity, the Earth, and all life. Both myths depict a time when the Earth itself was a giant *human* form (and remember, this is not “man” as it is currently, human is a designation). Accordingly, the killing of Ymir, from whose body the world is fashioned (his flesh becoming Earth, bones becoming mountains, etc.) symbolizes the cosmic process of densification and differentiation. It is the death of this original unity. As the Earth’s matter condensed and cooled, the one unified being had to separate to allow for further evolution. This is a *literal fact*, according to spiritual science. To the casual reader, I can only appeal to its structural coherence, that the fact that in its earliest stages the entire etheric substance of the Earth consisted of undifferentiated human archetypes makes perfect logical sense. 

Let us return again to the form of man in Lemuria, particularly on the nature of the soul of man. The human soul was inherently male-female (androgynous). In this early state, the soul could completely express its complete, bisexual nature directly onto the soft physical matter of the body. This is how sexual expression was related to man in those times. Regarding sexual reproduction, because each being contained both principles, reproduction was an internal process of self-fructification, as previously mentioned. A human being could bring forth another from within itself, without the need of a partner. The form of consciousness in man had humanity living in a dream-like, or picture-consciousness. People perceived the world primarily through hearing and touch, experiencing it as a flow of images and natural forces rather than clear, conceptual thoughts. They lacked memory, and a capacity for what we now call thinking.

The primary catalyst for the origin of the sexed distinction, this process of division, was the increasing density and solidification of the Earth’s substance. As matter became harder and more rigid, the soul could no longer perfectly impose its own bisexual (male-female) image upon it. Likewise, the physical form was forced by external nature to specialize. It could no longer sustain the full expression of both soul principles (Will and Imagination) in one form. Thus, it split into two specialized vessels. The male aspect of the soul was associated with Will (*Iccha Shakti*), and the female aspect with Imagination (*Kriya Shakti* or the rudimentary beginning of thought, with thought being described here as an inner generative process). With this new form, a loss of self-fructification (the force that was once directed *entirely* outward for reproduction) occurred, and was *partially* blocked. This surplus energy was redirected inward. When one considers that self-fructification can be read as reproduction, and follow that reproduction does not only mean sexual reproduction, but the upkeep and maintenance of being, the implication for this formative energy as that which builds and sustains the physical organism and drives reproduction becomes clearer. Previously, this force was entirely engaged in the act of self-generation and sustaining the plastic form of older man. As matter condensed and the body specialized into male *or* female form, this “one way street” became a dead end, so to say. An individual could no longer complete the creative circuit *alone*. A portion of this formative energy now existed as surplus to the external requirement of solitary reproduction, and was dammed up within the organism. Seeing as it was “dammed up”, this energy looked for a new outlet and so it turned inward and upward, becoming the building substance for a new interiority (the refined organization necessary for thought, memory, and individuated consciousness as ego). Steiner describes this as the fructification of the human being by the Spirit. 

The middle of the Lemurian epoch brought about the division of sexes from hermaphroditic, or androgynous man, for this reason. Another connection one may bring up is that the transition from the dreamy, picture-like consciousness (where one lived within spiritual images and directly perceived natural forces) to a sensory, brain-bound consciousness is the structure of the “Fall” of man, of his necessary descent into matter. The soul, to gain self-awareness had to build a physical body, with specialized sense organs, redirect creative energy inward to form a brain, and begin to rely on the senses and, eventually, logical thought to construct the “picture” of the world. There are a few notable consequences to this “Fall”. The first is the loss of intuitive knowledge. Humanity, as a whole, lost the old, instinctive clairvoyance and direct perception of spiritual beings and natural forces. The rudimentary emergence of the ego as a result of the withholding of the formative energy/soul-force inward (due, again, to the densification of the world and of man) resulted in the creation of an interior world for man. This separation from the outer world led to self-interest, or selfishness (and by extension desire). Likewise, man’s striving for inward development became selfish. This selfishness will play an interesting part in our story soon enough. On the other hand, there is a positive side. With isolated selfhood and sensory-based inner activity came the capacity for free choice, personal judgment, and moral responsibility. This is otherwise known as the *true* origin of Good and Evil. Man was no longer purely instinctive or guided by direct spiritual impulse. 

To recap for the sake of brevity, a division of man from bi-sexuality to uni-sexuality occurred. The two sexes developed along different, but complementary paths, particularly in early Lemurian society. The “Masculine Path” (Will) focused on mastery over external natural forces and the physical world. Education for boys involved extreme hardening and endurance to strengthen the will and kill the carnal, freeing the will-power (Iccha Shakti). Men were seen as conduits for divine, natural powers. The “Feminine Path” (Imagination/Memory) focused on absorbing inner meaning and the spiritual qualities of nature. Education for girls cultivated a dreamy imagination, and a calm receptivity in the face of natural beauty and terror, such that they should serve as poised conveyors of what nature says. Within temples, initiates engaged in direct “intercourse with the Beings who work at the building of the world itself”. This gazed into the “creative laboratory of Nature”, perceiving and participating in the spiritual forces behind the world. This experience consecrated the initiates (who, again, were female), and their very presence exerted a natural branded magical influence on others. It is not explicitly stated that this activity was segregated by sex, but it does establish that women developed the essential faculties that would qualify them for such work. Through this, women developed the first germs of memory, and the earliest moral concepts of “good and evil”. They became the first interpreters of nature and the originators of a more personal, inner life.

This “infection” still affects us today, manifest in specific traits. However, enough have been said on this topic.

If the Lemurian epoch (at least, starting in the middle of it) is characterized by a more “masculine” mode of interface- that of powerful will and mastery over the body and natural forces, then over time this mode would start to develop a more “feminine” bias towards highly developed memory. Therefore, this change, this metamorphosis of consciousness, physicality, and civilization marks a definitive step away from an instinctual union with nature, towards a more self-aware historically grounded existence. This new epoch is termed the “Atlantean”. Lemurian consciousness was a dreamlike picture-consciousness, where powerful will and direct perception of nature reigned, but it also lacked memory. No memory existed at this time to retain ideas/concepts or experiences. For this reason, the transitory state from the middle-Lemurian period, into the Atlantean found *women* with a specialized role. The education of girls was *sharply* differentiated from the “hardening” of boys, since it aimed at developing a specific soul quality. Girls were deliberately exposed to powerful natural phenomena and brutal fights between men. The intention wasn’t to provoke fear or aggression, but to allow them to perceive these events with calmness and feel an admiration for the display of strength and prowess, in an effort to foster a disposition to dreaming, to “revelling in fancy”. So, through their senses, women drew in the forces of nature, and allowed them to react upon their souls. This process formed “the germs of memory” as Steiner calls it, which to be clear was a monumental step in the development of human consciousness. True memory did not exist in humanity before this. Thus, the Atlantean awoke into a world of *memory*. While they did not yet possess our logical and abstract reason, they thought in vivid, remembered images and sense impressions. This newfound ability to recall the past gave their lives a sense of personal history and continuity, which became the very foundation of their societies. Perhaps this is what Plato meant when he said that all learning is remembering? He was, after all, an initiate of the Pythagorean mystery school. In *The Dialectic of Sex*, Firestone presented black male revolutionaries as *mimicking* the white patriarchal power they claimed to oppose, arguing that their intention was to simply dominate black women as a compensation for their societal subordination. Another example of non-solidarity. The redirected gaze shifted the focus from the anti-black violence of the state to an internal critique of black male character, a form of “victim blaming”, of which modern feminists ironically detest. This also helped cement the institutional template of an enduring archetype of the black male radical as a hypocritical patriarch, which would then be later uncritically digested by gender analysis. Using this “pathology” of black masculinity, we can trace other damning tropes into feminist origins. Rape, as a patriarchal weapon, or, the thesis that black men use rape as a tool to dominate black women within a patriarchal framework is credited to feminists Joyce Williams and Karen Holmes. The idea that the black male saw violence and sexual aggression as forms of compensation for a lack of manhood imposed by racism is traced to the work of criminologist Lynn A. Curtis. Theories born from this feminist gaze, rooted in criminological assumptions, were sanitized and institutionalized by being detached from their white authors and repackaged as objective “gender studies”. Perhaps the worst figure one can point to in this dynamic is found in Bell Hooks. Far from offering any sort of liberatory “Black” feminism, Hooks crafted a political ethic that ultimately *served* white patriarchy and reinforced anti-black male stereotypes. There are two, out of many, interconnected manipulations we will focus on. The first is the pathologization of black masculinity, and the prescription of interracial marriage as salvation. 

Hooks’ central depiction of black men is a repackaging of aforementioned 19th-century racist ethnology and 1970s feminist dogma. We can trace her narrative directly to feminists like Firestone, Williams, and Karen Holmes. She popularized these white-authored theories within black discourse. Even the portrayal of pre-colonial African societies as brutally patriarchal by hooks is an uncritical repetition of racist ethnology from slavery apologists such as Dr. Samuel Cartwright, who painted African men as savage oppressors, to justify colonial “rescue” narratives. Naturally, hooks provides a pseudo-historical basis for viewing black masculinity as inherently pathological. This leads to our second manipulation- the marriage to white patriarchs as liberation. Having painted black men as irredeemably patriarchal threats; a more dangerous “enemy within” the black community, Hooks implicitly positions relationships with white men as a path to safety, validation, and freedom. This drastically overlooks a grotesque history. Anti-miscegenation laws were primarily enforced *against* black men, not against white men seeking black women. By reframing those same men as potential liberators in the present, the material threat shifts from a white male violence (which can be argued to be the structural violence of capital), to black male violence. Functionally, this is a grift which served to break black solidarity. It presents black nationalism and power movements as inherently sexist threats, while offering assimilation via marriage to the white ruling class as the normative feminist solution. This is not a threat to what can be called “white supremacy” in any way, it is an inversion from the inside out as a form of “managing” it, where black women are encouraged to seek individualistic rescue within a system, rather than grasp any higher social truth in order to combat it. 

The pursuit of feminism's pursuit of political legitimacy led it to forge alliances with both the carceral state and the security apparatus underpinning the American empire. The movement’s strategic shift from the late 20th century toward state-centric solutions culminated in the Violence Against Women Act, and the promotion of mandatory arrest and no-drop prosecution policies in domestic violence cases. The function of this was a consciously empowered police and legal prosecution base. This result was catastrophic for black communities, fueling the mass criminalization of black men, and with a bit of bitter irony, black women victims at high rates, as police often “misread” defensive actions or chaotic scenes as antagonistic. The feminist lobby accepted this “collateral damage” (although it did not harm the white feminist base) as the cost of doing political business with the state. The international front fared no better, presenting a parallel in the [alleged] documented relationships of feminist icons with the U.S. security state during the Cold War. It revealed a pattern of counterinsurgency within feminism, aimed at neutralizing radical threats and steering dissent toward liberal, American-friendly ends. The case of Gloria Steinem is the most direct. In the 50s and 60s, Steinem worked for the independent research service, a front organization funded by the CIA-backed Congress for Cultural Freedom. Her role was to guide non-communist student activism in Europe, steering the youth away from Marxist traditions, toward a more benign, western liberalism. While no evidence suggests Bell Hooks was a *direct* asset, her work fits intimately within the legacy of liberal co-opting. Her focus on individual psychology and “healing”, and of course personal relationships with patriarchy, is contextualized for us as a depoliticizes shift. Her, and similar frameworks, dovetailed the CIA’s broader cultural strategy to divert energy from anti-imperialist and anti-capitalist struggles (which were targeted for destruction by COINTELPRO and other such agencies). Angela Davis presents another figure. Unequivocally, she was a priority target of the FBI’s COINTELPRO program, and state repression due to her politics and association with the BPP. Her later career, however, can be characterized as an iconic example of assimilation. The shift from calling for the abolition of the prison-industrial complex, to a tenured professor and feminist icon embraced by mainstream institutions, reflects a neutralization one must undergo in order to be integrated into the mainstream in the first place. It must also be said that Angela Davis supported Jim Jones of Jonestown infamy. Was she caught by the charismatic allure of Jones? Or was supporting him one of the conditions under which she would be integrated into the status quo? We may never know. 

A question one might have had rise up within them is, “why should I care about the black struggle being talked about with respect to feminism? How does this relate to me?”. The black struggle is not a peripheral issue of social justice, it exposes the core of modernity’s spiritual crisis as alliance with a system that severs human qualities from their purpose. Such a severance is built on a more primary severance of antiblackness, which is the most prominent concretized form of the abstract severance of man from his purpose. *All* people today, are in a sense, although not to the same degree, defined as objects in order to enable a world of transactional individualism. The “girlboss” mastering herself and the feminist theorist using black suffering as a metaphor are symptoms of the same problem. Consequently, the black struggle is the struggle against the primary act of objectification upon which this fractured modern self is built, from grasping a new subjectivity, to finding this subjectivity reflected in the institutions. To ignore it, is to ignore the root of the malaise I describe, the problem everyone faces. Humanity’s task is a conscious integration. This task is impossible while the collective psyche is built on denying the full humanity of a part of itself. 

So where does this lead “feminism” exactly? There is in a sense, an inevitability to a kind of “feminism”, particularly in this age. The establishment of the universal rational subject, or even more broadly, the “I”, would have eventually expanded to include all people, including women. This perspective gains a mighty force alongside a paradigmatic shift in technology and economics. When a society’s primary productive force ceases to be brute physical strength (a biologically dimorphic advantage) and becomes organized intelligence and technical skill, the material ground for patriarchy begins to dissolve. However, the materialist explanation only describes the mechanism of change in the physical world, not its ultimate cause. In order to grasp the possibility of a feminism, one must look to the spiritual or towards the ideal, and understand such historical developments, such as Enlightenment ethos, or the Industrial Revolution, which implies the shift to intellectual labor, as themselves the external manifestations of a deeper, internal event. This event is the full incarnation and awakening of the human “I”, or ego. This awakening spirit demands social forms that allow it to exercise its freedom, and henceforth generates the philosophies and technologies that clear the *physical* stage for its emergence. The material erosion of gendered roles certainly cannot be the cause of liberation. It must be understood as an *effect* of a spiritual imperative. True women’s libration, and men’s liberation from the one-sided masculinity of this current epoch by extension, exists as a spiritual possibility where the genderless “I”, incarnated in a male or female body, consciously integrates and spiritualizes both polar principles in itself, and is allowed to freely accomplish its goals without the projection of external determination and subjugation. 

I personally used to think that masculinity and femininity were differently manifested in men and women, based on the gender of the practitioner. But if this is true, then it locks women into gender-locked roles, tied down by sex. We remain confined in a logic where a woman’s ambition is forever “feminine ambition”, and her strength is a “feminine strength”, always differing in kind and purpose from a man’s. Her potential is filtered through a gendered lens, her highest aspirations subtly redirected toward roles considered appropriate for her “nature”. Likewise, man’s vulnerability is seen essentially as weakness. What would it mean for the active principle of masculinity to be vulnerable? It goes against its very definition (of course, as conceived one-sidedly). Not only does this view change when one considers the true character of masculine and feminine, it also changes when we consider the soul's journey across lifetimes. If the spirit incarnates alternately as man and woman (as many traditions, as well as Steiner suggests, and of course there are exceptions here), then these categories of “male experience” and “female experience” at best are not the final destinations, and at worst are incoherent. The soul does this to gather all possible human experiences; to know the world from within the polarity of giving and receiving, acting and enduring, speaking and listening, pushing and pulling, and so on and so forth, in all their physical and social configurations. A soul may cultivate a formidable will in one life as a man, and in the next as a woman, learn to wield this will through patience and inner resilience. The point is not to perfect masculinity or femininity, but to master the full spectrum of human capacity, which transcends both. 

From this perspective, a “real” feminism becomes possible. One informed by the conscious understanding of human development. This feminism would not fight for women to merely occupy men’s roles, as the “girlboss” does. Instead, it would work to create a society where the individual’s spiritual task of integration is supported by the outer world. Practically, this means freedom for *every* person, in whatever body they happen to inhabit. This comes in the form of advocating for social and economic structures that allow for the full expression of human variety. Women *do* face legitimate problems, which we may consider as a neglect. A neglect of both women, sure, but also of the “feminine” mode of interfacing with the world, of which this current epoch is biased against. So if a “feminism” emerges which is informed by an anthroposophical outlook, and covers the practical side of menstruation/toiletry products and public infrastructure, there should be no problem in providing it support.
